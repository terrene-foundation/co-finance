# Academic Integrity Rules

## Scope

These rules apply to all academic work produced with AI assistance, including essays, research papers, thesis chapters, case study analyses, presentations, discussion posts, problem sets, and any other assignment submitted for academic credit or professional evaluation. They apply regardless of course level (undergraduate or postgraduate) and regardless of the finance subdomain.

## MUST Rules

### 1. Cite All Claims with Proper Sources

Every factual claim, statistic, data point, or theoretical assertion MUST be supported by a cited source. The only exceptions are statements of common knowledge within the field (e.g., "stocks are traded on exchanges").

**Correct**:

```
The efficient market hypothesis posits that asset prices fully reflect all available
information (Fama, 1970). Empirical challenges to the strong form of EMH have been
documented extensively, including evidence of insider trading profitability
(Jaffe, 1974; Seyhun, 1986).
```

**Incorrect**:

```
Studies show that markets are mostly efficient. Some people have found evidence
against this, but it is debatable.
```

(No sources cited. "Studies show" without attribution is not acceptable in academic work.)

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: BLOCK submission — uncited claims must be sourced or removed

### 2. Disclose AI Assistance per Institutional Policy

Any use of AI tools (including this system) in research, drafting, analysis, or editing MUST be disclosed according to your institution's AI use policy. When no institutional policy exists, default to full transparency.

**Recommended Disclosure Format**:

```
AI Disclosure Statement: This [paper/analysis/presentation] was developed with the
assistance of [tool name], which was used for [specific tasks: e.g., literature search
suggestions, structural feedback on drafts, data analysis guidance, grammar review].
All arguments, analysis, and conclusions are the author's own. All sources were
independently verified by the author.
```

**What to Disclose**:

- AI tools used and their role (drafting assistance, editing, data analysis, brainstorming)
- Which sections or tasks involved AI assistance
- Confirmation that the author verified all sources and takes responsibility for the content

**What NOT to Do**:

- Submit AI-generated text as entirely your own work without disclosure
- Assume that because your institution has not yet banned AI, disclosure is unnecessary
- Use AI disclosure as a substitute for doing the intellectual work yourself

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: BLOCK submission — disclosure must be present before any work is finalized

### 3. Never Fabricate, Falsify, or Misattribute Sources

All cited sources MUST be real, accurately represented, and verifiable. This is the most serious academic integrity violation and applies without exception.

**Fabrication** — inventing a source that does not exist:

```
Incorrect: "According to Chen and Roberts (2023) in the Journal of Financial Economics..."
(No such paper exists by these authors in this journal in this year.)
```

**Falsification** — misrepresenting what a source actually says:

```
Incorrect: "Fama and French (1993) argued that the CAPM is sufficient for explaining
stock returns." (They argued the opposite — that additional factors are needed.)
```

**Misattribution** — assigning an idea to the wrong source:

```
Incorrect: "The Black-Scholes model was developed by Markowitz (1952)."
(Markowitz developed Modern Portfolio Theory. Black-Scholes was published by
Black and Scholes in 1973, with contributions from Merton.)
```

**Required Practice**: Before submitting, verify every citation by confirming:

- The source exists (check the journal, book, or database)
- The author(s) and year are correct
- The cited claim actually appears in the source
- Page numbers (where required by citation style) are accurate

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: BLOCK submission — this is grounds for academic misconduct proceedings at most institutions

### 4. Paraphrase Substantively

Paraphrasing MUST involve genuine restatement of ideas in your own words and sentence structure. Changing a few words or rearranging a sentence while preserving the original structure is not paraphrasing — it is too-close paraphrasing, which most institutions treat as a form of plagiarism.

**Original Source** (Mishkin, 2019):

```
"Central banks use open market operations as their primary tool for controlling
the money supply and influencing short-term interest rates."
```

**Correct Paraphrase**:

```
The primary mechanism through which central banks manage liquidity and steer
short-term borrowing costs is the buying and selling of government securities
in the open market (Mishkin, 2019).
```

(Restated in the author's own words, with different sentence structure, while preserving the meaning and citing the source.)

**Incorrect Paraphrase**:

```
Central banks utilize open market operations as their main instrument for
managing the money supply and affecting short-term interest rates (Mishkin, 2019).
```

(Only substituted synonyms — "use" to "utilize," "primary" to "main," "controlling" to "managing," "influencing" to "affecting." The sentence structure is identical.)

**When in Doubt, Quote Directly**: If the original language is precise and important, use a direct quote with quotation marks and a page number rather than a weak paraphrase.

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: HIGH priority revision — rework the paraphrase or convert to a direct quote

### 5. Follow Self-Plagiarism Rules

You MUST NOT submit the same work, or substantially the same work, for credit in more than one course without explicit written permission from all instructors involved.

**What Counts as Self-Plagiarism**:

- Submitting an entire paper from a previous course as new work
- Reusing large sections (multiple paragraphs or entire analyses) from your own prior submissions without disclosure
- Submitting the same dataset analysis with minor modifications to two different courses

**What Is Acceptable**:

- Building on a prior paper with instructor permission, clearly noting which parts are new
- Citing your own prior work as a source (e.g., "As I explored in a previous analysis...")
- Using the same broad topic across courses, provided the specific research question, analysis, and writing are original

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: HIGH priority fix — disclose reuse or produce original work

### 6. Attribute Ideas, Not Just Quotes

Citation is required not only for direct quotes but also for ideas, frameworks, models, and arguments that originate from another author, even when you express them entirely in your own words.

**Correct**:

```
The trade-off theory of capital structure suggests that firms balance the tax
advantages of debt against the costs of financial distress when choosing their
leverage ratio (Kraus & Litzenberger, 1973).
```

**Incorrect**:

```
Firms balance the tax benefits of borrowing against the risk of going bankrupt
when they decide how much debt to take on.
```

(This is the trade-off theory — an attributable idea with known originators — presented without citation.)

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: HIGH priority fix — add attribution

## MUST NOT Rules

### 1. No Fabricated Sources

MUST NOT invent authors, papers, books, journals, or data that do not exist. This includes generating plausible-sounding but fictional references.

**Detection Patterns**:

- Author names that do not appear in academic databases for the claimed topic
- Journal names that do not exist or paper titles that cannot be found
- DOIs or URLs that lead nowhere

**Consequence**: BLOCK submission — fabrication is the most severe academic integrity violation

### 2. No Unattributed Copying

MUST NOT copy text from any source — published papers, textbooks, websites, AI output, or other students' work — without proper quotation marks and citation.

**Consequence**: BLOCK submission — this constitutes plagiarism regardless of intent

### 3. No Citation Padding

MUST NOT add citations to the reference list that were not actually consulted or referenced in the body of the paper. Every source in the reference list MUST correspond to at least one in-text citation, and vice versa.

**Detection Patterns**:

- Reference list contains 30 sources but the paper body only cites 12
- Sources in the reference list that address topics not discussed in the paper
- In-text citations that do not appear in the reference list

**Consequence**: HIGH priority fix — remove uncited references or add proper in-text citations

### 4. No Ghost Authorship

MUST NOT list authors who did not contribute to the work, and MUST NOT omit authors who did contribute.

**Consequence**: HIGH priority fix — correct the author list

## AI Assistance Disclosure (CO Principle 8)

Students MUST disclose AI assistance according to their institution's academic integrity policy. CO for Finance is designed for responsible human-AI collaboration where:

- The student makes all analytical decisions (thesis positions, evidence evaluation, conclusions)
- The AI assists with research, structure, and explanation
- The workspace progression creates an auditable trail of genuine student engagement
- Disclosure is a requirement, not an option

This is not about concealing AI involvement. It is about ensuring the student's intellectual contribution is genuine and demonstrable.

## Exceptions

Academic integrity exceptions are narrow:

1. **Collaborative assignments**: When an instructor explicitly authorizes group work, shared text among group members is permitted within the scope of the assignment. Document the collaboration agreement.
2. **Building on prior work**: When an instructor grants written permission to extend a previous submission, the prior work must be clearly identified and the new contributions must be distinct.
3. **Standardized language**: Certain methodological descriptions (e.g., "We use OLS regression with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors") are sufficiently standard that they do not require unique paraphrasing each time. However, extended methodological passages still require citation of the originating technique.
4. **AI use in permitted contexts**: When an instructor explicitly permits AI assistance for specific tasks, follow the instructor's guidelines exactly and still disclose the assistance as described in Rule 2 above.
