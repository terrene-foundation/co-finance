# Academic Writing Rules

## Scope

These rules apply to all written academic work, including essays, research papers, thesis chapters, literature reviews, case study analyses, discussion posts, executive summaries, and any prose intended for academic evaluation. They apply to both undergraduate and postgraduate work across all finance topics.

## Authentic Voice Preservation (CO Principle 8)

These writing rules exist to produce high-quality academic prose that reflects the student's genuine intellectual engagement. They are not designed to conceal AI involvement. The goal is authentic voice: writing that demonstrates the student's own analytical thinking, supported by proper evidence and clear structure.

Students must disclose AI assistance per institutional policy (see `rules/academic-integrity.md`). The workspace progression (research -> analysis -> drafting -> review) creates an auditable trail of the student's engagement with the material.

## MUST Rules

### 1. Use Formal Academic Tone

All academic writing MUST maintain a formal, professional tone appropriate for scholarly communication.

**Correct**:

```
The results indicate that portfolio diversification reduced volatility by
approximately 18% over the sample period. This finding is consistent with
Modern Portfolio Theory (Markowitz, 1952), which predicts that combining
assets with low correlations reduces overall portfolio risk.
```

**Incorrect**:

```
The results basically show that spreading your money around cut volatility
by like 18%. This totally lines up with what Markowitz said back in the day
about not putting all your eggs in one basket.
```

**Specific Requirements**:

- No contractions ("do not" instead of "don't," "cannot" instead of "can't")
- No slang, colloquialisms, or informal expressions ("a lot," "kind of," "pretty much")
- No first person unless the assignment explicitly permits it (use "this paper argues" rather than "I argue," unless instructed otherwise)
- No rhetorical questions in formal papers (state the point directly)
- No exclamation marks
- Use hedging language where appropriate ("suggests," "indicates," "appears to") rather than absolute claims ("proves," "definitely shows")

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: HIGH priority revision

### 2. Include All Essential Paper Components

Every academic paper MUST contain these structural elements, unless the assignment specifies a different format:

**Thesis Statement**: A clear, specific, arguable claim that the paper will defend. It should appear in the introduction, typically at the end of the opening section.

```
Correct: "This paper argues that the 2008 financial crisis exposed fundamental
weaknesses in the Basel II framework's reliance on internal risk models, and
that the subsequent Basel III reforms, while addressing capital adequacy, remain
insufficient in their treatment of systemic liquidity risk."

Incorrect: "This paper is about the financial crisis and banking regulations."
(This is a topic, not a thesis. It makes no arguable claim.)
```

**Evidence**: Data, citations, case studies, or empirical results that support the thesis. Every major claim in the paper must be backed by evidence.

**Analysis**: The author's own interpretation of the evidence — explaining what the evidence means, why it matters, and how it connects to the thesis. Analysis is what distinguishes a research paper from a book report.

**Conclusion**: A synthesis that restates the thesis in light of the evidence presented, discusses implications, acknowledges limitations, and suggests directions for future research.

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: HIGH priority fix — missing components must be added

### 3. Support Every Claim with Evidence

Every assertion that is not common knowledge MUST be supported by one of the following:

- A cited source (peer-reviewed article, textbook, official data)
- Empirical data or statistical results presented in the paper
- A logical argument built on previously established and cited premises

**Correct**:

```
Emerging market currencies tend to depreciate during periods of global risk
aversion, as investors repatriate capital to safe-haven assets (Fratzscher, 2012).
This pattern was evident during the COVID-19 market shock of March 2020, when
the MSCI Emerging Markets Currency Index fell 5.3% in a single month
(MSCI, 2020).
```

**Incorrect**:

```
Emerging market currencies always crash during crises. Everyone knows that
investors flee to safety when things get bad.
```

(No source. "Everyone knows" is not evidence. "Always" is an unsupported absolute claim.)

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: HIGH priority fix — add evidence or qualify the claim

### 4. Use Proper Paragraph Structure

Each paragraph MUST follow a clear structure that advances the paper's argument:

**Topic Sentence**: States the paragraph's main point and connects it to the thesis.

**Evidence**: Presents data, quotes, or citations that support the topic sentence.

**Analysis**: Explains what the evidence means and why it matters.

**Transition**: Connects to the next paragraph's idea (can be at the end of the current paragraph or the beginning of the next).

**Correct**:

```
[Topic Sentence] The Fama-French three-factor model significantly improved
the explanatory power of asset pricing relative to the single-factor CAPM.
[Evidence] Fama and French (1993) demonstrated that adding size (SMB) and
value (HML) factors to the market risk premium increased the adjusted R-squared
from approximately 0.70 under CAPM to over 0.90 for diversified portfolios.
[Analysis] This improvement suggests that systematic risk, as captured by
market beta alone, is an incomplete description of the risks that investors
are compensated for bearing. Size and value appear to proxy for additional
sources of undiversifiable risk that the market factor misses.
[Transition] However, the three-factor model itself has been challenged by
the discovery of additional anomalies, most notably the momentum effect.
```

**Incorrect**:

```
The Fama-French model has three factors. The factors are market, size, and value.
Size is measured by SMB and value is measured by HML. The model was published in
1993. It works better than CAPM. Momentum is also important in finance.
```

(No analysis. Reads as a list of facts. The final sentence is disconnected.)

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: HIGH priority revision

### 5. Follow the Assigned Citation and Formatting Style

All papers MUST use the citation and formatting style specified by the assignment or institution. When no style is specified, default to APA 7th edition.

**Common Styles in Finance**:

| Style | Typical Use | In-Text Format |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **APA 7th** | Most finance and business courses | (Author, Year) |
| **Chicago Author-Date** | Economics and some finance journals | (Author Year) |
| **Chicago Notes-Bibliography** | History of finance, qualitative research | Footnotes/endnotes |
| **Harvard** | Common in UK/Australian institutions | (Author Year) |

**Formatting Requirements** (unless the assignment specifies otherwise):

- 12-point Times New Roman or similar serif font
- Double-spaced body text
- 1-inch (2.54 cm) margins on all sides
- Page numbers
- Title page with paper title, author name, institution, course, and date
- Headings consistent with the chosen style guide
- Reference list on a separate page

See `rules/citation-standards.md` for detailed formatting rules for each citation style.

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: HIGH priority fix — reformat to the correct style

### 6. Write Clear, Concise Sentences

Academic writing MUST be precise and efficient. Every sentence should earn its place.

**Correct**:

```
The Federal Reserve raised the federal funds rate by 75 basis points in
June 2022, its largest single increase since 1994 (Federal Reserve, 2022).
```

**Incorrect**:

```
It is important to note that the Federal Reserve, which is the central bank
of the United States of America, made the decision to raise what is known as
the federal funds rate by an amount of 75 basis points during the month of
June in the year 2022, which was actually the largest increase in a single
meeting that had occurred since the year 1994.
```

(Padded with unnecessary qualifiers and filler phrases. Academic writing values precision, not word count.)

**Common Filler Phrases to Eliminate**:

- "It is important to note that..." (just state the point)
- "In today's modern world..." (vague and unnecessary)
- "As we all know..." (if everyone knows, you do not need to say it)
- "The fact that..." (usually removable without changing meaning)
- "In order to..." (replace with "to")
- "Due to the fact that..." (replace with "because")

**Enforced by**: peer-reviewer agent
**Violation**: MEDIUM priority revision

## MUST NOT Rules

### 1. No Unsupported Generalizations

MUST NOT make sweeping claims without evidence.

**Detection Patterns**:

```
"The stock market always recovers from crashes."
"Diversification eliminates risk."
"Emerging markets are inherently unstable."
"Everyone agrees that central bank independence is beneficial."
```

All of these are either false as stated or require significant qualification and citation.

**Consequence**: HIGH priority fix — qualify the claim, add evidence, or remove it

### 2. No Plagiarized Structure

MUST NOT replicate the organizational structure and argument flow of a source even when the words are changed. If your paper follows the same section order, makes the same points in the same sequence, and reaches the same conclusions as a single source, this constitutes structural plagiarism.

**Consequence**: HIGH priority fix — develop an original argument structure

### 3. No Wikipedia or Non-Academic Sources as Primary Evidence

MUST NOT cite Wikipedia, Investopedia, blog posts, or social media as primary evidence for academic claims. These may be used for initial orientation to a topic, but the paper must cite the underlying primary or peer-reviewed sources.

**Acceptable Uses of Non-Academic Sources**:

- News articles (Wall Street Journal, Financial Times, Reuters) for reporting on events
- Central bank press releases and official statements as primary documents
- Industry reports (McKinsey, BIS, IMF) for data and analysis
- Regulatory filings (SEC EDGAR) as primary sources

**Consequence**: HIGH priority fix — replace with peer-reviewed or authoritative sources

### 4. No Emotional or Persuasive Language in Analytical Papers

MUST NOT use language designed to persuade through emotion rather than evidence in analytical or research papers.

**Detection Patterns**:

```
"The devastating impact of reckless monetary policy..."
"Greedy bankers caused the crisis..."
"The brilliant insights of Keynesian economics..."
```

**Correct Alternative**:

```
"The contractionary effects of the 2022-2023 tightening cycle..."
"Excessive risk-taking by financial institutions contributed to the crisis (FCIC, 2011)..."
"Keynesian fiscal policy prescriptions, as evaluated by Blanchard and Leigh (2013)..."
```

**Consequence**: HIGH priority revision — replace with neutral, evidence-based language

## Exceptions

Academic writing exceptions are limited:

1. **Reflective or opinion assignments**: When an instructor explicitly requests personal reflection, first person and less formal tone are appropriate. Still cite sources for factual claims.
2. **Presentations and slide decks**: Bullet points and abbreviated phrasing replace full paragraphs. Formal tone is still expected, but sentence fragments are acceptable for readability on slides.
3. **Discussion posts**: Some courses permit a more conversational tone in online discussion forums. Follow the instructor's stated expectations. Evidence requirements still apply.
4. **Creative or policy briefs**: When writing for a non-academic audience (e.g., a simulated policy memo to a finance minister), adjust the tone to suit the genre while maintaining intellectual rigor and proper sourcing.
