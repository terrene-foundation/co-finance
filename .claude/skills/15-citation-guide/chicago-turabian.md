# Chicago/Turabian Citation Guide

The Chicago Manual of Style (CMOS) is widely used in economics, history, and some finance journals. Turabian is a student-oriented subset of Chicago style. Chicago offers two citation systems: Notes-Bibliography (NB) and Author-Date (AD). Economics and finance typically use Author-Date; humanities use Notes-Bibliography.

## Why Chicago/Turabian Matters

Several leading economics journals (*American Economic Review*, *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, *Econometrica*) use Chicago Author-Date style. If your research crosses into economics, or if your department specifies Chicago, you need to know both systems.

## System 1: Notes-Bibliography (NB)

Used in humanities. Citations appear as footnotes or endnotes, with a full bibliography at the end.

### Footnote Format

First citation: full details. Subsequent citations: shortened form.

**First footnote**:
> 1. Eugene F. Fama, "Efficient Capital Markets: A Review of Theory and Empirical Work," *Journal of Finance* 25, no. 2 (1970): 383-417, https://doi.org/10.2307/2325486.

**Shortened footnote** (subsequent citations):
> 5. Fama, "Efficient Capital Markets," 390.

**Bibliography entry** (note the differences from the footnote):
> Fama, Eugene F. "Efficient Capital Markets: A Review of Theory and Empirical Work." *Journal of Finance* 25, no. 2 (1970): 383-417. https://doi.org/10.2307/2325486.

### Key NB Source Types

**Book**:

Footnote:
> 1. Zvi Bodie, Alex Kane, and Alan J. Marcus, *Investments*, 12th ed. (New York: McGraw-Hill Education, 2021), 245.

Bibliography:
> Bodie, Zvi, Alex Kane, and Alan J. Marcus. *Investments*. 12th ed. New York: McGraw-Hill Education, 2021.

**Edited volume chapter**:

Footnote:
> 2. Michael R. Roberts and Toni M. Whited, "Endogeneity in Empirical Corporate Finance," in *Handbook of the Economics of Finance*, vol. 2A, ed. George M. Constantinides, Milton Harris, and Rene M. Stulz (Amsterdam: Elsevier, 2013), 495.

Bibliography:
> Roberts, Michael R., and Toni M. Whited. "Endogeneity in Empirical Corporate Finance." In *Handbook of the Economics of Finance*, vol. 2A, edited by George M. Constantinides, Milton Harris, and Rene M. Stulz, 493-572. Amsterdam: Elsevier, 2013.

## System 2: Author-Date (AD)

Used in sciences and social sciences, including economics and finance. More similar to APA.

### In-Text Citations

**One author**: (Fama 1970) or Fama (1970)

**Two authors**: (Fama and French 1993) -- note: "and" not "&"

**Three or more authors**: (Fama et al. 2015)

**Page reference**: (Fama 1970, 383)

**Multiple works**: (Fama 1970; Jensen 1986)

### Reference List Format

The reference list is titled "References" (not "Bibliography" -- that term is reserved for the NB system).

**Journal article**:
> Fama, Eugene F., and Kenneth R. French. 1993. "Common Risk Factors in the Returns on Stocks and Bonds." *Journal of Financial Economics* 33 (1): 3-56. https://doi.org/10.1016/0304-405X(93)90023-5.

**Book**:
> Bodie, Zvi, Alex Kane, and Alan J. Marcus. 2021. *Investments*. 12th ed. New York: McGraw-Hill Education.

**Working paper**:
> Baker, Malcolm, and Jeffrey Wurgler. 2019. "Do Strict Capital Requirements Raise the Cost of Capital? Bank Regulation, Capital Structure, and the Low-Risk Anomaly." NBER Working Paper 25864. Cambridge, MA: National Bureau of Economic Research. https://doi.org/10.3386/w25864.

**Institutional report**:
> International Monetary Fund. 2025. *Global Financial Stability Report: Navigating Uncertainty*. Washington, DC: International Monetary Fund.

**Newspaper article**:
> Mackintosh, James. 2025. "Why Passive Investing Keeps Winning." *Wall Street Journal*, March 10, 2025.

**Dataset**:
> Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis. 2026. "10-Year Treasury Constant Maturity Rate." FRED Economic Data. Accessed March 1, 2026. https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS10.

**Website**:
> U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission. 2024. "Investor Bulletin: How Fees and Expenses Affect Your Investment Portfolio." Last modified January 15, 2024. https://www.sec.gov/investor/alerts.

## Key Differences: Chicago AD vs. APA 7th

| Feature | Chicago AD | APA 7th |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| In-text connector | "and" | "&" |
| Year placement in reference | After author name | After author name (in parentheses) |
| Article title | In quotation marks | No quotation marks, sentence case |
| Journal volume/issue | 33 (1): 3-56 | *33*(1), 3-56 |
| Publisher location | Included | Not included (APA 7th dropped this) |
| "et al." threshold | 4+ authors | 3+ authors |
| DOI format | https://doi.org/xxx. | https://doi.org/xxx |

### Worked Example: Same Source in Both Styles

**Chicago AD**:
> Fama, Eugene F., and Kenneth R. French. 1993. "Common Risk Factors in the Returns on Stocks and Bonds." *Journal of Financial Economics* 33 (1): 3-56. https://doi.org/10.1016/0304-405X(93)90023-5.

**APA 7th**:
> Fama, E. F., & French, K. R. (1993). Common risk factors in the returns on stocks and bonds. *Journal of Financial Economics*, *33*(1), 3-56. https://doi.org/10.1016/0304-405X(93)90023-5

**Key differences in this example**: Chicago uses full first names, "and" not "&", year after author, article title in quotes, no italics on volume, and a period after the DOI.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Mixing NB and AD systems**: Choose one system and use it consistently throughout. Never use footnotes for some citations and parenthetical for others.

2. **Using "&" in Chicago style**: Chicago always uses "and" between author names, both in-text and in the reference list. The ampersand is an APA convention.

3. **Omitting publisher location in Chicago**: Unlike APA 7th (which dropped locations), Chicago still requires city of publication for books.

4. **Incorrect capitalization of article titles**: In Chicago AD, article titles are in headline case *and* quotation marks. In APA, titles are in sentence case with no quotation marks.

5. **Forgetting the period after DOIs**: Chicago style places a period after the DOI link. APA does not.

6. **Using "Bibliography" with Author-Date**: The NB system uses "Bibliography"; the AD system uses "References." Using the wrong heading signals unfamiliarity with the style.
