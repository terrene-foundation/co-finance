# Harvard Referencing Style

Harvard is an author-date citation system widely used in UK, Australian, and some European universities. Unlike APA and Chicago, there is no single official Harvard manual -- institutions often publish their own Harvard guides. This file covers the most widely accepted conventions.

## Why Harvard Style Matters

If you study at a UK or Australian university, Harvard is likely the default citation style for your business and finance courses. Because there is no single standard, always check your institution's specific Harvard guide for any local variations.

## In-Text Citations

### Basic Format

**One author**: (Fama 1970) or Fama (1970) states that...

**Two authors**: (Fama and French 1993) or (Fama & French 1993) -- conventions vary by institution; check which is preferred.

**Three or more authors**: (Fama et al. 1993) from the first citation.

**Page number**: (Fama 1970, p. 383) or (Fama 1970, pp. 383-385)

**Multiple works**: (Fama 1970; Jensen 1986; Myers 1984) -- semicolons, alphabetical or chronological per your institution's guide.

**Multiple works by same author**: (Fama 1970, 1991) or (Fama 1970a, 1970b) if published in the same year.

**No author**: (World Bank 2025) -- use the organization name.

**No date**: (Smith n.d.)

**Secondary citation** (citing a source you found cited in another source):
(Bhattacharya 1979, cited in Allen and Michaely 2003)

**Note**: Always try to find the original source. Use secondary citations only when the original is unavailable.

## Reference List

The reference list appears at the end of the paper under the heading "References" or "Reference List." Entries are arranged alphabetically by the first author's surname.

### Journal Article

> Surname, Initials. (Year) 'Title of article', *Journal Name*, Volume(Issue), pp. Page range. doi:xxxxx.

**Example**:
> Fama, E.F. and French, K.R. (1993) 'Common risk factors in the returns on stocks and bonds', *Journal of Financial Economics*, 33(1), pp. 3-56. doi:10.1016/0304-405X(93)90023-5.

**Note**: Some Harvard guides use single quotes around article titles; others do not. Some capitalize the article title; others use sentence case. Follow your institution's guide.

### Book

> Surname, Initials. (Year) *Title of Book*. Edition. Place of publication: Publisher.

**Example**:
> Bodie, Z., Kane, A. and Marcus, A.J. (2021) *Investments*. 12th edn. New York: McGraw-Hill Education.

### Edited Book Chapter

> Surname, Initials. (Year) 'Title of chapter', in Editor Surname, Initials. (ed.) *Title of Book*. Place: Publisher, pp. Page range.

**Example**:
> Roberts, M.R. and Whited, T.M. (2013) 'Endogeneity in empirical corporate finance', in Constantinides, G.M., Harris, M. and Stulz, R.M. (eds.) *Handbook of the Economics of Finance*. Vol. 2A. Amsterdam: Elsevier, pp. 493-572.

### Working Paper

> Surname, Initials. (Year) *Title of Paper*. Working Paper No. xxx. Institution. Available at: URL (Accessed: Day Month Year).

**Example**:
> Baker, M. and Wurgler, J. (2019) *Do strict capital requirements raise the cost of capital?* NBER Working Paper No. 25864. Cambridge, MA: National Bureau of Economic Research. Available at: https://www.nber.org/papers/w25864 (Accessed: 1 March 2026).

### Institutional/Government Report

> Organization Name (Year) *Title of Report*. Place: Publisher. Available at: URL (Accessed: Day Month Year).

**Example**:
> International Monetary Fund (2025) *Global Financial Stability Report: Navigating Uncertainty*. Washington, DC: IMF. Available at: https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/GFSR (Accessed: 10 March 2026).

### Website

> Author or Organization (Year) *Title of Webpage*. Available at: URL (Accessed: Day Month Year).

**Example**:
> U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (2024) *Investor Bulletin: How Fees and Expenses Affect Your Investment Portfolio*. Available at: https://www.sec.gov/investor/alerts (Accessed: 15 March 2026).

### Newspaper Article

> Surname, Initials. (Year) 'Title of article', *Newspaper Name*, Day Month, p. Page or Available at: URL (Accessed: Day Month Year).

**Example**:
> Mackintosh, J. (2025) 'Why passive investing keeps winning', *The Wall Street Journal*, 10 March. Available at: https://www.wsj.com/articles/passive-investing (Accessed: 15 March 2026).

### Dataset

> Organization (Year) *Title of Dataset* [Dataset]. Available at: URL (Accessed: Day Month Year).

**Example**:
> Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis (2026) *Federal Funds Effective Rate* [Dataset]. FRED Economic Data. Available at: https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFF (Accessed: 1 March 2026).

## Key Differences: Harvard vs. APA 7th vs. Chicago AD

| Feature | Harvard | APA 7th | Chicago AD |
|---------|---------|---------|------------|
| Article title format | Often in single quotes | No quotes, sentence case | In double quotes, headline case |
| In-text connector | "and" or "&" (varies) | "&" | "and" |
| Page abbreviation | p. / pp. | p. / pp. | Comma only |
| "Available at" for URLs | Yes | No | No |
| "Accessed" date required | Yes (for online) | No | Recommended |
| Edition abbreviation | "edn." | "ed." | "ed." |
| DOI format | doi:xxxxx | https://doi.org/xxxxx | https://doi.org/xxxxx. |
| Publisher location | Required | Not required | Required |

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming all Harvard guides are identical**: They are not. Always check your university's specific Harvard guide. Variations exist in punctuation, capitalization, and formatting.

2. **Omitting "Available at" and "Accessed" for online sources**: Harvard style requires both the URL and the access date for online sources.

3. **Inconsistent use of "and" vs. "&"**: Pick one based on your institution's guide and use it consistently throughout.

4. **Forgetting "edn." for editions**: Harvard typically uses "edn." rather than "ed." for edition. Check your institution's guide.

5. **Not including page numbers for direct quotes**: In-text citations of direct quotes must include page numbers: (Fama 1970, p. 383).

6. **Mixing Harvard with APA formatting**: These styles look similar but differ in key details (quotes around titles, DOI format, accessed dates). Do not mix them.
