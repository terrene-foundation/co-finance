# Source Evaluation

Not all sources are created equal. A peer-reviewed article in the *Journal of Finance* carries far more weight than a blog post or a Wikipedia entry. Learning to evaluate sources is a core research skill -- your arguments are only as strong as the evidence supporting them.

## Why Source Evaluation Matters

In finance, acting on bad information can lead to real losses -- both academic (poor grades, rejected papers) and financial (bad investment decisions). The ability to distinguish reliable sources from unreliable ones is essential for producing credible research and making informed decisions.

## The CRAAP Test

A systematic framework for evaluating sources, developed by librarians at California State University.

### Currency

**Question**: When was the information published or last updated?

| Source Type | Acceptable Age |
|-------------|---------------|
| Market data | Must be current (same day or specified date) |
| Regulatory information | Check for updates/amendments since publication |
| Empirical research | Generally acceptable if within 10-15 years; older if seminal |
| Theoretical papers | No expiry (Modigliani-Miller 1958 is still foundational) |
| Textbooks | Current edition preferred; core concepts are stable |

**Finance-specific concern**: Financial regulations, market structures, and data availability change frequently. A study from 2005 on high-frequency trading may be completely outdated.

### Relevance

**Question**: Does the source address your research question?

- Is the source about the specific topic, market, or time period you are studying?
- Is the level appropriate? (An introductory textbook is not appropriate for a PhD literature review.)
- Does it provide evidence, theory, or methodology relevant to your research?

### Authority

**Question**: Who created this information, and what are their credentials?

| Source | Authority Indicators |
|--------|---------------------|
| Journal article | Author affiliations, journal reputation, peer review |
| Working paper | Author reputation, hosting institution (NBER, SSRN) |
| Industry report | Organization credibility (IMF, World Bank, BIS) |
| News article | Reporter expertise, publication reputation |
| Website/blog | Author credentials, institutional backing |
| Dataset | Provider reputation, methodology documentation |

**Finance-specific check**: Is the author affiliated with a university, central bank, or reputable research institution? Or are they affiliated with a firm that might have commercial interests?

### Accuracy

**Question**: Is the information supported by evidence?

- Are claims backed by data and citations?
- Can you verify the findings against other sources?
- Is the methodology described in sufficient detail?
- Are limitations acknowledged?

**Red flags**: No citations, vague methodology, extraordinary claims without extraordinary evidence, data that cannot be independently verified.

### Purpose

**Question**: Why was this information created?

| Purpose | Reliability | Examples |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Inform/educate** | Generally high | Textbooks, journal articles, encyclopedias |
| **Analyze/persuade** | Moderate (check for bias) | Opinion pieces, policy papers, advocacy research |
| **Sell/promote** | Low (conflicts of interest) | Broker reports, fund marketing materials, sponsored content |
| **Entertain** | Low (not intended as evidence) | Financial media, social media posts |

**Finance-specific concern**: Sell-side research (from investment banks) may have conflicts of interest. The analyst's employer may have a business relationship with the company being analyzed.

## The Peer Review Process

### How Peer Review Works

1. Author submits manuscript to a journal
2. Editor assesses whether the paper fits the journal's scope
3. Editor sends to 2-3 anonymous referees (experts in the field)
4. Referees write detailed reports evaluating methodology, contribution, and quality
5. Editor decides: accept, revise and resubmit, or reject
6. Most papers go through 2-4 rounds of revision before acceptance

### Why Peer Review Matters

Peer review is an imperfect but essential quality filter. It catches methodological errors, identifies overlooked literature, and ensures that claims are supported by evidence. A peer-reviewed article has survived scrutiny by experts.

### Limitations of Peer Review

- **Publication bias**: Journals prefer significant results; null findings are harder to publish
- **Speed**: The process takes 1-3 years; findings may be outdated by publication
- **Conservatism**: Truly novel ideas may be rejected by reviewers committed to existing paradigms
- **Errors persist**: Peer review reduces but does not eliminate errors

### Journal Quality in Finance

| Tier | Journals | Description |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Top 3** | *Journal of Finance*, *Journal of Financial Economics*, *Review of Financial Studies* | Premier outlets; extremely selective |
| **Top field** | *Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis*, *Review of Finance*, *Journal of Money, Credit and Banking* | Highly respected; strong peer review |
| **Good field** | *Financial Management*, *Journal of Banking & Finance*, *Journal of Corporate Finance* | Solid research; widely cited |
| **Economics generalist** | *American Economic Review*, *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, *Econometrica* | Top outlets that publish finance papers |

## Predatory Journals

### What Are Predatory Journals?

Journals that exploit the open-access model by charging publication fees without providing genuine peer review. They accept virtually any paper that pays.

### How to Identify Them

| Red Flag | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Aggressive solicitation** | Unsolicited emails inviting you to submit or join the editorial board |
| **Rapid acceptance** | Papers accepted within days (genuine review takes months) |
| **No identifiable editor** | Editor is unknown or has no academic track record |
| **No indexing** | Not indexed in Web of Science, Scopus, or JSTOR |
| **Vague scope** | Journal covers everything from "finance to agriculture to medicine" |
| **Dubious metrics** | Invented impact metrics instead of standard Journal Citation Reports |

### Verification Tools

- **Beall's List** (archived): Identified potential predatory publishers
- **DOAJ (Directory of Open Access Journals)**: Vetted list of legitimate open-access journals
- **Scopus/Web of Science**: If a journal is indexed here, it has met minimum quality standards
- **Think. Check. Submit.** (thinkchecksubmit.org): Checklist for evaluating journals

## Primary vs. Secondary Sources

### Primary Sources

Original data, documents, or research.

**Examples in finance**:
- Company annual reports and SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q)
- Original datasets (CRSP, Compustat)
- Central bank reports and minutes (Federal Reserve FOMC minutes)
- Original research articles presenting new empirical findings
- Legislative texts (Dodd-Frank Act)

### Secondary Sources

Analysis, interpretation, or compilation of primary sources.

**Examples in finance**:
- Textbooks (synthesize many primary studies)
- Literature review articles
- Financial news analysis
- Industry overview reports
- Encyclopedia entries

### Rule of Thumb

Cite primary sources whenever possible. Use secondary sources for context and overview, but trace their claims back to the primary source when the claim is important to your argument.

## Worked Example: Evaluating a Source

You find this claim: "Passive investing is destroying price discovery and will cause the next financial crisis."

**Source**: A blog post by an active fund manager.

**CRAAP evaluation**:
- **Currency**: Published recently -- acceptable
- **Relevance**: Directly relevant to your research on passive investing
- **Authority**: The author is a fund manager, not an academic researcher. They have a financial interest in discrediting passive investing.
- **Accuracy**: The post cites no peer-reviewed research. Claims are assertions, not evidence-based conclusions.
- **Purpose**: To persuade; potentially to promote active management services.

**Verdict**: Use with extreme caution. Acknowledge the perspective but do not treat it as evidence. Instead, find the peer-reviewed research on the topic (e.g., Sushko & Turner, 2018, BIS Quarterly Review).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Citing Wikipedia**: Wikipedia can be useful for initial orientation, but it is not an acceptable academic source. Use it to find the original sources cited in the article, then cite those.

2. **Treating all journal articles as equal**: An article in a predatory journal is not equivalent to one in the *Journal of Finance*. Journal quality matters.

3. **Ignoring working papers**: Unpublished working papers from NBER, SSRN, or university websites often contain the most recent research. They are acceptable to cite, but note they are not peer-reviewed.

4. **Over-relying on textbooks**: Textbooks are secondary sources. For your literature review, cite the original research papers.

5. **Not checking for conflicts of interest**: A report from an investment bank about the value of investment banking services should be read skeptically.

6. **Confusing age with irrelevance**: Fama (1970) on market efficiency is over 50 years old but remains one of the most cited papers in finance. Seminal papers do not expire.
