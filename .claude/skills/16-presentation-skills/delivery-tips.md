# Delivery Tips

Great content on great slides is wasted if the delivery is poor. How you present -- your pacing, voice, body language, and handling of questions -- determines whether the audience absorbs your message or tunes out.

## Why Delivery Matters

Research on communication consistently shows that delivery accounts for more of the audience's impression than content alone. You can have the best analysis in the room, but if you speak in a monotone, read from your slides, and avoid eye contact, the audience will not engage.

## Pacing

### Structure Your Time

For a 15-minute presentation with 12 slides:

| Section | Time | Slides |
|---------|------|--------|
| Introduction and motivation | 2 min | 1-2 |
| Main content | 9 min | 3-9 |
| Conclusion and recommendation | 2 min | 10-11 |
| Summary/thank you | 1 min | 12 |
| Buffer for transitions | 1 min | -- |

### Speed

- Normal speaking pace: 120-150 words per minute
- Slow down for key points and complex ideas
- Pause after stating something important -- silence gives the audience time to absorb
- If you find yourself rushing through slides, you have too many slides

### The Power of Pausing

Pauses serve multiple purposes:
- **After a key statement**: Let the point sink in
- **Before a transition**: Signal that you are moving to a new topic
- **After asking a rhetorical question**: Let the audience mentally formulate an answer
- **When you lose your place**: A pause feels natural to the audience even if it feels eternal to you

## Voice

### Projection

Speak loudly enough for the back row to hear without straining. If using a microphone, speak at a normal volume and let the mic do the work.

### Variety

Vary your pitch, speed, and volume to maintain attention:
- **Important point**: Slow down and drop your voice slightly
- **Exciting finding**: Speed up slightly and raise your energy
- **Transition**: Brief pause, then slightly louder to recapture attention

### Fillers

"Um," "uh," "like," "you know," "so," and "basically" are verbal fillers. Everyone uses them, but overuse is distracting.

**How to reduce fillers**:
- Become aware of your specific fillers (record yourself practicing)
- Replace fillers with pauses (silence is more authoritative)
- Practice transitions between slides -- these are where fillers cluster

## Body Language

### Eye Contact

- Look at the audience, not the screen or your notes
- Make eye contact with different parts of the room (left, center, right)
- Hold eye contact with one person for 3-5 seconds before moving to another
- If looking at individuals feels uncomfortable, look at foreheads or the space between people

### Posture and Movement

- Stand tall, shoulders back, feet shoulder-width apart
- Do not stand behind the podium the entire time -- move deliberately
- Face the audience when speaking (do not turn your back to read slides)
- Use purposeful movement to emphasize transitions (move to the other side of the room when changing topics)

### Gestures

- Use open-handed gestures when making a point
- Avoid crossing arms, hands in pockets, or fidgeting
- Hold a pen or pointer if you need something for your hands
- Point at the screen to draw attention, but face the audience while doing so

## Handling Q&A

### Preparation

Before presenting, anticipate the three most likely questions and prepare answers. In finance presentations, common question categories include:
- **Methodology**: "Why did you use this approach instead of...?"
- **Assumptions**: "What if your growth rate assumption is wrong?"
- **Alternatives**: "Have you considered...?"
- **Data**: "Where did you get this data? What is the sample period?"

### During Q&A

1. **Listen fully**: Do not start formulating your answer while the questioner is still talking
2. **Repeat or paraphrase the question**: "So the question is about whether our results hold with a different benchmark. Great question."
3. **Answer concisely**: 30-60 seconds per answer. If you need longer, offer to discuss after the presentation
4. **Admit when you do not know**: "I have not analyzed that specific scenario, but it is a good direction for future work." Honesty builds credibility; bluffing destroys it
5. **Bridge to your key messages**: "That connects to our broader finding that..."

### Difficult Questions

| Question Type | Strategy |
|--------------|----------|
| **Hostile/challenging** | Stay calm, acknowledge the point, respond with evidence |
| **Off-topic** | "That is an interesting point. It is a bit outside the scope of today's presentation, but I would be happy to discuss it afterward." |
| **Multi-part** | "There are several parts to that question. Let me start with..." |
| **You do not know the answer** | "That is a great question. I do not have the data to answer that right now, but I will look into it." |
| **Clarifying (confused questioner)** | Rephrase your point differently; do not just repeat the same words louder |

## Managing Nerves

### Before the Presentation

- **Practice aloud**: At least three full run-throughs. Practice standing up, speaking at full volume, clicking through slides. Silent rehearsal in your head is insufficient.
- **Know your opening cold**: Memorize your first 60 seconds. If you can start strong, confidence builds.
- **Arrive early**: Check the room, test the projector, familiarize yourself with the space.
- **Physical preparation**: Slow deep breaths (4 seconds in, 6 seconds out). Stand in a confident posture for two minutes before you start. Avoid excess caffeine.

### During the Presentation

- **Focus on the message, not yourself**: Anxiety increases when you focus on how you look or sound. Shift focus to what the audience needs to hear.
- **Make eye contact with friendly faces**: Locate 2-3 people who are nodding or smiling. Speaking to receptive faces builds confidence.
- **Slow down**: Nervousness accelerates speech. Consciously speak 10-20% slower than feels natural.
- **Accept imperfection**: A stumble or a forgotten point is not a disaster. The audience does not know your script. Pause, collect yourself, and continue.

### Reframing Anxiety

- Nervousness and excitement produce the same physiological response (elevated heart rate, adrenaline). Tell yourself "I am excited" rather than "I am nervous" -- the reframe is supported by research (Brooks, 2014).
- Some nervousness sharpens performance. Elite speakers still feel nervous; they have learned to channel it.

## Professional Presence

### Dress

Dress one level above your audience. For a finance class presentation, business casual is usually appropriate. For a case competition or job interview, business formal.

### Timing

Start on time. End on time or slightly early. Going over time signals disrespect for the audience and poor preparation.

### Technical Setup

- Arrive 10-15 minutes early to test equipment
- Bring your presentation on a USB drive and email it to yourself as backup
- Know how to present in "presenter view" (so you see your notes; the audience sees only the slides)
- Have a printed backup of key slides in case of technical failure

## Common Mistakes

1. **Reading slides verbatim**: Your slides are a visual aid, not a script. Speak naturally and use the slides to reinforce (not replace) your spoken message.

2. **Apologizing for your presentation**: "I did not have much time to prepare" or "This is not my area of expertise" -- these undermine your credibility before you have started. Present with confidence regardless.

3. **Turning your back to the audience**: When referencing a slide, gesture toward it while facing the audience. Glance briefly at the screen, then look back.

4. **No practice run**: Winging it is obvious to the audience. Practice creates fluency, reveals timing issues, and builds confidence.

5. **Running over time**: This is one of the most common and most penalized mistakes in academic and professional presentations. Practice with a timer.

6. **Ending with "Any questions?"**: End with your key takeaway, then open the floor for questions. The last thing the audience hears should be your message, not a procedural question.
