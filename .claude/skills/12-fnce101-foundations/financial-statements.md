# Financial Statements

Financial statements are the language of business. They provide a structured, standardized way to report what a company owns, what it owes, how much revenue it generates, and where its cash comes from and goes. Learning to read financial statements is learning to see through the numbers to the economic reality of a business.

## Why Financial Statements Matter

You cannot value what you do not understand. Before applying any valuation model -- DCF, multiples, or comparable analysis -- you need to know how to extract the relevant information from financial statements. They answer three fundamental questions:

1. **Income Statement**: How profitable was the company this period?
2. **Balance Sheet**: What does the company own and owe right now?
3. **Cash Flow Statement**: Where did cash actually come from and where did it go?

Think of it this way: the income statement is the movie (what happened over a period), the balance sheet is the photograph (a snapshot at a point in time), and the cash flow statement is the bank statement (actual cash movements).

## The Income Statement

Reports revenues, expenses, and profits over a period (quarter or year).

### Structure

| Line Item | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Revenue (Sales)** | Total income from selling goods or services |
| - Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) | Direct costs of producing goods sold |
| = **Gross Profit** | Revenue - COGS |
| - Operating Expenses (SG&A, R&D) | Indirect costs: selling, general, admin, research |
| = **EBIT (Operating Income)** | Earnings Before Interest and Taxes |
| - Interest Expense | Cost of debt financing |
| = **EBT (Earnings Before Tax)** | Pre-tax profit |
| - Income Tax | Taxes owed on pre-tax income |
| = **Net Income** | Bottom-line profit available to shareholders |

### Key Relationships

> **Gross Margin = Gross Profit / Revenue**

> **Operating Margin = EBIT / Revenue**

> **Net Margin = Net Income / Revenue**

> **EPS = Net Income / Shares Outstanding**

### Worked Example: Reading an Income Statement

| | FY 2024 ($ millions) |
|---|---|
| Revenue | $5,000 |
| COGS | ($2,800) |
| **Gross Profit** | **$2,200** |
| Operating Expenses | ($1,200) |
| **EBIT** | **$1,000** |
| Interest Expense | ($150) |
| **EBT** | **$850** |
| Tax (25%) | ($212.5) |
| **Net Income** | **$637.5** |

Gross Margin = $2,200 / $5,000 = **44.0%**
Operating Margin = $1,000 / $5,000 = **20.0%**
Net Margin = $637.5 / $5,000 = **12.75%**

**Interpretation**: The company keeps 44 cents of every revenue dollar after direct costs, 20 cents after all operating costs, and roughly 13 cents as bottom-line profit.

## The Balance Sheet

A snapshot of what the company owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and the residual claim of shareholders (equity) at a specific date.

### The Accounting Identity

> **Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity**

This always holds. Every dollar of assets is financed by either debt (liabilities) or equity.

### Structure

**Assets** (what the company owns):

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Current Assets** | Cash, accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses |
| **Non-Current (Long-Term) Assets** | Property/plant/equipment (PP&E), intangible assets, goodwill |

**Liabilities** (what the company owes):

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Current Liabilities** | Accounts payable, short-term debt, accrued expenses, current portion of long-term debt |
| **Non-Current Liabilities** | Long-term debt, pension obligations, deferred tax liabilities |

**Shareholders' Equity** (residual claim):

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Common Stock & Paid-In Capital** | Money invested by shareholders |
| **Retained Earnings** | Cumulative net income not paid out as dividends |
| **Treasury Stock** | Shares repurchased by the company (reduces equity) |

### Key Balance Sheet Metrics

> **Book Value of Equity = Total Assets - Total Liabilities**

> **Net Working Capital (NWC) = Current Assets - Current Liabilities**

> **Tangible Book Value = Book Value - Intangible Assets - Goodwill**

## The Cash Flow Statement

Tracks actual cash inflows and outflows, organized into three activities.

### Structure

**Operating Activities**: Cash from the core business.
- Starts with net income
- Adds back non-cash charges (depreciation, amortization)
- Adjusts for changes in working capital

**Investing Activities**: Cash spent on long-term assets.
- Capital expenditures (buying PP&E)
- Acquisitions
- Sales of assets or investments

**Financing Activities**: Cash from or to capital providers.
- Issuing or repaying debt
- Issuing or repurchasing equity
- Paying dividends

> **Change in Cash = Cash from Operations + Cash from Investing + Cash from Financing**

### Free Cash Flow

The cash available to all investors (both debt and equity holders) after maintaining the business.

> **Free Cash Flow (FCFF) = Cash from Operations - Capital Expenditures**

Or from the income statement:

> **FCFF = EBIT x (1 - Tax Rate) + Depreciation - CapEx - Change in NWC**

**Why free cash flow matters more than net income**: Net income includes non-cash items (depreciation, amortization) and can be manipulated through accounting choices. Free cash flow represents actual cash generated.

### Worked Example: Deriving Free Cash Flow

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Net Income | $637.5M |
| + Depreciation & Amortization | $200M |
| - Increase in Accounts Receivable | ($50M) |
| - Increase in Inventory | ($30M) |
| + Increase in Accounts Payable | $40M |
| = **Cash from Operations** | **$797.5M** |
| - Capital Expenditures | ($300M) |
| = **Free Cash Flow** | **$497.5M** |

**Interpretation**: Although net income was $637.5M, the company generated $497.5M in actual free cash flow after reinvesting in the business.

## Financial Ratio Analysis

Ratios convert raw financial data into standardized metrics that allow comparison across companies and over time.

### Liquidity Ratios (Can the company pay its short-term bills?)

> **Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities**

A current ratio above 1.0 means the company has more short-term assets than short-term obligations. A ratio of 2.0 is often considered healthy, though this varies by industry.

> **Quick Ratio (Acid-Test) = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities**

More conservative than the current ratio because inventory may not be quickly convertible to cash.

> **Cash Ratio = Cash and Cash Equivalents / Current Liabilities**

Most conservative liquidity measure.

### Profitability Ratios (How efficiently does the company generate profit?)

> **Return on Equity (ROE) = Net Income / Shareholders' Equity**

How much profit the company generates per dollar of equity. One of the most important ratios for equity investors.

> **Return on Assets (ROA) = Net Income / Total Assets**

How efficiently the company uses all its assets to generate profit.

> **DuPont Decomposition**:

> **ROE = Net Margin x Asset Turnover x Equity Multiplier**
> **ROE = (Net Income / Revenue) x (Revenue / Total Assets) x (Total Assets / Equity)**

This decomposition reveals whether high ROE comes from high margins, efficient asset use, or leverage.

### Worked Example: DuPont Analysis

| Item | Value |
|------|-------|
| Net Income | $637.5M |
| Revenue | $5,000M |
| Total Assets | $8,000M |
| Shareholders' Equity | $3,200M |

Net Margin = $637.5M / $5,000M = 12.75%
Asset Turnover = $5,000M / $8,000M = 0.625
Equity Multiplier = $8,000M / $3,200M = 2.5

ROE = 12.75% x 0.625 x 2.5 = **19.92%**

**Interpretation**: This company achieves nearly 20% ROE. The equity multiplier of 2.5 tells us the company uses significant leverage (for every $1 of equity, it has $2.50 of assets). If we reduce leverage, ROE would fall -- the high return partly reflects financial risk.

### Leverage Ratios (How much debt does the company use?)

> **Debt-to-Equity (D/E) = Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity**

> **Debt-to-Assets = Total Debt / Total Assets**

> **Interest Coverage Ratio = EBIT / Interest Expense**

An interest coverage ratio below 2.0 is a warning sign -- the company may struggle to cover its interest payments.

> **Debt-to-EBITDA = Total Debt / EBITDA**

Used by lenders and credit agencies. Higher ratios indicate greater default risk.

### Efficiency Ratios (How well does the company manage its operations?)

> **Inventory Turnover = COGS / Average Inventory**

> **Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) = 365 / Inventory Turnover**

> **Receivables Turnover = Revenue / Average Accounts Receivable**

> **Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) = 365 / Receivables Turnover**

> **Payables Turnover = COGS / Average Accounts Payable**

> **Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) = 365 / Payables Turnover**

> **Cash Conversion Cycle = DIO + DSO - DPO**

The cash conversion cycle measures how many days it takes to convert inventory into cash. Lower is better -- it means the company gets paid faster relative to when it pays its suppliers.

### Worked Example: Cash Conversion Cycle

A retailer has DIO = 45 days, DSO = 30 days, DPO = 60 days.

Cash Conversion Cycle = 45 + 30 - 60 = **15 days**

**Interpretation**: On average, the company must finance 15 days of operations from its own resources. A negative cycle (e.g., Amazon) means the company collects from customers before paying suppliers -- it is effectively financed by its suppliers.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing net income with cash flow**: A company can report positive net income while running out of cash (e.g., rapidly growing receivables that have not been collected). Always check the cash flow statement.

2. **Ignoring off-balance-sheet items**: Operating leases (prior to IFRS 16/ASC 842), special purpose entities, and contingent liabilities may not appear on the balance sheet but represent real obligations.

3. **Comparing ratios across industries without context**: A current ratio of 1.2 is fine for a utility (stable cash flows) but may be dangerously low for a seasonal retailer. Always compare within the same industry.

4. **Using book value as market value**: Book values are historical cost (minus depreciation). Market values reflect current economic reality. A building purchased for $1 million 20 years ago may be worth $5 million today -- but the balance sheet still shows the depreciated historical cost.

5. **Forgetting that ratios require context**: A single ratio in isolation is almost meaningless. Compare ratios to (a) the company's own history (trend analysis) and (b) peer companies in the same industry (cross-sectional analysis).

6. **Overlooking the quality of earnings**: Watch for aggressive revenue recognition, one-time gains classified as recurring, and changes in accounting policies that artificially inflate earnings.

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1**: A company has current assets of $500,000 (including $150,000 in inventory) and current liabilities of $300,000. Calculate the current ratio and quick ratio.

**Problem 2**: Using the DuPont decomposition, analyze why Company A (ROE = 25%, Net Margin = 5%, Asset Turnover = 2.0) has a higher ROE than Company B (ROE = 18%, Net Margin = 15%, Asset Turnover = 0.6). Which company's ROE is more sustainable?

**Problem 3**: A company reports net income of $100 million but its cash from operations is only $20 million. What are three possible explanations for this discrepancy?

**Problem 4**: Calculate the cash conversion cycle for a company with COGS = $800M, Revenue = $1,200M, Average Inventory = $100M, Average Receivables = $150M, and Average Payables = $80M.
