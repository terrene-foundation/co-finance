# Behavioral Finance

Cognitive biases, psychological traps, and debiasing strategies that affect investment decisions. Essential knowledge for financial education platforms teaching realistic, self-aware investing.

## Overview

This skill covers the intersection of psychology and finance:

- Core cognitive biases that distort investment decisions
- Loss aversion, herding, and emotional trading patterns
- Overconfidence, anchoring, and confirmation bias
- Practical debiasing strategies and systematic decision frameworks

## Reference Documentation

### Foundational Biases

- **[cognitive-biases](cognitive-biases.md)** - Overview of cognitive biases in investing: prospect theory, mental accounting, framing effects, availability heuristic, representativeness, status quo bias. Real-world market examples.

### Emotional Biases

- **[loss-aversion-herding](loss-aversion-herding.md)** - Loss aversion (losses hurt 2x more than equivalent gains), disposition effect, herding behavior, FOMO, panic selling. Research citations and market examples.

### Information Processing Biases

- **[overconfidence-anchoring](overconfidence-anchoring.md)** - Overconfidence bias (overtrading, concentrated positions), anchoring (purchase price, round numbers, analyst targets), confirmation bias, hindsight bias. Debiasing strategies.

### Corrective Strategies

- **[debiasing-strategies](debiasing-strategies.md)** - Practical debiasing: investment checklists, pre-commitment strategies, systematic rebalancing, rules-based investing, journaling, devil's advocate process, base rate thinking.

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when:

- Teaching investors about common psychological traps
- Building tools that help counteract cognitive biases
- Designing investment decision frameworks
- Creating content about why investors underperform markets
- Explaining market anomalies driven by behavioral factors
- Building financial education curricula that include self-awareness
