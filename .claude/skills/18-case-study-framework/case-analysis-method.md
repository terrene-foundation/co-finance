# Case Analysis Method

The Harvard case method is the dominant approach to business and finance education worldwide. It presents a real or realistic business situation and asks you to step into the role of the decision-maker. There is no single correct answer -- what matters is the quality of your analysis and the defensibility of your recommendation.

## Why the Case Method Matters

Finance is not a spectator sport. Textbooks teach you the tools; cases teach you how to use them under conditions of ambiguity, incomplete information, and conflicting objectives -- exactly the conditions you will face in a real career.

## The Three Phases

### Phase 1: Read

**Objective**: Extract the facts and identify what the case is really about.

**First read** (20-30 minutes): Read the entire case quickly. Do not take notes. Get the big picture.
- What is the setting? (Industry, company, time period)
- Who is the protagonist? (Who makes the decision?)
- What is the central question or dilemma?
- What is the timeline for the decision?

**Second read** (30-45 minutes): Read carefully and take structured notes.
- Highlight key financial data (revenue, margins, growth rates, valuations)
- Identify the main characters and their perspectives
- Note any assumptions or constraints stated in the case
- Mark data you will need for calculations
- List what information is missing (this is often deliberate)

**Organize your notes into**:
1. Facts (objective data from the case)
2. Assumptions (what the case asks you to assume)
3. Issues (problems or opportunities identified)
4. Stakeholders (who is affected and how)

### Phase 2: Analyze

**Objective**: Apply frameworks and quantitative tools to develop insights.

**Step 1: Problem identification**. State the core problem in one sentence.
- "The CEO must decide whether to acquire Company B at $500M before a competing bidder enters."
- "The portfolio manager must choose between maintaining the current allocation or hedging against rising interest rates."

**Step 2: Situation analysis**. Use appropriate frameworks (see [analytical-frameworks.md](analytical-frameworks.md)):
- External analysis: PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces
- Internal analysis: SWOT, Value Chain
- Financial analysis: Ratio analysis, DCF, comparable valuation

**Step 3: Generate alternatives**. Identify 2-4 realistic options.
- Always include "do nothing" / "status quo" as one option
- Options should be mutually exclusive and collectively cover the reasonable range
- Each option should be specific enough to evaluate

**Step 4: Evaluate alternatives**. For each option, assess:
- Financial impact (NPV, IRR, payback, effect on margins)
- Strategic fit (alignment with company strategy and capabilities)
- Risk (what could go wrong? how likely? how severe?)
- Feasibility (can the company actually execute this?)
- Stakeholder impact (employees, shareholders, customers, regulators)

**Step 5: Recommend**. Choose one option and justify it.

### Phase 3: Discuss

**Objective**: Defend your recommendation and engage with alternative perspectives.

In class discussions or case competitions:
- **Lead with your recommendation** (do not build suspense)
- **Support with evidence** from your analysis
- **Acknowledge counterarguments** and explain why your recommendation is still superior
- **Be prepared to adjust** if new information or perspectives emerge

## The Recommendation Test

A strong recommendation is:

| Criterion | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Specific** | Not vague or generic | "Acquire Company B for $450M" not "Pursue growth" |
| **Actionable** | Can be implemented immediately | Includes timeline and key steps |
| **Supported by evidence** | Grounded in data from the case | References specific numbers and analysis |
| **Addresses the core problem** | Solves the actual problem, not a side issue | Directly responds to the central dilemma |
| **Accounts for risk** | Acknowledges what could go wrong | Includes mitigation strategies |

## Worked Example: Reading a Case

**Case summary**: Alpha Corp, a mid-cap industrial manufacturer, is considering acquiring Beta Inc, a competitor, for $800 million. Beta has strong brand recognition but declining margins. Alpha's CEO believes cost synergies of $50 million per year are achievable. Alpha's WACC is 9%. The board needs a recommendation by next month.

**Phase 1 notes**:
- **Setting**: Industrial manufacturing, M&A context
- **Protagonist**: Alpha Corp's CEO
- **Central question**: Should Alpha acquire Beta for $800M?
- **Key data**: Price = $800M, synergies = $50M/year, WACC = 9%, Beta's margins declining
- **Missing information**: Beta's standalone value, integration risks, financing structure

**Phase 2 analysis**:
- Synergy value = $50M / 0.09 = $556M (perpetuity at WACC)
- Need Beta's standalone DCF to determine total value
- Premium = $800M - Beta standalone value
- NPV of acquisition = (Standalone + Synergies) - Purchase Price
- Risk factors: integration failure, cultural mismatch, declining margins may not reverse

**Phase 3 recommendation**: "Proceed with the acquisition, but negotiate the price down to $700M. At $800M, the deal requires nearly full realization of synergies to create value. At $700M, the NPV is positive even if only 70% of projected synergies materialize. I recommend a phased integration plan with clear milestones for synergy capture."

## Time Management for Case Analysis

### In-Class Cold Call (2-3 minutes)

1. State the problem (15 seconds)
2. Give your recommendation (15 seconds)
3. Support with 2-3 key points (90 seconds)
4. Acknowledge the main risk (20 seconds)

### Written Case Assignment (2-3 hours)

| Phase | Time |
|-------|------|
| First read | 20 min |
| Second read with notes | 30 min |
| Quantitative analysis | 45 min |
| Framework analysis | 30 min |
| Writing the recommendation | 30 min |
| Proofreading | 15 min |

### Case Competition (2-4 hours with team)

| Phase | Time |
|-------|------|
| Individual read | 30 min |
| Team alignment on key issues | 15 min |
| Divide analysis tasks | 5 min |
| Parallel analysis (financial, strategic, operational) | 60 min |
| Reconvene and synthesize | 30 min |
| Build presentation | 30-45 min |
| Practice delivery | 15-20 min |

## Common Mistakes

1. **Jumping to a solution before analyzing**: The urge to "solve" the case immediately is strong. Resist it. Spend at least half your time on analysis before forming a recommendation.

2. **Retelling the case**: "Alpha Corp is a manufacturer that..." -- the professor and the audience already know this. Focus on analysis and insight, not summary.

3. **Ignoring the numbers**: Even qualitative cases usually contain financial data. Crunch the numbers. A recommendation without quantitative support is an opinion.

4. **Not making a decision**: "Alpha could either acquire or not acquire" is not a recommendation. Pick a side, justify it, and defend it.

5. **Ignoring implementation**: "Alpha should acquire Beta" is incomplete. How? When? Who leads integration? What are the milestones?

6. **Analysis paralysis**: You will never have complete information. The case deliberately withholds some data. Make reasonable assumptions, state them explicitly, and proceed.
