# Analytical Frameworks

Frameworks are structured tools for organizing analysis. They do not give you the answer -- they ensure you ask the right questions and do not miss important factors. In case analysis, choosing the right framework for the situation is itself a skill.

## When to Use Which Framework

| Framework | Best For | Key Question |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **SWOT** | Quick situational snapshot | What are our strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats? |
| **Porter's Five Forces** | Industry attractiveness | How competitive is this industry? Where does profit go? |
| **PESTEL** | Macro-environment scan | What external forces affect this business? |
| **Value Chain** | Operational analysis | Where does the company create (or destroy) value? |
| **BCG Matrix** | Portfolio/business unit strategy | Which businesses should get investment? Which should be divested? |

## SWOT Analysis

### Structure

|  | Positive | Negative |
|--|----------|----------|
| **Internal** | Strengths | Weaknesses |
| **External** | Opportunities | Threats |

### How to Use Effectively

**Do not** just list items. **Do** prioritize and connect them:
- Which strengths can exploit which opportunities? (Strategy)
- Which weaknesses make which threats dangerous? (Vulnerability)
- Which weaknesses prevent capturing which opportunities? (Constraint)

### Worked Example: SWOT for a Regional Bank

| Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-----------|-----------|
| Strong local brand, loyal customer base | Limited digital banking capabilities |
| Low cost of funds (stable deposits) | Small scale relative to national banks |
| Deep knowledge of local market | Concentrated geographic exposure |

| Opportunities | Threats |
|--------------|---------|
| Fintech partnership for digital services | Big tech entry into financial services |
| Underserved small business lending market | Rising interest rates compressing NIM |
| Wealth management for aging demographics | Regulatory compliance costs increasing |

**Strategic insight**: The bank's strongest play is to leverage its local brand and customer relationships (strength) to capture the underserved small business market (opportunity), while partnering with a fintech to address its digital weakness before big tech enters (threat).

## Porter's Five Forces

Assesses the competitive intensity and attractiveness of an industry.

### The Five Forces

**1. Threat of New Entrants**
- Barriers to entry: capital requirements, economies of scale, regulatory licenses, brand loyalty
- High barriers = lower threat = more attractive industry

**2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers**
- Few suppliers or differentiated inputs = high supplier power
- Many substitutable suppliers = low supplier power

**3. Bargaining Power of Buyers**
- Few large buyers or undifferentiated products = high buyer power
- Many fragmented buyers or unique products = low buyer power

**4. Threat of Substitutes**
- Alternative products that serve the same function
- High threat when switching costs are low and substitutes are close in quality

**5. Rivalry Among Existing Competitors**
- Intensity depends on: number of competitors, industry growth rate, product differentiation, exit barriers
- Slow growth + many competitors + commodity products = intense rivalry

### Worked Example: Five Forces for Asset Management Industry

| Force | Assessment | Key Factors |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **New Entrants** | Moderate | Low capital requirements for small firms, but distribution and track record create barriers |
| **Supplier Power** | Low | Labor market is competitive; data providers have some power |
| **Buyer Power** | High | Institutional investors are price-sensitive; fee compression is real |
| **Substitutes** | High | Index funds and ETFs substitute for active management at lower cost |
| **Rivalry** | High | Thousands of competitors; performance hard to differentiate; fee pressure |

**Conclusion**: The asset management industry is structurally challenging. High buyer power and the threat of low-cost substitutes (index funds) compress fees. Firms must differentiate through performance, client service, or specialized strategies.

## PESTEL Analysis

Scans the macro-environment for factors that affect the business.

| Factor | Description | Finance Examples |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Political** | Government policy, stability, regulation | Tax reform, trade wars, sanctions |
| **Economic** | GDP growth, inflation, interest rates, exchange rates | Recession impact on loan defaults, rate hikes on bond portfolios |
| **Social** | Demographics, cultural trends, consumer behavior | Aging population increasing demand for retirement products |
| **Technological** | Innovation, automation, digital disruption | Algorithmic trading, blockchain, robo-advisors |
| **Environmental** | Climate change, sustainability, ESG | Carbon pricing, green bonds, ESG investing mandates |
| **Legal** | Regulations, compliance requirements | Dodd-Frank, MiFID II, data privacy (GDPR) |

### How to Use PESTEL Effectively

Do not just list factors. For each relevant factor:
1. **Describe** the trend or development
2. **Assess impact**: Is it positive, negative, or neutral for the firm?
3. **Determine likelihood**: How certain is this development?
4. **Identify timeline**: Is this imminent or long-term?

## Value Chain Analysis

Identifies where in its operations a company creates (or could create) competitive advantage.

### Primary Activities

1. **Inbound logistics**: Receiving and storing inputs
2. **Operations**: Transforming inputs into finished products
3. **Outbound logistics**: Delivering to customers
4. **Marketing and sales**: Promoting and selling products
5. **Service**: After-sale support

### Support Activities

1. **Firm infrastructure**: Management, planning, finance, legal
2. **Human resource management**: Recruiting, training, compensation
3. **Technology development**: R&D, process improvement
4. **Procurement**: Purchasing inputs

### Finance Application

For a bank, the value chain might be:
- **Inbound**: Gathering deposits (cost of funds)
- **Operations**: Credit analysis, risk management, loan processing
- **Outbound**: Disbursing loans, managing portfolios
- **Marketing**: Client acquisition, relationship management
- **Service**: Account management, advisory services

**Insight**: A bank that excels at credit analysis (operations) and relationship management (service) creates value through lower default rates and higher client retention.

## BCG Matrix

Classifies business units or products based on market growth and relative market share.

### The Four Quadrants

| | High Market Share | Low Market Share |
|--|-------------------|------------------|
| **High Growth** | **Stars**: Invest heavily; future cash generators | **Question Marks**: Invest selectively or divest |
| **Low Growth** | **Cash Cows**: Harvest cash; fund stars | **Dogs**: Divest or restructure |

### Finance Application

For a diversified financial services firm:
- **Star**: Wealth management (growing, high share) -- invest
- **Cash Cow**: Traditional retail banking (mature, high share) -- harvest
- **Question Mark**: Cryptocurrency trading platform (growing, low share) -- invest or exit?
- **Dog**: Declining insurance product line (mature, low share) -- divest

### Limitations

- Growth rate and market share are not the only determinants of attractiveness
- Does not account for synergies between business units
- Static snapshot; does not capture dynamics

## Combining Frameworks

The most effective case analyses use multiple frameworks:

1. **Start with PESTEL** for the big picture (macro environment)
2. **Apply Five Forces** for industry-level analysis (competitive dynamics)
3. **Use Value Chain** for firm-level analysis (where the company creates value)
4. **Synthesize with SWOT** (bringing together internal and external factors)
5. **Quantify with financial analysis** (DCF, ratios, comparable valuation)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Framework as checklist**: Listing every box in SWOT or every force in Porter's without prioritizing or drawing insights. The framework is a tool for thinking, not a form to fill out.

2. **Using the wrong framework**: Porter's Five Forces analyzes industry structure, not individual companies. SWOT is for a specific firm. Do not confuse levels of analysis.

3. **Ignoring the numbers**: Frameworks provide qualitative structure, but finance cases require quantitative analysis too. Always complement frameworks with financial modeling.

4. **Treating all factors as equally important**: Prioritize. In most cases, 2-3 factors are decisive and the rest are secondary. Focus your analysis on what matters most.

5. **Analysis without recommendation**: Frameworks help you analyze, but the goal is a decision. Do not stop at analysis -- make a recommendation.

6. **Forcing a framework**: Not every case needs all five frameworks. Use what is relevant and skip what is not.
