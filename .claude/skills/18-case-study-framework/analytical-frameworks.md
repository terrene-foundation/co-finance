# Analytical Frameworks for Case Studies

The most commonly used frameworks in business and finance case analysis, and when each is appropriate.

## Porter's Five Forces

**Purpose**: Assess industry attractiveness and competitive dynamics.

| Force | Description | Key Questions |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **Rivalry among existing competitors** | Intensity of competition | How many competitors? Price competition? Differentiation? |
| **Threat of new entrants** | Barriers to entry | Capital requirements? Regulatory barriers? Brand loyalty? |
| **Threat of substitutes** | Alternative products/services | What else could customers use? Switching costs? |
| **Bargaining power of buyers** | Customer leverage | Concentration? Price sensitivity? Switching costs? |
| **Bargaining power of suppliers** | Supplier leverage | Concentration? Unique inputs? Switching costs? |

**Use when**: Analyzing an industry's long-term profitability potential or evaluating a firm's competitive position.

## SWOT Analysis

| Internal | External |
|----------|----------|
| **Strengths**: What the firm does well | **Opportunities**: Favorable external trends |
| **Weaknesses**: Where the firm is vulnerable | **Threats**: Unfavorable external trends |

**Use when**: Quick situational overview. Good starting point but should be followed by deeper analysis.

**Tip**: Connect SWOT elements. A strength that addresses an opportunity is a strategic priority. A weakness that intersects with a threat is a critical vulnerability.

## PESTEL Analysis

Macro-environmental factors:

| Factor | Finance Examples |
|--------|----------------|
| **Political** | Government stability, trade policy, taxation |
| **Economic** | GDP growth, interest rates, inflation, exchange rates |
| **Social** | Demographics, consumer preferences, workforce trends |
| **Technological** | Fintech, blockchain, AI, automation |
| **Environmental** | ESG regulations, climate risk, carbon pricing |
| **Legal** | Securities regulation, bankruptcy law, data privacy |

**Use when**: Assessing the macro environment's impact on a firm or industry.

## BCG Growth-Share Matrix

| | High Market Share | Low Market Share |
|---|-----------------|-----------------|
| **High Growth** | Stars (invest) | Question Marks (invest or divest) |
| **Low Growth** | Cash Cows (harvest) | Dogs (divest) |

**Use when**: Portfolio analysis for multi-business firms. Deciding where to allocate capital.

## Value Chain Analysis

Identifies where value is created (and where costs accumulate) across the firm's activities:

**Primary activities**: Inbound logistics --> Operations --> Outbound logistics --> Marketing & Sales --> Service
**Support activities**: Firm infrastructure, HR, Technology, Procurement

**Use when**: Understanding competitive advantage or identifying cost reduction opportunities.

## Choosing the Right Framework

| Situation | Best Framework(s) |
|-----------|-------------------|
| "Should we enter this industry?" | Porter's Five Forces + PESTEL |
| "What is our competitive advantage?" | Value Chain + SWOT |
| "Which business units should we invest in?" | BCG Matrix + Financial analysis |
| "What external risks do we face?" | PESTEL |
| "Quick overview of the situation" | SWOT |
| "How much is this company worth?" | DCF (see dcf-walkthrough.md) |

## Common Mistakes

1. **Using a framework as a checklist**: Do not mechanically fill in every box. Focus on the factors that actually matter for the case.
2. **SWOT without prioritization**: A 20-item SWOT list is useless. Identify the 2-3 most important items in each quadrant.
3. **Forcing a framework**: Not every case needs Porter's Five Forces. Use the framework that fits the question.
4. **Ignoring financial analysis**: Frameworks provide qualitative structure, but cases usually require quantitative analysis too.
