# Dividend Policy

How firms decide whether, when, and how much to return to shareholders. Dividend policy is one of the three core decisions in corporate finance (along with investment and financing). Despite decades of research, it remains one of the most debated topics -- what Fischer Black famously called "the dividend puzzle."

## Why Dividend Policy Matters

Investors care about dividends because dividends are real cash in their pocket. But should firms pay dividends, or should they retain earnings and reinvest? The answer depends on which theory you believe -- and on the firm's specific circumstances.

## Dividend Theories

### 1. Dividend Irrelevance (Miller & Modigliani, 1961)

In a perfect market, dividend policy is irrelevant to firm value.

> **V = V (regardless of dividend policy)**

**Intuition**: If a firm does not pay dividends, its share price increases by the retained amount. Shareholders can create "homemade dividends" by selling shares. The total wealth is the same either way.

**Key assumptions**: No taxes, no transaction costs, no information asymmetry, no agency problems.

**Why it matters**: Like MM's capital structure irrelevance, it is the starting point. Dividends matter in practice precisely because these assumptions do not hold.

*Source: Miller, M.H. & Modigliani, F. (1961). "Dividend Policy, Growth, and the Valuation of Shares." Journal of Business, 34(4), 411-433.*

### 2. Bird-in-Hand Theory (Gordon & Lintner)

Investors prefer the certainty of dividends over the uncertainty of future capital gains. Therefore, firms that pay higher dividends have a lower cost of equity and higher value.

> **r_e decreases as dividend yield increases**

**Intuition**: A bird in the hand (dividend today) is worth two in the bush (promised capital gains tomorrow). Dividends are "safer" because they are cash received now.

**Criticism**: MM argued this is a fallacy. The risk of the firm's cash flows does not change just because you label part of the return "dividend" instead of "capital gain." The business risk is unchanged.

*Source: Gordon, M.J. (1963). "Optimal Investment and Financing Policy." Journal of Finance, 18(2), 264-272.*

### 3. Tax Preference Theory

If dividends are taxed at a higher rate than capital gains, investors prefer firms to retain earnings and deliver returns through share price appreciation.

> **V = V - PV(Tax Disadvantage of Dividends)**

**Historical context**: In the US, dividends were taxed as ordinary income (up to 39.6%) while long-term capital gains were taxed at lower rates (20%). The 2003 tax reform equalized rates at 15% (now 20% for high earners), reducing but not eliminating the tax preference.

**Implication**: Firms with high-tax-bracket shareholders should pay fewer dividends.

### 4. Signaling Theory

Dividends convey information about management's confidence in future earnings. Increasing dividends signals that management expects sustainably higher cash flows.

**Key evidence**:
- Dividend increases are associated with positive stock price reactions (1-3% average abnormal return)
- Dividend cuts are associated with large negative reactions (-5% to -10%)
- This is because managers are reluctant to cut dividends, so an increase signals genuine confidence

**Implication**: Firms initiate and increase dividends to signal quality. This explains why firms maintain stable or growing dividends even when earnings fluctuate.

### 5. Agency Theory (Free Cash Flow Hypothesis)

Dividends reduce the free cash flow available to managers, limiting wasteful spending and empire-building.

> **Dividends as a disciplinary mechanism**: Returning cash to shareholders prevents managers from investing in negative-NPV projects for personal prestige.

*Source: Jensen, M.C. (1986). "Agency Costs of Free Cash Flow, Corporate Finance, and Takeovers." American Economic Review, 76(2), 323-329.*

### 6. Clientele Effect

Different investors prefer different dividend policies based on their tax situations and income needs. Firms attract a "clientele" that matches their policy.

- **High-dividend clientele**: Tax-exempt institutions (pension funds, endowments), retirees needing income
- **Low-dividend clientele**: High-income individuals who prefer capital gains, growth investors

**Implication**: Changing dividend policy may alienate the existing clientele without gaining new investors, creating temporary price pressure.

## Share Buybacks (Repurchases)

An alternative to dividends for returning cash to shareholders. The firm buys its own shares on the open market or through a tender offer, reducing shares outstanding.

### Buybacks vs. Dividends

| Feature | Dividends | Buybacks |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **Tax treatment** | Taxed as income when paid | Tax deferred; only taxed when shares are sold (capital gains) |
| **Flexibility** | Strong expectation of continuation; cuts are penalized | One-time or irregular; no commitment to repeat |
| **Signal** | Signals sustainable cash flow | May signal undervaluation or lack of investment opportunities |
| **Effect on EPS** | No change in shares outstanding | Reduces shares outstanding, increasing EPS |
| **Investor choice** | All shareholders receive cash | Only selling shareholders receive cash; others get larger ownership stake |

### Why Buybacks Have Grown

Buybacks have surpassed dividends as the dominant form of payout in the US since the late 1990s. Reasons include:
- Tax advantage (deferred capital gains vs. immediate dividend income)
- Flexibility (no commitment to repeat)
- EPS management (reduces denominator, increasing EPS)
- Stock option considerations (dividends reduce stock price, hurting option holders; buybacks support price)

## Payout Ratios

### Dividend Payout Ratio

> **Dividend Payout Ratio = Dividends per Share / Earnings per Share = Total Dividends / Net Income**

Measures the fraction of earnings paid as dividends. The remainder is retained.

- Low payout (< 30%): Growth firms retaining earnings for investment
- Moderate payout (30-60%): Mature firms balancing growth and income
- High payout (> 60%): Stable, mature firms with limited growth opportunities

### Dividend Yield

> **Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend per Share / Current Share Price**

Measures the income return from holding the stock, expressed as a percentage.

### Retention Rate

> **Retention Rate = 1 - Dividend Payout Ratio = Retained Earnings / Net Income**

### Sustainable Growth Rate

> **g = ROE x Retention Rate = ROE x (1 - Dividend Payout Ratio)**

The maximum rate a firm can grow using only retained earnings (no new external financing).

### Worked Example: Payout Analysis

A firm has:
- Net income: $200 million
- Dividends paid: $60 million
- Share price: $40
- Shares outstanding: 100 million
- ROE: 15%

Dividends per share = $60M / 100M = $0.60
EPS = $200M / 100M = $2.00

Dividend payout ratio = $0.60 / $2.00 = **30%**
Dividend yield = $0.60 / $40.00 = **1.5%**
Retention rate = 1 - 0.30 = **70%**
Sustainable growth rate = 15% x 70% = **10.5%**

**Interpretation**: The firm retains 70% of earnings, which at its 15% ROE implies it can sustain a 10.5% growth rate without raising new capital.

## Dividend Payment Process

1. **Declaration date**: Board of directors declares the dividend (amount and payment date)
2. **Ex-dividend date**: First trading day on which the stock trades without the dividend. Buyers on this date do NOT receive the upcoming dividend. Stock price typically drops by approximately the dividend amount.
3. **Record date**: One business day after ex-dividend date. Only shareholders of record receive the dividend.
4. **Payment date**: Dividend is actually paid to shareholders.

## Lintner's Dividend Smoothing Model

Firms adjust dividends gradually toward a target payout, rather than making large changes each period.

> **Delta D_t = c x (Target Payout x E_t - D_(t-1))**

Where:
- Delta D_t = Change in dividend this period
- c = Speed of adjustment (typically 0.3-0.5)
- Target Payout = Long-run target payout ratio
- E_t = Current earnings
- D_(t-1) = Last period's dividend

**Intuition**: If earnings double, the firm does not double its dividend immediately. It increases gradually, because it wants to be confident the earnings increase is sustainable before committing to a higher dividend.

*Source: Lintner, J. (1956). "Distribution of Incomes of Corporations Among Dividends, Retained Earnings, and Taxes." American Economic Review, 46(2), 97-113.*

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming high dividends mean high returns**: Dividend yield is only one component of total return. A stock with a 5% dividend yield but declining share price may deliver lower total returns than a growth stock with no dividend.

2. **Treating dividend cuts as always negative**: Sometimes a dividend cut is the right decision (e.g., to fund a valuable acquisition or survive a downturn). The market reaction depends on the reason.

3. **Ignoring the tax implications**: In many jurisdictions, dividends and capital gains are taxed differently. The after-tax return is what matters to investors.

4. **Confusing payout ratio with dividend yield**: Payout ratio is dividends/earnings (firm's perspective). Dividend yield is dividends/price (investor's perspective). A firm can have a high payout ratio but low yield if its P/E is high.

5. **Assuming buybacks are always better than dividends**: Buybacks destroy value if the firm repurchases overvalued shares. They create value only when shares are fairly valued or undervalued.
