# International Finance Analysis Frameworks

Systematic frameworks for analyzing global financial events and connecting them to international finance theory. Derived from the 2026 Oil-Trade-Monetary Policy Triangle deep-dive project.

## 1. The Impossible Trinity Mapping

**When to use**: Any event involving central bank policy, exchange rates, or capital flows.

### Framework

Every country occupies a corner of the Mundell-Fleming trilemma:

| Position | Choice                                              | Sacrifice                   | Examples                |
| -------- | --------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Corner A | Free capital flows + Independent monetary policy    | Fixed exchange rate         | US, UK, Japan, Eurozone |
| Corner B | Free capital flows + Fixed exchange rate            | Independent monetary policy | Hong Kong, Saudi Arabia |
| Corner C | Independent monetary policy + Managed exchange rate | Free capital flows          | China                   |

### Application Pattern

1. Classify each affected country's trilemma position
2. Identify how the event stress-tests their chosen corner
3. Note where the crisis reveals the cost of their trade-off
4. Compare countries at different corners to show how the same shock produces different outcomes

### Key Insight

The trilemma is a structural constraint, not a policy recommendation. Countries can't escape it — they can only choose which trade-off they accept.

## 2. Oil Shock Transmission Chain

**When to use**: Any event disrupting energy supply or causing commodity price spikes.

### The 5-Step Chain

```
Step 1: Physical disruption → Insurance withdrawal → Shipping halt
Step 2: Spot prices surge (document: pre-crisis → peak → average)
Step 3: Broader commodity disruption (LNG, fertilizer, freight)
Step 4: Consumer pass-through (direct: weeks; indirect: months)
Step 5: Central bank reaction (policy reversal, rate reassessment)
```

### IMF Multiplier (Quantitative Tool)

For sustained oil price increases:

- **Inflation impact**: (% oil rise / 10) x 0.4 percentage points
- **Growth impact**: (% oil rise / 10) x 0.15-0.2 percentage points

**Example**: 60% oil rise → +2.4pp inflation, -0.9pp GDP growth

### Country Vulnerability Matrix

Classify countries by:

- Oil import dependence (% from affected region)
- Exchange rate regime (does weakness amplify costs?)
- Existing inflation level (starting from 2% vs. 5% matters)
- Domestic production capacity (partial buffer?)
- Fiscal capacity to subsidize (India subsidizes; does the budget allow it?)

## 3. Double Supply Shock Analysis

**When to use**: When two or more supply-side inflation sources hit simultaneously.

### Compounding Effects

A product that crosses borders AND requires energy is hit multiple times:

1. Energy input cost increase
2. Tariff/trade barrier cost
3. Energy transport cost increase
4. Exchange rate amplification (for weak-currency countries)

### Combined Impact Calculation

```
Total inflation impact = Oil contribution + Tariff contribution + Exchange rate amplification
```

Goldman Sachs framework:

- Tariff contribution: 0.5-0.8 percentage points
- Oil contribution: IMF multiplier (see above)
- Combined: 3+ percentage points for dual shock

### The Central Bank Bind (Stagflation Trap)

```
                If raise rates → Growth falls further (adds demand-side hit to supply-side problem)
                If hold rates → Both problems persist (uncertainty continues)
                If cut rates → Inflation accelerates (credibility damaged)
```

No option solves both problems. This IS the diagnosis: stagflation.

## 4. Trade Diversion vs. Trade Destruction

**When to use**: Analyzing tariff or trade policy effects.

### Key Distinction

- **Trade destruction**: Trade that disappears entirely (deadweight loss)
- **Trade diversion**: Trade that reroutes to different partners (redistribution)

### Evidence Pattern

Document these metrics:

- Target country export change (% YoY)
- Target country export destinations shift (which new partners?)
- Imposing country bilateral deficit change
- Intermediate country absorption (who benefits from diversion?)

### The Tariff Effectiveness Test

Tariffs "work" depends on the goal:

- Protecting domestic industry? (Partially)
- Reducing bilateral deficit? (Usually not — diversion offsets)
- Reducing target's economic power? (Usually not — new markets found)
- Generating revenue? (Yes, but who pays? — cite NY Fed 90% finding)

## 5. Dollar Paradox Analysis

**When to use**: When exchange rate movements contradict textbook predictions.

### Textbook vs. Reality

| Prediction                     | Mechanism                        | Reality Check                    |
| ------------------------------ | -------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Tariffs strengthen currency    | Reduced imports → less FX demand | Retaliation may offset           |
| Rate hikes strengthen currency | Higher yield attracts capital    | Risk-off may dominate            |
| Safe-haven flows to USD        | Crisis → flight to safety        | Only if crisis is external to US |

### Forces That Override Textbook

1. Retaliation effect (reduces export demand for currency)
2. Uncertainty premium (investors demand discount)
3. Fiscal deficit expansion (increases currency supply)
4. De-dollarization trend (structural demand reduction)
5. Rate expectations (anticipated cuts reduce yield differential)

### Exchange Rate Pass-Through Feedback

A weaker domestic currency amplifies import costs, including oil. Document:

- Oil price change in USD terms
- Exchange rate change
- Effective oil price change in local currency terms

**Example**: Oil +60% in USD. Euro +14% vs USD → ~40% effective rise. Yen -X% vs USD → >60% effective rise.

## 6. Country Vulnerability Assessment

**When to use**: Comparing how the same global shock affects different economies.

### Profile Template

For each country, assess:

| Factor                  | Assessment                                        |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Oil vulnerability       | [Extreme / High / Moderate / Low]                 |
| Tariff exposure         | [Direct / Indirect / Self-imposed / Low]          |
| Monetary policy freedom | [Constrained / Moderate / Free]                   |
| Market reaction         | [Key index, % change]                             |
| Trilemma trade-off      | [What their position costs them in this crisis]   |
| Unique factor           | [What makes this country's situation distinctive] |

### Negative Feedback Loop Detection

Watch for self-reinforcing cycles:

```
Market selloff → Capital flight → Currency weakness → Higher import costs → Worse outlook → More selling
```

South Korea 2026 pattern: the "canary in the coal mine" — maximum vulnerability on all three dimensions (energy-dependent + export-dependent + financially open).

**When to use**: Putting current events in historical context.

### Comparison Template

### "Which Parallel Fits Best?" Framework

| If you think...                     | Best parallel | Because...  |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------- | ----------- |
