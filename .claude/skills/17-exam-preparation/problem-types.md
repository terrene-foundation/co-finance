# Finance Exam Problem Types

How to recognize and approach different types of exam questions.

## Type 1: Calculation Problems

**What they test**: Your ability to apply formulas correctly and interpret results.

### Strategy: The 5-Step Method

1. **Identify**: What are you solving for? (PV, FV, YTM, NPV, WACC, etc.)
2. **State the formula**: Write it out before plugging in numbers
3. **Define variables**: List what you know and what you need to find
4. **Compute**: Show all steps -- partial credit requires visible work
5. **Interpret**: What does the number mean? (e.g., "NPV > 0, so accept the project")

### Common Calculation Traps

- **Rate/period mismatch**: Monthly payments require monthly rates (annual rate / 12)
- **Rounding too early**: Keep intermediate calculations precise; round only the final answer
- **Sign conventions**: In TVM problems, cash outflows are negative, inflows are positive
- **Forgetting to annualize**: If you calculate a monthly return, the exam likely wants the annual return

## Type 2: Conceptual Questions

**What they test**: Understanding of theories, assumptions, and relationships.

### Strategy

- **Define the concept clearly** before elaborating
- **Use the textbook language** -- examiners look for key terminology
- **Explain the intuition** in addition to the technical definition
- **Give an example** to demonstrate understanding

### Example Question and Answer

**Q**: "Explain why the pecking order theory predicts that profitable firms will have less debt."

**A**: "The pecking order theory (Myers & Majluf, 1984) states that firms prefer internal financing first, then debt, then equity, due to information asymmetry costs. Profitable firms generate more retained earnings (internal funds), so they do not need to issue debt to finance investment. Therefore, the pecking order predicts an inverse relationship between profitability and leverage -- not because debt is bad, but because profitable firms simply do not need it."

## Type 3: Case Analysis

**What they test**: Your ability to apply multiple concepts to a real-world scenario.

### Strategy

- **Read the case fully** before writing anything
- **Identify the core question**: What decision needs to be made?
- **Apply relevant frameworks**: Which theories or tools are appropriate?
- **Use the case data**: Reference specific numbers from the case
- **Make a recommendation**: Take a position and support it

## Type 4: Essay Questions

**What they test**: Depth of understanding, ability to synthesize, and quality of argumentation.

### Strategy

- **Plan before writing**: 5 minutes of outlining saves 15 minutes of rambling
- **Structure your answer**: Introduction (state your thesis) --> Body (2-3 supporting arguments) --> Conclusion
- **Use theory and evidence**: Reference specific models, authors, and empirical findings
- **Address counterarguments**: Show you understand the debate, not just one side
- **Stay on topic**: Answer the question asked, not the question you wish was asked

## Time Management

| Question Type | Suggested Time Allocation |
|--------------|--------------------------|
| Short calculations | 1-2 minutes each |
| Long calculations | 5-10 minutes each |
| Conceptual (short answer) | 3-5 minutes each |
| Essay questions | 15-25 minutes each |
| Case analysis | 20-30 minutes |

**Rule**: Allocate time proportional to marks. If a question is worth 20% of marks, spend approximately 20% of time on it.
