# Creating Effective Formula Sheets

How to build a formula sheet that actually helps during exams (and during study).

## Principles

### 1. Organize by Topic, Not by Textbook Chapter

Group formulas by the type of problem they solve:
- Time value of money formulas together
- Risk and return formulas together
- Valuation formulas together

### 2. Show Relationships Between Formulas

Formulas are not isolated -- they connect to each other. Show these links.

**Example**: 
- Gordon Growth: P_0 = D_1 / (r - g)
- Rearranged: r = D_1/P_0 + g (cost of equity from DDM)
- Since D_1 = D_0 x (1 + g), you can write: P_0 = D_0(1+g) / (r - g)

### 3. Define Every Variable

For each formula, list what every symbol means and its units.

### 4. Include the Decision Rule

Formulas are tools for making decisions. Include the rule:
- NPV: Accept if NPV > 0
- IRR: Accept if IRR > WACC
- Sharpe: Higher is better

### 5. Note Common Variants and Pitfalls

**Example for TVM**:
- Ordinary annuity: payments at END of period
- Annuity due: payments at BEGINNING -- multiply by (1 + r)
- Watch for: semi-annual compounding (divide rate by 2, multiply periods by 2)

## Template Structure

For each formula entry:

> **Name**: [Formula name]
> **Formula**: [Mathematical expression]
> **Variables**: [What each symbol means]
> **When to use**: [What problem type this solves]
> **Decision rule**: [How to interpret the result]
> **Watch out for**: [Common mistakes]

## What NOT to Include

- Derivations (you need the result, not the proof)
- Lengthy explanations (use keywords and abbreviations)
- Formulas you already know by heart (wastes space)
- Formulas not on the exam syllabus

## Study Tip: Build Your Own

The act of creating a formula sheet is itself a powerful study technique. It forces you to:
- Review all formulas
- Understand how they relate
- Decide what is most important
- Organize your knowledge

Even if the exam is closed-book, building the sheet is valuable study. For open-book exams, a well-organized sheet saves critical time.
