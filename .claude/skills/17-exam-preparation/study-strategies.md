# Study Strategies

Not all study methods are equally effective. Decades of cognitive science research shows that some techniques -- active recall, spaced repetition, and practice testing -- dramatically outperform the most popular methods (re-reading and highlighting). This guide covers evidence-based strategies tailored to finance courses.

## Why Study Strategy Matters

Most students default to re-reading notes and highlighting textbooks. These passive methods feel productive but produce weak long-term retention. Switching to active methods can double or triple your retention with the same study time.

## Active Recall

### What It Is

Testing yourself on material from memory, without looking at your notes. Retrieval practice strengthens the neural pathways to the information, making it easier to access during an exam.

### How to Apply It in Finance

**Flashcard method**: Write the concept on one side and the definition, formula, or explanation on the other.

- Front: "What is the Gordon Growth Model formula?"
- Back: "P_0 = D_1 / (r - g), where D_1 = next period's dividend, r = required return, g = growth rate"

**The blank page method**: After studying a topic (e.g., bond duration), close your notes and write everything you can remember on a blank page. Then open your notes and check what you missed. Re-study the gaps and repeat.

**Practice problems without solutions visible**: Work through problems before looking at the answer key. The struggle of retrieval is what builds memory.

### Worked Example: Active Recall for CAPM

Step 1: Study the CAPM section of your notes.
Step 2: Close your notes.
Step 3: On a blank page, write from memory:
- The CAPM formula
- What each variable represents
- The assumptions of the model
- An example calculation
- At least two limitations

Step 4: Open your notes and check. You forgot that beta measures systematic risk only (not total risk). This is now flagged for extra review.

## Spaced Repetition

### What It Is

Reviewing material at increasing intervals over time, rather than cramming everything in one session. Based on the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve: you forget 50-80% of new information within 48 hours unless you review it.

### Recommended Schedule

| Review | Timing After First Study |
|--------|------------------------|
| Review 1 | 1 day later |
| Review 2 | 3 days later |
| Review 3 | 7 days later |
| Review 4 | 14 days later |
| Review 5 | 30 days later |

### How to Implement

**Method 1: Leitner box system**. Sort flashcards into boxes:
- Box 1: Review daily (new or missed cards)
- Box 2: Review every 3 days (correct once)
- Box 3: Review weekly (correct twice)
- Box 4: Review biweekly (correct three times)
- Box 5: Review monthly (mastered)

When you get a card wrong, it moves back to Box 1 regardless of which box it was in.

**Method 2: Digital tools**. Anki or similar spaced repetition software automatically schedules reviews based on your performance.

### Starting Timeline

For a finance final exam, begin spaced repetition at least 4-6 weeks before the exam. Starting earlier is always better.

## The Feynman Technique

### What It Is

Explaining a concept in simple, plain language as if teaching it to someone with no background in finance. If you cannot explain it simply, you do not understand it well enough.

### The Four Steps

1. **Choose a concept**: e.g., "Why bond prices move inversely to interest rates"
2. **Explain it in simple language**: Write or speak an explanation using no jargon
3. **Identify gaps**: Where did you get stuck or resort to jargon?
4. **Simplify and re-study**: Go back to your notes, fill the gaps, try again

### Worked Example: Feynman Technique for Duration

**Attempt 1**: "Duration measures... um... it is the weighted average time... something about interest rate sensitivity..."

**Gap identified**: I know duration has something to do with interest rate sensitivity, but I cannot explain why or connect the weighted average time interpretation to the price sensitivity interpretation.

**Re-study**: Duration is the weighted average time until a bond's cash flows are received. It also approximates the percentage price change for a 1% change in yield. These two interpretations are connected because bonds with more distant cash flows are more sensitive to rate changes -- a change in the discount rate affects distant cash flows more than near-term ones.

**Attempt 2**: "Think of a seesaw. A bond's cash flows (coupons and principal) are like weights placed at different points along the seesaw. Duration tells you the balance point -- the average time until you receive your money. The further out the balance point (longer duration), the more the bond's price swings when interest rates change. That is because distant cash flows are discounted more heavily, so a change in the discount rate has a bigger impact on them."

**This explanation is clearer, uses an analogy, and connects the two interpretations.**

## Practice Testing

### Why Practice Tests Work

Practice testing combines active recall with exam-like conditions. It trains you to retrieve information under time pressure and helps you identify weak areas before the exam.

### How to Use Practice Tests Effectively

1. **Simulate exam conditions**: Timed, closed-book, no distractions
2. **Use old exams**: Ask your professor for past exams or find them in the library
3. **Work textbook problems**: End-of-chapter problems are designed to test the chapter's concepts
4. **Review errors systematically**: After each practice test, categorize your mistakes:
   - Did I not know the concept? (Knowledge gap -- re-study)
   - Did I know it but apply it wrong? (Application gap -- practice more problems)
   - Did I make a careless error? (Execution gap -- slow down, check work)

## Interleaving

### What It Is

Mixing different topics or problem types during a study session, rather than studying one topic at a time (blocking).

### Why It Works

Interleaving forces your brain to distinguish between problem types and choose the right approach -- exactly what an exam requires. Blocking (studying all TVM problems, then all CAPM problems) feels easier but produces weaker learning because you always know which method to apply.

### How to Interleave for Finance

Instead of: 60 minutes on TVM, then 60 minutes on bond pricing, then 60 minutes on CAPM.

Do: Mix problems from all three topics in a single 90-minute session. After each problem, switch to a different topic.

**Warning**: Interleaving feels harder and more frustrating than blocking. This is a feature, not a bug -- the difficulty is what strengthens learning.

## Creating a Study Plan

### 6-Week Exam Preparation Timeline

| Week | Focus | Method |
|------|-------|--------|
| 6 | First pass through all material; create flashcards | Active recall, Feynman technique |
| 5 | Review weak areas; begin spaced repetition | Spaced repetition, practice problems |
| 4 | Work through textbook problems (blocked by topic) | Practice testing |
| 3 | Work through mixed problem sets (interleaved) | Interleaving, practice testing |
| 2 | Full-length practice exams under timed conditions | Exam simulation |
| 1 | Review flagged gaps; light practice; rest | Targeted review, spaced repetition |

### Daily Study Structure (2-Hour Session)

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 0-10 min | Spaced repetition flashcard review |
| 10-40 min | Active recall: study new topic, then blank-page recall |
| 40-50 min | Break (critical for consolidation) |
| 50-90 min | Practice problems (interleaved from multiple topics) |
| 90-100 min | Review errors and update flashcards |
| 100-120 min | Feynman technique: explain one concept aloud |

## Common Mistakes

1. **Re-reading as the primary strategy**: Re-reading feels productive because the material becomes familiar. But familiarity is not the same as understanding. Active recall is far more effective.

2. **Cramming the night before**: Cramming produces short-term memory that fades within 24-48 hours. For cumulative finals, this means you remember almost nothing from early topics. Start early, space your practice.

3. **Highlighting everything**: Highlighting is passive and produces almost no learning benefit. If you must highlight, limit yourself to one sentence per page.

4. **Studying without testing**: If you only read and never test yourself, you do not know what you do not know. Regular self-testing reveals gaps before the exam does.

5. **Avoiding difficult topics**: The topics you avoid are usually the ones you need to study most. Confront weak areas early and allocate extra time to them.

6. **Studying in long, unbroken sessions**: Attention declines after 45-50 minutes. Take 10-minute breaks between study blocks. The Pomodoro technique (25 minutes study, 5 minutes break) works well for many students.
