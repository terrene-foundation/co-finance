# The International Monetary System

The institutional framework governing how exchange rates are determined, how international payments are made, and how countries adjust to imbalances. Understanding its evolution -- from gold standard to Bretton Woods to the current hybrid system -- provides essential context for all international finance topics.

## The Gold Standard (1870s-1914)

### How It Worked

Each country defined its currency in terms of a fixed weight of gold. Exchange rates were effectively fixed because any two currencies could be converted through gold.

**Price-specie flow mechanism** (David Hume, 1752):
1. Country A runs a trade deficit
2. Gold flows out of Country A to pay for imports
3. Less gold means less money supply, causing prices to fall in Country A
4. Lower prices make Country A's exports cheaper, improving the trade balance
5. Equilibrium is restored automatically

### Strengths and Weaknesses

| Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-----------|------------|
| Exchange rate stability | No independent monetary policy |
| Automatic adjustment mechanism | Deflationary bias (gold supply does not grow with the economy) |
| Discipline on fiscal policy | Adjustment falls disproportionately on deficit countries |
| Low inflation over the long run | Subject to gold supply shocks (discoveries, hoarding) |

### End of the Gold Standard

The gold standard was suspended during World War I as countries printed money to finance the war. Attempts to restore it in the 1920s failed. The Great Depression (1929-33) delivered the final blow, as countries abandoned gold convertibility to pursue expansionary monetary policies.

## The Bretton Woods System (1944-1971)

### Design

Negotiated in July 1944 at Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, by 44 Allied nations. Key architects: John Maynard Keynes (UK) and Harry Dexter White (US).

**Key features**:
- US dollar pegged to gold at $35 per ounce
- All other currencies pegged to the US dollar (adjustable pegs: +/- 1% band)
- Countries could devalue or revalue in cases of "fundamental disequilibrium" (with IMF approval)
- Capital controls were permitted (and common)
- Creation of the IMF and World Bank

### The Triffin Dilemma

Named after economist Robert Triffin (1960):

1. The world needed growing supplies of dollars for international trade and reserves
2. The US supplied dollars by running balance of payments deficits
3. But persistent deficits eroded confidence in the dollar's convertibility to gold
4. If the US eliminated deficits, the world would face a dollar shortage
5. If it continued deficits, eventually convertibility would be abandoned

This was an inherent contradiction that could not be permanently resolved.

### The Collapse

- By the late 1960s, US gold reserves were insufficient to cover all foreign-held dollars
- France (under de Gaulle) demanded gold for its dollar holdings
- Speculation against the dollar intensified
- **August 15, 1971**: President Nixon suspended dollar-gold convertibility ("Nixon shock")
- **Smithsonian Agreement (December 1971)**: Attempted to save fixed rates with wider bands (+/- 2.25%) and a devalued dollar ($38/oz gold). Failed within two years.
- **March 1973**: Major currencies began floating

## The Current System: Managed Floating

The current international monetary system is a hybrid -- often called a "non-system" because there is no single set of rules governing all countries.

### Exchange Rate Arrangements (IMF Classification)

| Category | Examples | Share of IMF Members |
|----------|---------|---------------------|
| Free floating | USD, EUR, GBP, JPY, AUD, CAD | ~30% |
| Managed floating | India, Brazil, Russia | ~25% |
| Conventional peg | Saudi Arabia (to USD), Denmark (to EUR) | ~20% |
| Currency board | Hong Kong, Bulgaria | ~5% |
| No separate legal tender | Ecuador (USD), Montenegro (EUR) | ~10% |
| Other (crawling peg, stabilized arrangement) | Various | ~10% |

### The US Dollar's Dominant Role

Despite the end of Bretton Woods, the US dollar remains the dominant international currency:
- ~60% of global foreign exchange reserves (down from ~70% in 2000)
- ~88% of all forex transactions involve the dollar on one side
- The primary invoicing currency for commodities (oil, gold, grains)
- The dominant currency for international bond issuance

**Why**: Network effects (everyone uses it because everyone else uses it), deep and liquid US financial markets, US economic size, and the US military and geopolitical role.

**"Exorbitant privilege"** (Valery Giscard d'Estaing): The US benefits from the dollar's reserve status because the world's demand for dollars allows the US to borrow cheaply and run persistent current account deficits.

## The Euro (EUR)

### Creation

The euro was introduced on January 1, 1999 (electronic) and January 1, 2002 (physical notes and coins). Currently used by 20 EU member states (the "Eurozone").

### Optimum Currency Area (OCA) Theory

Robert Mundell (1961) identified the conditions under which countries benefit from sharing a currency:

| OCA Criterion | Description | Does the Eurozone Meet It? |
|---------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| Labor mobility | Workers can move freely to find jobs | Partially (legal freedom, but language/cultural barriers) |
| Capital mobility | Capital flows freely | Yes |
| Fiscal transfers | A common fiscal authority redistributes funds | Weak (EU budget is small ~1% of GDP) |
| Business cycle synchronization | Countries experience similar booms and recessions | Mixed (Germany vs. Greece) |
| Flexible prices and wages | Prices adjust to absorb shocks | Limited |

**The Eurozone crisis (2010-15)** exposed the weakness: countries like Greece, Ireland, Portugal, Spain, and Italy could not devalue their currencies, had limited fiscal transfers from richer members, and faced rigid labor markets -- all OCA failures.

## Special Drawing Rights (SDR)

The SDR is an international reserve asset created by the IMF in 1969. It is not a currency but a potential claim on freely usable currencies of IMF members.

**SDR basket** (as of 2022 review):
- US Dollar: 43.38%
- Euro: 29.31%
- Chinese Yuan: 12.28%
- Japanese Yen: 7.59%
- British Pound: 7.44%

**Use**: Countries can exchange SDRs for freely usable currencies with other IMF members. SDR allocations supplement official reserves without requiring any country to run a deficit.

## The International Monetary Fund (IMF)

### Core Functions

1. **Surveillance**: Monitors global economic conditions and each member's economic policies (Article IV consultations)
2. **Lending**: Provides financial assistance to countries facing balance of payments difficulties
3. **Technical assistance**: Helps countries build institutional capacity (central banking, fiscal management, statistics)

### IMF Lending Facilities

| Facility | Purpose | Conditionality |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| Stand-By Arrangement (SBA) | Short-term BOP crises | Standard (fiscal, monetary, structural reforms) |
| Extended Fund Facility (EFF) | Longer-term structural problems | More extensive reforms |
| Rapid Financing Instrument (RFI) | Emergency (natural disasters, pandemics) | Light conditionality |
| Flexible Credit Line (FCL) | Precautionary, for strong economies | No conditionality (pre-qualified) |

### Conditionality Debate

IMF lending comes with conditions -- typically fiscal austerity, monetary tightening, and structural reforms. Critics argue:
- **Pro**: Conditions ensure the borrowing country addresses the root causes of the crisis and can repay the IMF
- **Con**: Austerity can deepen recessions, increase poverty, and create political instability. "One size fits all" policies may not suit every country

## Dollarization

When a country adopts a foreign currency (usually the USD) as legal tender, fully replacing its domestic currency.

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Official (full)** | Foreign currency is the sole legal tender | Ecuador (2000), El Salvador (2001), Panama (1904) |
| **Semi-official** | Foreign currency circulates alongside domestic currency | Cambodia (USD), many CFA franc zone countries (EUR-linked) |
| **Unofficial** | Citizens use foreign currency informally, especially for savings | Many countries with high inflation |

**Advantages**: Eliminates currency risk, lower interest rates, monetary credibility
**Disadvantages**: No independent monetary policy, no lender of last resort, loss of seigniorage revenue, cannot adjust to asymmetric shocks

## Ongoing Debates

### De-dollarization

Several countries (China, Russia, Brazil, India, Saudi Arabia) have explored alternatives to the dollar for trade settlement. Key developments include bilateral currency swap agreements, BRICS discussions about alternative payment systems, and China's promotion of the yuan. However, replacing the dollar faces enormous network-effect barriers.

### Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)

Over 100 countries are exploring CBDCs. The implications for the international monetary system include potential for faster cross-border payments, possible reduction in dollar dominance (if China's digital yuan gains traction), and new challenges for capital flow management.

### Global Financial Safety Net

The overlapping system of IMF facilities, regional arrangements (e.g., European Stability Mechanism, Chiang Mai Initiative), bilateral swap lines (e.g., Fed swap lines), and sovereign reserves that protect countries against financial crises.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming Bretton Woods was a pure gold standard**: It was a gold-exchange standard. Only the dollar was convertible to gold; other currencies were pegged to the dollar.

2. **Treating the current system as purely "floating"**: Many countries manage their exchange rates. True free floating is practiced by only about 30% of IMF members.

3. **Confusing SDRs with a currency**: SDRs are an accounting unit and reserve asset, not a currency. You cannot walk into a shop and pay with SDRs.

4. **Assuming the euro will inevitably fail (or inevitably succeed)**: The euro has survived serious crises but continues to face structural challenges. Its future depends on political decisions about fiscal integration.

5. **Equating dollarization with a currency board**: In a currency board (e.g., Hong Kong), the domestic currency still exists but is fully backed by foreign reserves. In dollarization, there is no domestic currency at all.
