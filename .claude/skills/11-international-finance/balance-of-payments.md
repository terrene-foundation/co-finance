# Balance of Payments

The balance of payments (BOP) is a systematic record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world over a given period (usually a quarter or year). It is the accounting framework for understanding a country's international economic position.

## Why the BOP Matters

The BOP tells you:
- Whether a country is a net exporter or importer of goods and services
- Whether foreign capital is flowing in or out
- How the central bank's foreign reserves are changing
- Whether the country's international position is sustainable

Think of it as a country's financial statement for its dealings with the rest of the world.

## BOP Structure

The BOP consists of three main accounts that must sum to zero (by construction):

> **Current Account + Capital Account + Financial Account + Statistical Discrepancy = 0**

### 1. Current Account (CA)

Records flows of goods, services, income, and transfers.

| Component | Examples | Typical Sign |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Trade balance** (goods) | Exports and imports of physical goods | Deficit for US, surplus for Germany/China |
| **Services balance** | Tourism, consulting, shipping, software, financial services | Surplus for US, UK |
| **Primary income** | Interest, dividends, profits on foreign investments | Depends on net foreign asset position |
| **Secondary income** (transfers) | Foreign aid, remittances, gifts | Usually deficit for developed countries |

> **Current Account = Trade Balance + Services Balance + Primary Income + Secondary Income**

A **current account deficit** means the country is spending more abroad than it is earning from abroad -- it must finance the difference by borrowing from or selling assets to foreigners.

A **current account surplus** means the country is earning more from abroad than it spends -- it is lending to or buying assets from foreigners.

### 2. Capital Account

Records capital transfers and acquisition/disposal of non-produced, non-financial assets.

This is a small account for most countries. Examples:
- Debt forgiveness
- Migrant transfers (assets brought when moving countries)
- Sales of patents, copyrights, trademarks

### 3. Financial Account

Records transactions in financial assets and liabilities.

| Component | Examples |
|-----------|----------|
| **Direct investment** (FDI) | Building a factory abroad, acquiring a controlling stake in a foreign company |
| **Portfolio investment** | Buying foreign stocks or bonds (non-controlling stakes) |
| **Other investment** | Bank loans, trade credits, currency and deposits |
| **Reserve assets** | Central bank transactions in foreign exchange reserves, gold, SDRs |

> **Financial Account = Direct Investment + Portfolio Investment + Other Investment + Reserve Assets**

A **financial account surplus** (net capital inflow) means foreigners are investing more in the country than its residents are investing abroad.

## The BOP Identity

> **CA + KA + FA = 0**

(where FA is defined with the sign convention that a surplus means net capital inflow)

Equivalently:

> **Current Account Deficit = Net Capital Inflow**

**Intuition**: If a country buys more from the rest of the world than it sells (current account deficit), it must finance the difference by receiving capital from abroad (financial account surplus). The money has to come from somewhere.

### Worked Example: BOP Identity

Country X has:
- Exports of goods: $200 billion
- Imports of goods: $300 billion
- Services balance: +$30 billion
- Primary income: +$10 billion
- Secondary income: -$15 billion
- Capital account: $0

Current Account = (200 - 300) + 30 + 10 - 15 = **-$75 billion** (deficit)

By the BOP identity, the financial account must show a net capital inflow of approximately $75 billion (plus any statistical discrepancy). This means foreigners are investing $75 billion more in Country X than Country X's residents are investing abroad.

## Twin Deficits Hypothesis

The "twin deficits" hypothesis argues that a government budget deficit leads to a current account deficit.

**The logic** (from national income accounting):

> **CA = (S_private - I) + (T - G)**

Where:
- S_private = Private sector saving
- I = Private investment
- T = Tax revenue
- G = Government spending
- (T - G) = Government budget balance

If the government runs a budget deficit (T < G) and private saving and investment do not change, the current account must deteriorate.

**US Example**: The US has run "twin deficits" for much of the period since the 1980s -- persistent budget deficits alongside persistent current account deficits.

**Criticism**: The twin deficits relationship does not always hold. Private saving may increase to offset government dissaving (Ricardian equivalence). Many countries have had fiscal deficits without current account deficits, and vice versa.

## Current Account Sustainability

A current account deficit is not inherently bad -- it depends on what the imported capital is used for.

**Sustainable deficit**: Foreign capital finances productive investment (new factories, infrastructure, technology) that will generate future export earnings or import substitution.

**Unsustainable deficit**: Foreign capital finances consumption or unproductive investment (real estate bubbles, government consumption). The country accumulates debt without building capacity to repay.

**Warning signs of unsustainability**:
- CA deficit exceeding 4-5% of GDP persistently
- Rising short-term external debt
- Declining foreign exchange reserves
- Real exchange rate appreciation driven by capital inflows rather than productivity gains
- Heavy reliance on portfolio investment (hot money) rather than FDI

## BOP Crises

A BOP crisis occurs when a country cannot finance its current account deficit -- foreign creditors lose confidence and capital inflows dry up or reverse (capital flight).

**Typical sequence**:
1. Country runs persistent, large current account deficits
2. Deficits are financed by short-term capital inflows
3. A shock (domestic or external) triggers a loss of confidence
4. Capital outflows accelerate; foreign reserves deplete
5. The exchange rate collapses (if floating) or the peg breaks (if fixed)
6. The country may need IMF assistance

See [currency-crises.md](currency-crises.md) for detailed models.

## BOP Analysis: Key Ratios

| Ratio | Formula | Interpretation |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| CA/GDP | Current Account / GDP | > 4% deficit is often considered unsustainable |
| Trade balance/GDP | (Exports - Imports) / GDP | Core measure of trade competitiveness |
| FDI/GDP | Foreign Direct Investment / GDP | Higher FDI ratio suggests more stable financing |
| Reserves/Imports | Foreign Reserves / Monthly Imports | < 3 months of import coverage is concerning |
| Short-term debt/Reserves | Short-term External Debt / Reserves | > 1.0 is a warning sign (Guidotti-Greenspan rule) |

## Common Mistakes

1. **Treating a current account deficit as always bad**: A deficit can be healthy if it reflects productive investment. The US ran current account deficits throughout its high-growth industrialization period in the 19th century.

2. **Confusing the trade balance with the current account**: The current account includes services, income, and transfers in addition to goods trade. Some countries have goods trade deficits but current account surpluses (due to services or investment income).

3. **Forgetting the BOP must balance**: If someone claims a country has both a current account deficit and a financial account deficit, something is wrong. By construction, one finances the other.

4. **Ignoring the composition of capital flows**: $10 billion in FDI (building factories) is very different from $10 billion in short-term portfolio flows (hot money). FDI is stable; portfolio flows can reverse overnight.

5. **Assuming trade deficits are caused by trade policy alone**: Current account balances are fundamentally determined by the difference between national saving and investment. Tariffs may shift trade between partners but typically do not change the overall balance.

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1**: A country has goods exports of $150B, goods imports of $220B, services exports of $80B, services imports of $50B, primary income of +$15B, and secondary income of -$10B. Calculate the current account balance. What must the financial account balance approximately equal?

**Problem 2**: Explain why a country with a high saving rate (like Japan or Germany) tends to run current account surpluses, using the relationship CA = S - I.

**Problem 3**: A small economy has a current account deficit of 8% of GDP, financed primarily by short-term portfolio flows. Foreign reserves cover 2 months of imports. Assess the sustainability of this situation.
