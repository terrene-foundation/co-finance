# Currency Crises

A currency crisis occurs when a country experiences a rapid, large depreciation of its currency, often accompanied by speculative attacks, capital flight, and depletion of foreign exchange reserves. Understanding why crises happen is critical for international finance students -- they reshape economies, destroy wealth, and expose the vulnerabilities of monetary systems.

## What Constitutes a Currency Crisis?

There is no single definition, but common quantitative thresholds include:
- Nominal depreciation exceeding 15-25% in a single year
- Foreign reserve losses exceeding 10-15% in a quarter
- A sharp interest rate spike as the central bank defends the currency

Qualitatively, a crisis involves a loss of confidence in the currency, triggering a self-reinforcing cycle of selling pressure.

## First-Generation Models: Krugman (1979)

### The Setup

A government runs persistent fiscal deficits financed by money creation (printing money). The central bank simultaneously tries to maintain a fixed exchange rate by selling foreign reserves.

### The Mechanism

1. Government runs budget deficits, financed by expanding the money supply
2. Money supply growth creates inflationary pressure and downward pressure on the exchange rate
3. To maintain the fixed peg, the central bank sells foreign reserves and buys domestic currency
4. Reserves gradually deplete
5. When reserves fall to a critical level, speculators launch an attack, buying up remaining reserves in exchange for domestic currency
6. The peg collapses

### Key Insight

The crisis is **inevitable and predictable**. Speculators can calculate when reserves will run out and rationally attack just before that point. The speculative attack merely accelerates a collapse that was going to happen anyway.

> **The timing of the attack is determined by**: shadow exchange rate > fixed rate, where the shadow rate is what the exchange rate would be under a float given current fundamentals.

**Analogy**: Imagine a person spending more than they earn every month and drawing down their savings account to maintain their lifestyle. Friends (speculators) can see the bank account declining. Eventually, they stop lending because they know the money will run out. The lifestyle collapses -- not because of the friends' behavior, but because the spending was unsustainable.

*Source: Krugman, P. (1979). "A Model of Balance-of-Payments Crises." Journal of Money, Credit and Banking, 11(3), 311-325.*

## Second-Generation Models: Obstfeld (1994)

### The Setup

The government has a choice: maintain the peg (at a cost) or abandon it. The crisis occurs because speculator expectations become self-fulfilling.

### The Mechanism: Self-Fulfilling Expectations

1. The government maintains a fixed rate, but it involves costs (high interest rates, unemployment)
2. If speculators believe the peg will hold, there is no attack, and the cost of maintaining it is low -- so the government maintains it
3. If speculators believe the peg will fail, they attack. Defending against the attack requires very high interest rates, which impose severe economic costs
4. Facing these costs, the government rationally chooses to abandon the peg
5. The speculators' belief was self-fulfilling: the attack caused the very outcome they predicted

### Key Insight

Crises can occur even when fundamentals are not clearly unsustainable. There are **multiple equilibria**: a "good" equilibrium (no attack, peg survives) and a "bad" equilibrium (attack, peg collapses). A shift in expectations can move the economy from one to the other.

**Analogy**: Think of a bank run. The bank is solvent if all depositors keep their money in. But if enough depositors believe others will withdraw, they all rush to withdraw, and the bank becomes insolvent -- even though it was fundamentally sound.

*Source: Obstfeld, M. (1994). "The Logic of Currency Crises." Cahiers Economiques et Monetaires, 43, 189-213.*

## Third-Generation Models: Twin Crises (1990s)

### The Setup

Currency crises and banking crises occur together ("twin crises"), driven by financial sector vulnerabilities and moral hazard.

### The Mechanism

1. Financial liberalization leads to excessive lending, often in foreign currency
2. Banks and corporations borrow in dollars but earn revenue in domestic currency (currency mismatch)
3. An external shock or loss of confidence triggers capital outflows
4. The domestic currency depreciates
5. Currency depreciation dramatically increases the domestic-currency value of foreign-currency debt
6. Borrowers cannot repay, leading to a banking crisis
7. The banking crisis deepens the recession, causing further capital flight and currency depreciation
8. A vicious cycle ensues

### Key Insight

**Balance sheet effects** amplify the crisis. A 50% depreciation doubles the domestic-currency value of foreign-currency debt. Companies that were solvent yesterday are bankrupt today.

*Source: Kaminsky, G.L. & Reinhart, C.M. (1999). "The Twin Crises: The Causes of Banking and Balance-of-Payments Problems." American Economic Review, 89(3), 473-500.*

## Contagion

Currency crises often spread across countries. Mechanisms include:

| Channel | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Trade linkages** | Crisis in one country reduces imports from trading partners | Mexico crisis 1994 --> impact on Latin American exporters to Mexico |
| **Financial linkages** | Banks exposed to crisis country suffer losses, pull capital from similar countries | European bank losses in Asia 1997 --> reduced lending to all emerging markets |
| **Wake-up call** | Crisis in one country causes investors to re-examine similar vulnerabilities elsewhere | Thailand 1997 --> "wake-up" to similar weaknesses in Indonesia, Korea |
| **Herding/Panic** | Investors flee all "risky" assets regardless of fundamentals | Flight to safety: sell all emerging market assets |
| **Common lender** | A bank or investor with losses in one country withdraws from others | Japanese banks pulling out of all Asian economies in 1997-98 |

## Historical Case Studies

### Mexican Peso Crisis (1994-95) -- "Tequila Crisis"

**Background**: Mexico maintained a crawling peg. Large current account deficit (~7% of GDP) financed by short-term dollar-denominated government bonds (Tesobonos).

**Trigger**: Political instability (Zapatista uprising, assassination of presidential candidate) led to capital flight. Reserves depleted as the central bank defended the peso.

**Crisis**: December 1994 -- peso devalued 15%, then floated. Depreciated from 3.4 to 7.5 per dollar within months. GDP fell 6.2% in 1995.

**Resolution**: $50 billion rescue package (US Treasury, IMF, BIS).

**Lesson**: Short-term, dollar-denominated debt creates extreme vulnerability. Even countries with good reforms can be hit by confidence crises.

### Asian Financial Crisis (1997-98)

**Countries affected**: Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea, Malaysia, Philippines (and broader contagion).

**Background**: Fixed or semi-fixed exchange rates. Rapid credit growth, much of it in US dollars. Property and stock market bubbles. Weak financial regulation.

**Trigger**: Thailand's baht peg collapsed July 2, 1997, after months of speculative attacks and reserve depletion. Contagion spread rapidly.

**Impact**:
- Indonesia: Rupiah fell from 2,600 to over 16,000 per dollar. GDP fell 13.1% in 1998.
- South Korea: Won fell from 900 to 1,700 per dollar. Required $57 billion IMF package.
- Thailand: Baht fell from 25 to 55 per dollar. GDP fell 10.5% in 1998.

**Resolution**: IMF programs with controversial conditionality (fiscal austerity, high interest rates, structural reforms). Malaysia imposed capital controls instead.

**Lessons**: Currency mismatches on corporate balance sheets amplify crises. Crony capitalism and weak financial regulation create hidden vulnerabilities. IMF conditionality was debated -- austerity may have worsened the downturn.

### Argentine Crisis (2001-02)

**Background**: Currency board: 1 peso = 1 US dollar (since 1991). Board succeeded in ending hyperinflation but made the economy rigid. Persistent fiscal deficits, rising public debt.

**Problem**: Dollar strengthened in the late 1990s, making Argentine exports uncompetitive. Recession from 1998. Debt-to-GDP ratio rose to 60%+.

**Crisis**: Capital flight and bank runs in late 2001. Government froze bank deposits ("corralito"). Currency board abandoned January 2002. Peso collapsed from 1:1 to 4:1. Default on $95 billion in sovereign debt (then the largest sovereign default in history). GDP fell 10.9% in 2002.

**Lesson**: A fixed exchange rate without an exit mechanism, combined with fiscal indiscipline, is a recipe for disaster.

## Early Warning Indicators

Research has identified several indicators that tend to signal an approaching currency crisis:

| Indicator | Warning Threshold | Rationale |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|
| Current account deficit / GDP | > 4-5% | Unsustainable external imbalance |
| Real exchange rate appreciation | > 15-20% above trend | Overvaluation, loss of competitiveness |
| Foreign reserve decline | Rapid depletion | Central bank running out of ammunition |
| Short-term debt / Reserves | > 1.0 (Guidotti-Greenspan rule) | Cannot cover all short-term obligations |
| Credit growth | > 20% per year | Potential lending bubble |
| M2 / Reserves | Rising rapidly | Domestic money supply outstripping foreign backing |
| Banking sector NPLs | Rising | Financial fragility building |

## The Impossible Trinity

A country cannot simultaneously have all three of:
1. **Fixed exchange rate**
2. **Free capital mobility**
3. **Independent monetary policy**

It must give up one:
- **US**: Free capital flows + independent monetary policy --> floating exchange rate
- **Hong Kong**: Free capital flows + fixed rate --> no independent monetary policy (currency board)
- **China (historically)**: Fixed rate + independent policy --> capital controls

Currency crises often occur when countries try to maintain all three, which is inherently unstable.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Treating all crises as the same type**: First-gen (fiscal), second-gen (self-fulfilling), and third-gen (balance sheet) crises have different causes and require different policy responses.

2. **Blaming speculators for causing crises**: In first-generation models, speculators merely hasten an inevitable collapse. In second-generation models, they can trigger one of multiple possible outcomes, but the vulnerability must exist first.

3. **Assuming fixed rates prevent crises**: Fixed rates can mask building vulnerabilities and delay adjustment, often making the eventual crisis worse.

4. **Ignoring the impossible trinity**: Believing a country can have a fixed rate, open capital account, and independent monetary policy simultaneously.

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1**: A country has a fixed exchange rate and persistent budget deficits financed by money creation. Foreign reserves are declining at $2 billion per quarter from a starting level of $30 billion. Using a first-generation framework, explain the dynamics and estimate when the peg might collapse.

**Problem 2**: Country A has strong fundamentals but a fixed exchange rate. Country B, which has similar economic characteristics, just suffered a currency crisis. Using second-generation and contagion concepts, explain how Country A might also experience a crisis.

**Problem 3**: A corporation in an emerging market borrows $100 million at an exchange rate of 50 local currency units per dollar. The local currency depreciates to 80 per dollar.
