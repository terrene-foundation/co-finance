# Event 5: Central Bank "Super Week" and Monetary Policy Divergence

## Summary

Seven major central banks will announce decisions in March 16-19 — the most consequential week for global monetary policy in years. Central banks globally are caught between competing forces: oil-driven inflation (argues against rate cuts) vs. slowing growth and geopolitical uncertainty (argues for cuts).

## Financial Significance

- **The stagflation dilemma returns**: Supply-side inflation (oil) + weakening demand = no good options for central banks
- **BOE reversal**: The swing from "expected to cut" to "70% odds of hike" illustrates how a single geopolitical event can completely reverse policy expectations
- **Dollar weakness paradox**: Tariffs were expected to strengthen the dollar; the opposite occurred. Retaliation weakens the dollar
- **EM divergence**: Turkey at 37% vs Switzerland at 0% — an extraordinary range reflecting vastly different economic conditions

## Discussion Angles

- How should central banks respond to a supply-side oil shock? Compare the "lean against inflation" vs "support growth" arguments
- Why is the dollar weakening despite higher tariffs? Does this contradict textbook expectations?
- What is the "impossible trinity" (monetary independence, fixed exchange rate, free capital flows) and how does it constrain these central banks differently?
- How does the yen carry trade work, and what happens when safe-haven demand conflicts with carry trade positioning?
- Why are some EM central banks cutting (Brazil, Turkey) while others hold or hike (India, Australia)?
