# Event 1: US-Israel War on Iran and Strait of Hormuz Crisis

## Summary

On February 28, 2026, the US and Israel launched joint military strikes on Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei and senior officials. Iran retaliated with missile and drone attacks. The Strait of Hormuz — through which ~20% of global oil supply transits — experienced a de facto closure as insurance companies withdrew coverage and tanker traffic dropped to near zero.

## Timeline

- **Feb 28**: Joint US-Israel strikes begin
- **Mar 3-9**: Peak market disruption; oil surges from ~$71 to $110+/barrel (~55% increase)
- **Mar 9**: Brent briefly touches $120/barrel
- **Mar 10**: Prices fall sharply after Trump signals war will end "very soon"

## Countries/Regions Affected

United States, Israel, Iran, Gulf states; downstream impact on all energy-importing nations — especially Japan (90% of oil from Middle East, 70% through Hormuz), South Korea, China, India, Europe.

## Financial Significance

- **Oil**: Biggest weekly price surge since crude futures began trading in 1983
- **Consumer impact**: US gasoline prices rose 48 cents/gallon in the first week
- **Trade disruption**: Not just crude oil — LNG, LPG, jet fuel, fertilizer, and containerized trade between Asia and Middle East/Europe all affected
- **IMF estimate**: Every 10% rise in oil prices adds 0.4% to inflation and reduces growth by 0.15%
- **Safe-haven flows**: Capital rushed into gold, Japanese yen, Swiss franc

## Discussion Angles

- How do energy supply shocks differ from demand-driven price changes in their financial market effects?
- Why are Asian economies disproportionately affected compared to the Americas?
- How does the "financialization" of oil (futures, options, swaps) amplify or dampen physical supply shocks?
- Compare this to the 1973 oil embargo — what's structurally different about the global energy system today?
