# Event 2: US Supreme Court Strikes Down IEEPA Tariffs

## Summary

On February 20, 2026, the US Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in _Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump_ that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) does not authorize presidential tariffs, holding that tariff power is a "taxing power" reserved for Congress under Article I. This eliminated the legal basis for ~$142-175 billion in tariffs collected from 330,000+ businesses.

## Timeline

- **Feb 20**: Supreme Court ruling issued
- **Feb 24**: Administration pivots — imposes 10% global tariff under Section 122 of Trade Act of 1974 (capped at 15%, limited to 150 days)
- **Mar 4**: Additional 10% tariff on China; tariffs on Canada/Mexico take effect; USMCA-compliant goods exempted until April 2
- **Mar 11**: USTR launches Section 301 investigations against 16 countries (China, EU, Mexico, Japan, India, South Korea, Taiwan, and others) to build longer-term legal tariff authority

## Countries/Regions Affected

United States vs. all major trading partners. The 16 Section 301 targets represent the vast majority of US trade volume.

## Financial Significance

- **Constitutional watershed**: Most consequential trade law decision in decades — fundamentally constrains presidential trade authority
- **Refund liability**: $142-175 billion in potentially unconstitutional tariffs across 53 million customs entries must be processed for refunds
- **150-day cliff**: Section 122 authority expires July 24, 2026 — creating urgency for Section 301 investigations
- **US effective tariff rate**: Rose to ~13% trade-weighted, the highest since the 1930s-1940s
- **NY Fed finding**: Nearly 90% of tariff costs borne by American firms and consumers, not foreign exporters

## Discussion Angles

- How does the separation of powers (executive vs. legislative) affect trade policy credibility and predictability?
- What happens to business investment and supply chain planning when the legal basis for tariffs shifts every few weeks?
- Compare Section 122 (temporary, capped) vs. IEEPA (broad, uncapped) vs. Section 301 (targeted, investigation-based) — how do legal constraints shape economic outcomes?
- Who actually bears the cost of tariffs? Discuss the NY Fed finding in the context of trade theory.
