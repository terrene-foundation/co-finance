# Event 12: Sanctions Developments

## Key Actions (Late February - March 9)

### Russia/Energy

- **UK/EU coordination**: UK indicated it could act alongside the EU to ban maritime services for Russian oil shipments, going beyond current restrictions, even without US backing
- **Significance**: Would meaningfully tighten enforcement of existing oil price caps

### Rwanda/DRC

- **March 2**: US imposed sanctions on Rwandan Defence Force and four senior officers for supporting M23 rebels in the Democratic Republic of Congo
- **Significance**: Demonstrates US willingness to target African military actors

### Sudan

- EU added four RSF (Rapid Support Forces) commanders to sanctions list following UN Security Council designations

### Venezuela

- US continues to control Venezuelan oil sales "indefinitely" following January 2026 military operation that removed Maduro

## Financial Significance

- **Compliance complexity**: Simultaneous sanctions regimes against Russia, Iran, Venezuela, Sudan, Rwanda create enormous compliance burdens for banks, insurers, and shipping companies
- **Maritime insurance**: A key enforcement tool — banning maritime services effectively blocks tanker operations
- **Energy market effects**: Russian oil sanctions interact with Iran war disruptions to further constrain global supply

## Discussion Angles

- How effective are financial sanctions as a tool of foreign policy? Compare success/failure cases
- What is "secondary sanctions risk" and how does it affect banks and companies in third countries?
- How do sanctions on Russian oil interact with the Iran war to compound energy market disruptions?
- What role does the global financial infrastructure (SWIFT, correspondent banking) play in sanctions enforcement?
