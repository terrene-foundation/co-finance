# Class Discussion Guide: The Oil-Trade-Monetary Policy Triangle

_Ready-to-use discussion materials for a 50-75 minute International Finance class session_

## Session Structure (Suggested)

| Time      | Activity                                  | Purpose                               |
| --------- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| 0-10 min  | Opening questions (whole class)           | Warm up, gauge baseline understanding |
| 10-25 min | Conceptual questions (pairs/small groups) | Connect events to theory              |

## 1. Opening Questions (Accessible — Any Student Can Engage)

These require no prior knowledge of theory. They get students thinking about the situation from a human/business perspective.

**Q1**: "Oil prices jumped 60% in two weeks because of the Iran war. If you ran a trucking company, an airline, or a grocery store, what would you do right now?"

_Purpose_: Gets students thinking about real-world business responses to price shocks before introducing theory.

**Q2**: "The Supreme Court just told the President he can't impose tariffs the way he has been. $175 billion in refunds may be owed. If you were a business that had been paying those tariffs for a year, what would you want to happen?"

_Purpose_: Makes the tariff issue tangible — it's not abstract trade policy, it's real money that businesses paid and may get back.

**Q3**: "South Korea's stock market crashed 18% in two days — worse than any other country. Why would South Korea be hit harder than, say, the United States or China?"

_Purpose_: Students intuitively grasp that energy dependence + export dependence = vulnerability. This sets up the theoretical framework naturally.

**Q4**: "If you were on the Federal Reserve's committee and had to decide interest rates next week, what would you do — raise, cut, or hold? Why?"

_Purpose_: Immediately puts students in the central banker's shoes. Most will realize there's no good answer — that IS the point.

## 2. Conceptual Questions (Connecting Events to Theory)

These require understanding of basic international finance concepts. Best for pair or small-group discussion.

**Q5**: "Using the Mundell-Fleming trilemma (impossible trinity), explain why Japan and China face the same oil shock but have very different policy options."

_Expected answer direction_: Japan chose free capital flows + monetary independence → floating yen → can't control exchange rate → weak yen amplifies oil costs. China chose monetary independence + managed rate → capital controls → can set rates freely + manage yuan → better insulated from capital flight but at cost of financial openness.

**Q6**: "Trade theory predicts that tariffs should strengthen the imposing country's currency. The US imposed the highest tariffs since the 1930s, and the dollar weakened 9.4% in 2025. What went wrong with the prediction?"

_Expected answer direction_: Retaliation reduced demand for US exports (reducing dollar demand); uncertainty drove capital away; fiscal deficits increased dollar supply; the textbook model assumes no retaliation and stable expectations.

**Q7**: "The Bank of England was expected to cut interest rates in March. After the Iran war started, the probability of a rate _hike_ rose to 70%. What changed, and what does this tell us about how central banks actually make decisions?"

_Expected answer direction_: Oil shock added an expected 1 percentage point to UK inflation (possible 5%). The BOE's single-variable change (oil price) completely reversed the policy outlook. Central banks are not on autopilot — they're constantly re-optimizing based on new information. The 5-4 vote (five to hold, four to cut — before the shock) shows how close the margin was.

**Q8**: "China's exports grew 21.8% despite US tariffs of ~30%. Does this mean tariffs don't work?"

_Expected answer direction_: Tariffs "work" in the narrow sense of reducing US imports from China. But they don't reduce China's total exports — trade diversion redirects goods to ASEAN, Europe, etc. Whether tariffs "work" depends on what goal you're measuring: protecting US industries (partly), reducing bilateral deficit (not really), reducing China's economic power (definitely not).

## 3. Analytical Questions (Requiring Synthesis)

These are harder and require connecting multiple events and concepts. Best for advanced students or group work.

**Q9**: "Trace the full causal chain: Iran war → oil price → inflation → central bank response → exchange rates → emerging market capital flows. At each step, identify the key assumption that could break the chain."

_Expected chain_:

1. Iran war → oil price surge _(assumption: Hormuz remains closed)_
2. Oil surge → inflation _(assumption: prices stay elevated long enough for pass-through)_
3. Inflation → central banks hold/raise rates _(assumption: they don't accommodate)_
4. Higher rates → stronger dollar/developed currencies _(assumption: rate differential matters more than risk-off)_
5. Stronger developed currencies → EM capital outflows _(assumption: investors chase yield in developed markets)_

_Key break point_: If Trump ends the war "very soon" (as he signaled March 10), oil drops, and the entire chain reverses.

**Q10**: "Compare the Fed's situation to the ECB's. Both face inflation pressure from the oil shock. Why are their policy options different?"

_Expected answer direction_: The Fed has a dual mandate (price stability + employment) — it must balance both. The ECB has a single mandate (price stability) — it can focus on inflation alone. The US is a net energy producer (partially insulated); Europe is a net importer (more exposed). The euro has appreciated 14% vs. dollar (cushioning oil costs in euro terms); the dollar has weakened (amplifying costs). The US imposed tariffs (adding domestic inflation); the EU is a tariff victim, not a tariff imposer.

**Q11**: "Germany just broke its balanced-budget rule to spend more on defense. How does one EU member's fiscal expansion affect monetary policy for the entire 20-member eurozone?"

_Expected answer direction_: Germany's increased Bund issuance raises yields across Europe (benchmark effect). Higher German growth from fiscal stimulus is inflationary for the eurozone, potentially keeping ECB rates higher. Other EU members may feel pressure to also increase spending, compounding the effect. The ECB cannot set different rates for Germany vs. Greece — one policy for 20 economies with different fiscal stances.

## 4. Debate Prompts (Structured Debates)

### Debate A: Volcker vs. Burns

**Resolved**: "The Federal Reserve should raise interest rates at the March meeting, even if it causes a recession, because failing to fight inflation now will make it worse later."

**For** (Volcker position):

- Dallas Fed paper: 1970s inflation was caused by monetary policy errors, not oil shocks
- Burns' accommodation led to a decade of stagflation; Volcker's pain lasted 2 years and solved it
- If inflation expectations become unanchored, the eventual cost is much higher
- The Fed's credibility — built over 40 years since Volcker — is at stake

**Against** (Burns/Accommodation position):

- Today is different: US produces oil domestically; economy is less energy-intensive
- Raising rates into a supply shock adds a demand hit on top — double punishment for consumers
- 92,000 jobs already lost; a rate hike could tip the economy into recession
- The tariff inflation isn't the Fed's problem to solve — it's fiscal/trade policy's problem

### Debate B: Tariff Effectiveness

**Resolved**: "Unilateral tariffs are an ineffective tool for reducing trade deficits, as demonstrated by China's export surge and the dollar's decline."

**For** (tariffs are ineffective):

- China's exports up 21.8% — trade diversion, not destruction
- US exports to China down 26% — retaliation is making the deficit worse
- Dollar weakened 9.4% — opposite of textbook prediction
- NY Fed: 90% of costs on domestic firms/consumers, not foreign exporters
- Smoot-Hawley historical parallel: retaliation made things worse

**Against** (tariffs have value):

- Tariffs generate revenue ($142B+ collected under IEEPA)
- They provide leverage for trade negotiations (US-India $500B framework)
- They protect domestic industries from unfair competition
- Short-term pain for long-term restructuring of supply chains
- Section 301 investigations may produce more targeted, effective tariffs

### Debate C: Energy Security vs. Free Trade

**Resolved**: "The Strait of Hormuz crisis proves that energy security is more important than free trade, and countries should prioritize energy independence even at economic cost."

**For** (energy security first):

- 20% of global oil through one chokepoint is a structural vulnerability
- Japan (90% Middle East oil) saw its market crash — that's a national security risk
- The cost of energy independence (renewables, nuclear, domestic production) is finite; the cost of a Hormuz closure is catastrophic
- History: every major oil shock caused a recession

**Against** (free trade is more resilient):

- Autarky (self-sufficiency) is more expensive than trade — comparative advantage exists
- The IEA strategic reserves provided 400M barrels of cushion — that's the point of the system
- US shale + EVs + renewables are already reducing dependence organically
- Forcing energy independence means higher domestic energy costs permanently

## 5. Group Activities

### Activity A: Central Bank Committee Role-Play (25-30 minutes)

**Setup**: Divide the class into 5 groups. Each group represents one central bank: Fed, ECB, BOJ, BOE, PBOC.

**Task**: Each group must:

1. Decide their rate action (raise, cut, or hold) with justification
2. Present a 3-minute "policy statement" explaining their decision
3. Address questions from "the press" (other students)

**Materials needed**: Give each group their country's profile from the Case Studies section (05-country-case-studies.md).

**Debrief**: After all presentations, class votes on which central bank made the most convincing case. Discuss why different banks made different decisions despite facing the "same" global shock.

### Activity B: Country Briefing (20-25 minutes)

**Setup**: Assign each group one country: Japan, US, India, Germany, South Korea.

**Task**: Groups prepare a 5-minute briefing as if advising their country's finance minister:

1. What are the three biggest risks for our country from the current crisis?
2. What policy actions do we recommend?
3. What's the one thing we absolutely must NOT do?

**Debrief**: Compare across groups — where do the recommendations conflict? (e.g., if Japan wants a stronger yen, does that hurt the US? If India keeps buying Russian crude, does that affect US tariff negotiations?)

### Activity C: Causal Chain Mapping (15-20 minutes)

**Setup**: Give each group a large piece of paper or whiteboard space.

**Task**: Map the connections between:

- Iran war / oil prices
- SCOTUS ruling / tariffs / trade diversion
- Central bank decisions / interest rates
- Exchange rates / safe havens (gold, yen, franc)
- Emerging market capital flows
- Stock market crashes

Draw arrows showing causal links. Mark reinforcing loops (spiral arrows) and offsetting effects (dashed arrows).

**Debrief**: Compare maps across groups. Which connections did everyone identify? Which did only one group spot? The most interesting discussions come from connections students identify that weren't in the source material.

### Activity D: Historical Parallel Debate (20 minutes)

**Setup**: Assign each group one historical parallel: 1973, 1979, 1985, 2008, 1930.

**Task**: Groups must argue that their assigned decade is the most instructive parallel for understanding 2026. They must explain:

1. The key similarity
2. The key difference
3. What the historical lesson implies for policy today

**Debrief**: Class votes on which parallel is most instructive. The "right answer" is that 2026 is a combination of all of them — but the debate forces students to prioritize and articulate their reasoning.

## 6. Further Reading

### Key Academic Concepts to Explore

- **Mundell-Fleming model**: Robert Mundell, "Capital Mobility and Stabilization Policy" (1963)
- **Impossible trinity**: Maurice Obstfeld, Jay Shambaugh, Alan Taylor, "The Trilemma in History" (2005)
- **Dornbusch overshooting model**: Explains why exchange rates overshoot their long-run values
- **Volcker disinflation**: Goodfriend and King, "The Incredible Volcker Disinflation" (2005)
- **Terms of trade**: How commodity price changes affect national income

### Accessible Sources for Following the Story

- **Yale Budget Lab**: Real-time tariff tracking and analysis (budgetlab.yale.edu)
- **CNBC/Bloomberg**: Daily market and central bank coverage
- **Chatham House**: "How will the Iran war affect the global economy?" (ongoing analysis)
- **Dallas Fed**: Lutz Kilian's research on oil shocks and monetary policy
- **IMF Blog**: Updates on global economic impact (imf.org/en/Blogs)
- **Tax Foundation**: Trump Tariffs & Trade War Tracker
