## 1. The 1973 OPEC Embargo

### What Happened

On October 19, 1973, OAPEC (Arab members of OPEC) imposed an oil embargo on nations supporting Israel during the Yom Kippur War. Oil quadrupled from ~$3/barrel to ~$12/barrel. The embargo lasted 6 months (lifted March 1974).

### Similarities to 2026

- Geopolitical conflict involving Israel triggers oil supply disruption
- Supply shock causes simultaneous inflation and economic slowdown
- Central banks initially unsure how to respond
- Global recession followed

### Key Differences

| Factor                   | 1973                                                  | 2026                                                        |
| ------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Nature of disruption** | Deliberate embargo by producers                       | Collateral damage from military conflict                    |
| **Supply removed**       | ~7% of global supply                                  | ~20% (Hormuz carries 20M bbl/day) — **3x larger by volume** |
| **Price impact**         | ~300% increase (quadrupled)                           | ~50-70% (so far)                                            |
| **US oil production**    | Declining; US was a net importer                      | US produces ~13M bbl/day (shale revolution)                 |
| **Tariff overlay**       | None                                                  | ~13% effective rate — highest since 1930s                   |
| **Exchange rate regime** | Bretton Woods had just collapsed (1971)               | Floating rates well-established                             |
| **Strategic reserves**   | Didn't exist (IEA created in response to this crisis) | IEA released record 400M barrels                            |

### The Lesson

The 1973 crisis led to the creation of the IEA, strategic petroleum reserves, and a rethinking of energy security. The 2026 crisis may similarly catalyze structural changes — accelerated renewable energy adoption, rethinking of Hormuz-dependent supply chains, and new energy alliances.

## 2. The 1979 Iranian Revolution / Second Oil Shock

### What Happened

The Iranian Revolution overthrew the Shah in January 1979, disrupting Iranian oil production. Oil rose from ~$15 to ~$40/barrel (~167% increase). Fed Chairman Paul Volcker raised the fed funds rate to 20%, triggering two recessions but breaking inflation.

### Similarities to 2026

- Iran at the center of both crises
- Supply disruption through the same geographical chokepoint
- Central bank faces the "Volcker question": crush inflation now or accommodate?
- The "stagflation" diagnosis applies in both cases

### Key Differences

| Factor                      | 1979                                    | 2026                               |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| **Fed response**            | Volcker raised to 20% (all-time record) | Fed holding at 3.50-3.75%          |
| **Unemployment peak**       | 10.8% (1982)                            | TBD — 92,000 jobs lost in Feb 2026 |
| **Inflation peak**          | 14.8% (March 1980)                      | ~3% and rising                     |
| **Recession**               | Two back-to-back (1980, 1981-82)        | Not yet — risk is elevated         |
| **Duration**                | ~2 years of elevated prices             | Ongoing — duration unknown         |
| **Concurrent tariff shock** | None                                    | Section 122 + Section 301          |

### The Lesson

Volcker proved that credibility matters more than comfort. By accepting severe short-term pain (10.8% unemployment, deep recession), he broke inflation expectations permanently. The question for 2026: will today's central bankers have the same resolve, or will they follow Burns' path of accommodation?

The Dallas Fed's Lutz Kilian (February 2026) argues that the 1970s inflation was caused by **monetary policy errors**, not oil shocks. "The destabilization of inflation was the outcome of a sustained shift in monetary policy." If true, the implication is clear: the Fed's response matters more than the shock itself.

## 3. The 1985 Plaza Accord

### What Happened

Volcker's tight monetary policy + Reagan's fiscal expansion (tax cuts + defense spending) attracted capital inflows and caused the dollar to appreciate 46% from 1980 to 1985. The strong dollar made US exports uncompetitive. The G5 (US, UK, France, Germany, Japan) signed the Plaza Accord on September 22, 1985, coordinating currency market intervention to depreciate the dollar. It worked — the dollar fell 40% over 1985-87.

### Relevance to 2026

The Plaza Accord represents the last time major economies **cooperated** to address currency imbalances. It's relevant because:

- **2026 has the opposite dollar problem**: The dollar is weakening, not strengthening. But the underlying issue — trade imbalances and policy-driven currency distortions — is the same.
- **Coordination seems impossible today**: In 1985, 5 allied nations agreed to intervene jointly. In 2026, the US is in trade wars with most of its allies and China simultaneously. The G7 in 2013 explicitly agreed to refrain from currency intervention — a "sort of anti-Plaza Accord."
- **China is missing**: Any currency agreement today without Chinese participation would be ineffective. China, with its $1.2 trillion trade surplus and managed exchange rate, is central to global imbalances — but unlikely to participate in coordinated depreciation of its currency.

### The Lesson

The Plaza Accord worked because of **trust between allies** and **shared recognition of the problem**. Both are absent in 2026. Multilateral coordination on trade and currency issues has been replaced by unilateral action (tariffs, sanctions, capital controls). This fragmentation makes the 2026 crisis harder to resolve collectively.

**Discussion question**: Jeffrey Frankel (NBER) notes that the kind of deliberate currency depreciation at the heart of the Plaza Accord "would be anathema today." If coordinated currency management is no longer possible, what mechanism — if any — can resolve global trade imbalances?

## 4. The 2008 Oil Price Spike + Financial Crisis

### What Happened

Oil hit $147/barrel in July 2008, contributing to consumer price pressure and economic slowdown. This coincided with — and amplified — the unfolding global financial crisis from the subprime mortgage collapse.

### Similarities to 2026

- Oil price surge contributing to broader economic distress
- Financial market crashes (though 2008 was worse)
- Central banks forced to respond to multiple simultaneous crises
- Global coordination needed but difficult

### Key Differences

| Factor              | 2008                                 | 2026                                 |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------ |
| **Oil cause**       | Demand-driven (not supply shock)     | Supply-driven (Hormuz closure)       |
| **Primary crisis**  | Financial system collapse (subprime) | Geopolitical + trade policy          |
| **Banking system**  | On the verge of collapse             | Healthy (no systemic banking crisis) |
| **Policy response** | Massive rate cuts + QE + bailouts    | Rates constrained by inflation       |
| **Tariff factor**   | None                                 | ~13% effective rate                  |

### The Lesson

In 2008, the oil spike was a secondary factor that amplified the financial crisis. In 2026, the oil shock IS the primary crisis (alongside tariffs). The good news: the banking system is much healthier than 2008 (better capitalized, better regulated). The bad news: central banks have less room to cut rates because inflation is already a concern.

## 5. Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act (1930)

### What Happened

Congress passed the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act in June 1930, raising tariffs on over 20,000 imported goods. The average tariff on all imports peaked at 19.8% (1933). Over 1,000 economists petitioned President Hoover not to sign it. Trading partners retaliated. US imports fell from $4.4 billion (1929) to $1.3 billion (1932) — a 70% collapse.

### Relevance to 2026

| Factor                    | Smoot-Hawley (1930)                  | Trump Tariffs (2026)                                 |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Effective tariff rate** | 19.8% on all imports                 | ~13% (some estimates as high as 23%)                 |
| **Context**               | Great Depression already underway    | Slowing growth + oil shock                           |
| **Retaliation**           | Yes — 25+ countries retaliated       | Yes — China, Canada, EU, others                      |
| **Trade volume impact**   | US imports fell 70%                  | Too early to measure; China's exports to US down 26% |
| **Expert opposition**     | 1,000+ economists petitioned against | Widespread economist criticism                       |

### The Lesson

Smoot-Hawley is the cautionary tale for protectionist trade policy. It didn't cause the Great Depression, but it deepened it by triggering retaliation and collapsing trade volumes. The parallels to 2026 are imperfect — today's tariff rates are lower and the economy is structurally different — but the dynamic of **retaliation begetting retaliation** is remarkably similar.

**The key parallel**: Smoot-Hawley was meant to protect American workers. Instead, it destroyed American export jobs as trading partners retaliated. In 2026, tariffs were meant to reduce the trade deficit. Instead, China's exports surged to new markets while US exports to China fell 26%.

## What's Genuinely New About 2026

### Factors That Make It Worse

1. **Largest supply disruption by volume in history**: ~20M bbl/day through Hormuz vs. ~5M in 1973. Three times larger.
2. **No spare capacity accessible**: Saudi Arabia and UAE, holding most swing capacity, are behind the Hormuz closure.
3. **Simultaneous tariff shock**: No previous oil crisis coincided with a major tariff escalation. The "double supply shock" is unprecedented.
4. **US Strategic Petroleum Reserve at low levels**: Drawn down significantly during 2022, providing less cushion.
5. **Fertilizer disruption**: Up to 40% of global urea shipments stranded — threatening food security in developing countries.

### Factors That Make It Less Severe

1. **US shale revolution**: The US produces ~13M bbl/day — it wasn't a major producer during any previous oil crisis. This provides a partial domestic buffer.
2. **Electric vehicles**: Displacing ~1M bbl/day in China alone, rising ~600K bbl/day per year. Structural demand reduction.
3. **Renewables growth**: IEA projects renewables become the dominant global fuel by 2030. Less structural dependence on oil.
4. **Strategic reserves exist**: IEA coordinated a record 400M barrel release — an institution created because of the 1973 crisis.
5. **Economy is less energy-intensive**: Advanced economies use roughly half as much energy per unit of GDP as in the 1970s.
6. **Central bank credibility**: Decades of established inflation-fighting credibility (thanks to Volcker) provides an anchor — though it's being tested.

### The Truly Novel Element

What makes 2026 genuinely unprecedented is the **combination**: a major oil supply shock + the highest tariff rates since the 1930s + a Supreme Court ruling invalidating the legal basis for trade policy + a multipolar financial system (BRICS) offering alternatives to dollar-based trade. No previous crisis combined geopolitical disruption, constitutional crisis, trade war, and systemic financial architecture challenge simultaneously.

## Summary: Which Historical Parallel Fits Best?

| If you think...                          | The best parallel is...               | Because...                                                                        |
| ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| The central bank response matters most   | **1979 (Volcker)**                    | The question is whether the Fed will fight inflation decisively or accommodate it |
| Trade policy is the key driver           | **1930 (Smoot-Hawley)**               | Retaliation begetting retaliation, collapsing trade volumes                       |
| The oil supply shock dominates           | **1973 (OPEC Embargo)**               | Geopolitical conflict → supply disruption → stagflation                           |
| Coordination failure is the real problem | **1985 (Plaza Accord — its absence)** | The world can no longer coordinate on currency and trade                          |
| Multiple crises compound each other      | **2008**                              | Oil + financial stress + policy uncertainty all at once                           |

The honest answer: 2026 is a bit of all of them. The class debate about which parallel is "most instructive" is itself a valuable exercise in understanding the current moment.
