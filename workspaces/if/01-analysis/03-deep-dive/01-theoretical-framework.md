## Part 1: The Impossible Trinity (Mundell-Fleming Trilemma)

### What It Is

The impossible trinity is one of the most important ideas in international finance. It states that a country **cannot simultaneously have all three** of the following:

1. **Free capital flows** — money can move in and out of the country without restrictions
2. **Fixed exchange rate** — the currency is pegged to another currency or basket
3. **Independent monetary policy** — the central bank can set interest rates based on domestic conditions

A country can pick **any two**, but the third must be sacrificed. This isn't a policy recommendation — it's a logical constraint, like saying you can't be in two places at once.

### Why It Works This Way

Imagine a country that pegs its currency to the dollar and allows free capital flows. If its central bank tries to cut interest rates below US rates, investors will move their money to the US for higher returns. This capital outflow puts downward pressure on the currency, breaking the peg. The central bank must either:

- Raise rates back up (giving up monetary independence), or
- Impose capital controls (giving up free capital flows), or
- Let the currency float (giving up the fixed rate)

There's no fourth option.

### Real-World Analogy

Think of it like a triangle-shaped room where the ceiling is too low — you can stand in any corner, but you can't be in all three at once. Each country has "chosen a corner" based on its priorities:

```
                    Free Capital Flows
                          /\
                         /  \
                        /    \
              US, UK   / Pick \   Hong Kong,
              Japan   /  Two   \  Saudi Arabia
              EU     /          \
                    /____________\
        Independent              Fixed
        Monetary Policy          Exchange Rate
                    \           /
                     \  China  /
                      \       /
                       \_____/
                    Capital Controls
```

### How Major Economies Are Positioned (March 2026)

| Country            | Choice                                              | What they sacrifice                               |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| **United States**  | Free capital flows + Independent monetary policy    | Floating dollar (no fixed rate)                   |
| **Eurozone**       | Free capital flows + Independent monetary policy    | Floating euro                                     |
| **Japan**          | Free capital flows + Independent monetary policy    | Floating yen (but intervenes occasionally)        |
| **United Kingdom** | Free capital flows + Independent monetary policy    | Floating pound                                    |
| **China**          | Independent monetary policy + Managed exchange rate | Capital controls (money can't flow freely in/out) |
| **Hong Kong**      | Free capital flows + Fixed rate (pegged to USD)     | No independent monetary policy (follows the Fed)  |
| **Saudi Arabia**   | Free capital flows + Fixed rate (pegged to USD)     | No independent monetary policy (follows the Fed)  |

### Why This Matters Right Now

The Iran war has created a stress test for every country's trilemma position:

- **Japan** chose free capital flows + monetary independence (floating yen). The yen is now caught between safe-haven buying (pushing it up) and carry-trade selling (pushing it down). The BOJ can set rates freely, but it can't control what happens to the yen — and a weaker yen makes oil imports even more expensive.

- **China** chose monetary independence + managed exchange rate (capital controls). This means the PBOC can cut rates to support growth without worrying about massive capital outflows. But the controls limit Chinese investors' ability to diversify internationally and reduce foreign investment into China.

- **Hong Kong** chose free capital flows + fixed rate (follows the Fed). When the Fed holds rates high to fight inflation, Hong Kong must too — even if the local economy needs lower rates. Hong Kong imports the Fed's policy, for better or worse.

## Part 2: The Oil-Trade-Monetary Policy Triangle (Our Adaptation)

### The 2026 Parallel Trilemma

The Mundell-Fleming trilemma is about structural choices countries make. But the 2026 crisis has created a **parallel trilemma** — a set of three goals that policymakers want simultaneously but cannot all achieve:

```
                    Fight Inflation
                    (from oil + tariffs)
                          /\
                         /  \
                        /    \
                       / Can't \
                      /  have   \
                     /   all 3   \
                    /______________\
         Support                    Maintain
         Economic                   Trade Policy
         Growth                     Goals (tariffs)
```

**Here's why you can't have all three:**

1. **Fight inflation + Support growth**: To fight inflation, raise interest rates. But higher rates slow the economy — making the growth problem worse. To support growth, cut rates. But lower rates let inflation run hotter.

2. **Fight inflation + Maintain tariffs**: Tariffs push up consumer prices (90% of the cost falls on American firms and consumers, per the NY Fed). Removing tariffs would reduce inflation. But the administration wants to keep tariffs for trade policy reasons.

3. **Support growth + Maintain tariffs**: Tariffs raise costs for businesses that import inputs, slowing investment and hiring. They also invite retaliation, which hurts exporters. Removing tariffs would help growth but abandons trade objectives.

**Add the oil shock on top**, and you get a situation where inflation pressure comes from **two independent sources** (oil AND tariffs), making the trilemma even more binding.

### Worked Example: The Fed's Impossible Choice (March 2026)

The Federal Reserve holds rates at 3.50-3.75%. Here's the decision matrix:

| If the Fed...    | Inflation effect                                           | Growth effect                                             | Trade policy effect              |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| **Raises rates** | Helps (cools demand)                                       | Hurts (slows economy further; US lost 92,000 jobs in Feb) | Neutral (doesn't affect tariffs) |
| **Cuts rates**   | Hurts (stimulates demand when prices are already rising)   | Helps (cheaper borrowing supports investment/hiring)      | Neutral                          |
| **Holds steady** | Doesn't help enough (inflation at ~3% and rising from oil) | Doesn't help enough (economy slowing)                     | Neutral                          |

No option solves all three problems. This is the trilemma in action.

Goldman Sachs estimates tariffs have already added 0.5 percentage points to inflation, with another 0.3 expected in the first half of 2026. On top of that, the IMF estimates every 10% rise in oil prices adds 0.4 percentage points to inflation and reduces growth by 0.15-0.2 percentage points. Oil has risen ~50-70%. The combined effect is a **double supply shock** — something the 1970s Fed never had to contend with.

## Part 3: Key Concepts Students Need

### Supply-Side vs. Demand-Side Inflation

**Demand-side inflation** happens when people want to buy more than the economy can produce. The solution is straightforward: raise interest rates to cool demand. Central banks are good at this.

**Supply-side inflation** happens when the cost of producing things goes up — oil prices, tariffs on imported inputs, supply chain disruptions. Raising interest rates doesn't make oil cheaper or remove tariffs. It just slows demand on top of an already-constrained supply. This is why supply-side inflation is so much harder for central banks.

**Analogy**: Demand-side inflation is like a restaurant that's too crowded — you can raise prices to thin the crowd. Supply-side inflation is like the kitchen running out of ingredients — raising prices doesn't bring the ingredients back, it just means fewer people eat.

**The 2026 situation**: Both oil and tariffs are supply-side shocks. Central banks are being asked to use a demand-side tool (interest rates) to fight supply-side problems. It's like being handed a hammer when you need a wrench.

### Stagflation

**Stagflation** = **stag**nant growth + in**flation** happening simultaneously.

In normal times, inflation and unemployment move in opposite directions (the Phillips Curve). When the economy overheats, inflation rises but unemployment falls. When the economy cools, unemployment rises but inflation falls. Policymakers can trade one problem for the other.

Stagflation breaks this trade-off. The economy is weak (stagnant growth, rising unemployment) AND prices are rising (inflation). This means:

- If you fight inflation (raise rates), you make the growth problem worse
- If you fight the recession (cut rates), you make the inflation problem worse
- You're stuck

**How common is it?** Rare. The last serious bout was the 1970s (oil shocks). It's the nightmare scenario for central bankers because their primary tool (interest rates) can't solve both problems.

**2026 indicators**: US growth slowed to 1.4% in Q4 2025. The economy lost 92,000 jobs in February 2026. Meanwhile, core PPI inflation rose 0.8% in a single month, and oil is up 50-70%. Barry Bannister, chief equity strategist at Stifel, raised the probability of 1970s-style stagflation from 20% to 35% (Fortune, March 2026).

### Exchange Rate Pass-Through

**What it means**: How much of an oil price increase (or tariff increase) shows up in domestic consumer prices.

**It varies enormously by country**:

- **Japan**: Very high pass-through. Imports 90% of oil from the Middle East. The weak yen (157/USD) amplifies the effect — a barrel of oil costs more in yen than in dollars. Japan's import price index rose 2.8% year-on-year in February.
- **UK**: High. Imports ~40% of oil and ~60% of natural gas. The Iran war is already adding an estimated extra 1 percentage point to UK inflation. Worst case: inflation could hit 5%.
- **US**: Moderate. The US produces a lot of oil domestically, so it's partially insulated. But gasoline prices still jumped 19% in the past month (to $3.45/gallon national average). The Dallas Fed estimates ~50% pass-through from oil to gasoline prices.
- **Emerging markets**: Research shows commodity shocks have "stronger effects on domestic inflation in developing countries compared to advanced countries" — 1 to 6 percentage points per 100% oil price increase in Central/Eastern Europe.

**Key insight**: The same global oil shock creates very different inflation problems in different countries, which means central banks face very different pressures — explaining the extraordinary divergence in policy responses.

### Terms of Trade Shock

**Terms of trade** = the ratio of export prices to import prices. When oil prices surge:

- **Oil importers** (Japan, South Korea, India, Europe): Terms of trade deteriorate — they must export more to pay for the same amount of imports. This drains national income.
- **Oil exporters** (Saudi Arabia, Norway, Russia): Terms of trade improve — their main export is now worth more.

**Analogy**: Imagine you sell apples and buy oil. If oil prices double but apple prices stay the same, you now need to sell twice as many apples to fill your car. Your "terms of trade" just got worse.

For the US, it's complicated: the US is both a major producer and consumer of oil, so the terms of trade effect is muted. But tariff retaliation worsens US terms of trade through a different channel — other countries' retaliatory tariffs make US exports more expensive abroad.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The impossible trinity** forces every country to choose two of three goals (free capital flows, fixed exchange rate, independent monetary policy). There is no escape — it's a structural constraint.

2. **The 2026 crisis** has created a parallel trilemma: fight inflation vs. support growth vs. maintain tariffs. The oil shock + tariff shock = a "double supply shock" that makes this trilemma even harder.

3. **Supply-side inflation** is the central banker's nightmare because interest rates (their main tool) address demand, not supply. This is why stagflation is so feared.

4. **Different countries** face very different versions of this problem depending on their trilemma position, energy dependence, and trade exposure — which is why central bank policies are diverging so dramatically.
