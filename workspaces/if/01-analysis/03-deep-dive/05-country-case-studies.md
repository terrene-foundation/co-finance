## Case Study 1: Japan — The Most Exposed

### Profile

| Factor            | Detail                                                          |
| ----------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| GDP rank          | #4 globally                                                     |
| Oil dependence    | 90% imported from Middle East; 70% through Hormuz               |
| Trilemma position | Free capital flows + independent monetary policy + floating yen |
| Central bank rate | 0.75% (highest in 30 years; hiking cycle)                       |
| Key vulnerability | Weak yen amplifies dollar-denominated oil costs                 |

### How the Triangle Hits Japan

**Oil arm**: Japan is arguably the most energy-vulnerable major economy. With 90% of oil from the Middle East and 70% transiting the Strait of Hormuz, the shutdown struck at Japan's economic lifeline. The weak yen (~157/USD) makes this worse — a barrel of oil costs more yen than dollars, so Japan's effective oil price increase exceeds the 60% dollar-denominated surge. Japan's import price index rose 2.8% year-on-year even before the worst of the shock.

**Trade arm**: Japan is a Section 301 investigation target. Its export-oriented economy (autos, electronics, machinery) faces both higher input costs from oil AND potential new US tariffs. Japanese manufacturers can't easily absorb these costs — profit margins in Japanese manufacturing are already thin by international standards.

**Monetary policy arm**: The BOJ was trying to normalize after decades of zero/negative rates. Governor Ueda now faces an impossible choice: raise rates to support the yen (reducing import costs) but risk killing domestic demand; or hold/cut to support growth but let the yen weaken further (making oil even more expensive).

**Market reaction**: Nikkei 225 fell 6.45% on March 9, entering correction territory (10%+ down from February record high).

### Key Discussion Question

Japan chose to float the yen and allow free capital flows, sacrificing exchange rate stability. In the current crisis, a stronger yen would dramatically reduce Japan's oil import bill. Should Japan intervene to strengthen the yen? What would the trilemma implications be?

## Case Study 2: United States — The Conflicted Hegemon

### Profile

| Factor            | Detail                                                              |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| GDP rank          | #1 globally                                                         |
| Oil dependence    | World's largest producer (~13M bbl/day) AND consumer                |
| Trilemma position | Free capital flows + independent monetary policy + floating dollar  |
| Central bank rate | 3.50-3.75%                                                          |
| Key vulnerability | Self-imposed tariffs creating inflation; dollar is reserve currency |

### How the Triangle Hits the US

**Oil arm**: The US is partially insulated — it produces enough oil domestically to cover much of its needs. But American consumers still pay world-market prices at the pump (gas up 19% in a month to $3.45/gal). US shale producers benefit from higher prices, but the consumer pain is immediate and politically visible.

**Trade arm**: The US is uniquely in the position of having _imposed_ the tariffs that are contributing to its own inflation. The SCOTUS ruling struck down the IEEPA basis, forcing a scramble through Section 122 and Section 301. The NY Fed finding (90% of costs on domestic firms/consumers) means American businesses and households bear the tariff burden — not foreign exporters. Meanwhile, China's export surge (+21.8%) shows the tariffs aren't achieving their stated goal of reducing the trade deficit.

**Monetary policy arm**: The Fed's dual mandate (price stability + maximum employment) is in direct conflict. Inflation rising from oil + tariffs says "don't cut." 92,000 jobs lost in February says "cut." The Fed is also cleaning up an inflation problem partly created by another branch of government's trade policy.

**The dollar paradox**: Tariffs were supposed to strengthen the dollar (textbook prediction). Instead, the dollar has weakened 9.4% (2025) and continues declining. Retaliation, uncertainty, and fiscal deficits have dominated. A weaker dollar amplifies import costs — the opposite of what tariffs aimed to achieve.

### Key Discussion Question

The US government is simultaneously the cause (tariffs) and the responder (Fed policy) to part of this inflation. Should the Fed consider trade policy effects when setting rates, or should it treat tariffs as "fiscal policy" outside its purview — the way Burns treated oil prices as "outside monetary policy"?

## Case Study 3: India — The Rising Power Navigating Both Sides

### Profile

| Factor            | Detail                                                               |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| GDP rank          | #5 globally (fastest-growing large economy)                          |
| Oil dependence    | ~85% imported; 3rd-largest global importer                           |
| Trilemma position | Managed float + selective capital controls + independent policy      |
| Central bank rate | 5.25% (RBI paused easing)                                            |
| Key vulnerability | Fuel subsidies create fiscal cost; BRICS member but US trade partner |

### How the Triangle Hits India

**Oil arm**: India imports ~85% of its oil, making it extremely vulnerable to the Hormuz disruption. India subsidizes domestic fuel prices, so the fiscal cost of the oil shock falls on the government budget rather than directly on consumers. This cushions the inflation impact but creates a fiscal deficit problem.

**Trade arm**: India is in a unique position — it's negotiating a $500 billion trade framework with the US (reduced reciprocal tariff from 25% to 18%, commitment to buy US energy, aircraft, and tech). The US removed the "Russian crude penalty" tariff, effectively giving India permission to keep buying discounted Russian oil. India is playing both sides — maintaining BRICS membership while deepening US trade ties.

**Monetary policy arm**: The RBI paused its easing cycle at 5.25%, citing "intensifying external headwinds." Higher rates attract foreign portfolio flows and support the rupee — which helps keep oil import costs down. But they also constrain domestic growth. India's BSE SENSEX is the worst-performing major index globally in 2026 (-9% YTD).

**Strategic positioning**: India is using the crisis to strengthen its hand — trading US alignment on energy sourcing for tariff relief, while maintaining options through BRICS. India chairs BRICS in 2026, putting it at the center of the de-dollarization conversation while simultaneously deepening dollar-denominated trade with the US.

### Key Discussion Question

India is managing to sit between the US-led and BRICS-led financial systems. Is this sustainable long-term, or will India eventually have to choose? What does India's position tell us about the future of the multipolar financial system?

## Case Study 4: Germany/Eurozone — The Fiscal Rule-Breaker

### Profile

| Factor            | Detail                                                                           |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| GDP rank          | #3 globally (EU's largest economy)                                               |
| Oil dependence    | Eurozone imports ~60% of energy                                                  |
| Trilemma position | Free capital flows + independent monetary policy (ECB) + floating euro           |
| Central bank rate | 2.00% (ECB)                                                                      |
| Key vulnerability | Energy vulnerability (2022 Russia crisis repeat); broke fiscal rules for defense |

### How the Triangle Hits Germany/Eurozone

**Oil arm**: Europe is reliving its 2022 nightmare. The Russia/Ukraine crisis already demonstrated Europe's energy vulnerability. Now the Iran war is disrupting a different supply route. The euro's 14% appreciation vs. the dollar provides partial insulation — oil costs ~40% more in euro terms vs. ~60% in dollar terms. But Europe's industrial base (especially Germany's manufacturing) is highly energy-intensive.

**Trade arm**: The EU-US trade deal (negotiated in summer 2025, 15% ceiling) was frozen after the IEEPA ruling, then began thawing. The European Parliament is moving toward a vote with sunset and suspension clauses reflecting deep distrust of US trade commitments. EU trade with China is also complicated — EU imports of Chinese goods are surging as trade diverts from the US.

**Monetary policy arm**: The ECB's single mandate (price stability) gives it more political cover than the Fed to focus on inflation. But the ECB faces a divided governing council — some members want to wait and see, others worry about a repeat of the 2022 energy-driven inflation spike.

**The fiscal revolution**: Germany broke its constitutional balanced-budget rule (Schuldenbremse) to fund defense spending at 2.8% of GDP. This is a structural shift in European fiscal architecture — Germany was the EU's fiscal disciplinarian. Goldman Sachs estimates this fiscal expansion contributes nearly half of Germany's 1.1% GDP growth in 2026. But it increases Bund issuance, raising yields and potentially tightening financial conditions for the rest of the eurozone.

### Key Discussion Question

Germany breaking its fiscal rules while the ECB holds rates creates a fiscal/monetary tension. Should the ECB accommodate Germany's defense spending by keeping rates low, or fight inflation regardless? How does one country's fiscal expansion affect monetary policy for the entire 20-member currency union?

## Case Study 5: South Korea — The Canary in the Coal Mine

### Profile

| Factor            | Detail                                                           |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| GDP rank          | #13 globally                                                     |
| Oil dependence    | ~80% from Middle East                                            |
| Trilemma position | Free capital flows + independent monetary policy + floating won  |
| Central bank rate | 2.50%                                                            |
| Key vulnerability | Export-oriented manufacturing economy; extreme energy dependence |

### How the Triangle Hits South Korea

**Oil arm**: South Korea's economy runs on imported energy — ~80% from the Middle East. Its key industries (semiconductors, shipbuilding, automobiles, petrochemicals) are energy-intensive. The oil shock simultaneously raises production costs and threatens export competitiveness.

**Trade arm**: South Korea is a Section 301 investigation target. Its economy is built on exports — Samsung, Hyundai, SK Hynix, POSCO. Higher tariffs on Korean goods entering the US would compound the energy cost increase. South Korea also depends on China as its largest trading partner, adding complexity as US-China trade tensions reshape regional supply chains.

**Monetary policy arm**: The Bank of Korea held rates at 2.50% and is expected to hold through 2026. The won fell to ~1,490/USD on March 10 — the weakest since 2009. A weaker won makes oil imports more expensive (same dynamic as Japan's yen), but the central bank can't easily raise rates without slowing an already struggling economy.

**Market reaction**: The KOSPI crashed 18% in two days (March 3-4) — the worst since the 2008 financial crisis. Circuit breakers were activated. This was the most violent market reaction of any major economy, reflecting South Korea's position at the intersection of all three pressures: energy-dependent, export-dependent, and financially open.

### Why South Korea Was the Canary

South Korea was the hardest-hit market because it sits at the intersection of maximum vulnerability on all three dimensions:

1. **Most energy-dependent** of the major Asian exporters
2. **Most export-dependent** (trade = ~70% of GDP, vs. ~25% for the US)
3. **Most financially open** (free capital flows, floating currency — no capital controls like China)

When the shock hit, foreign investors could — and did — pull money out instantly. The won collapsed, making energy imports more expensive, making the economic outlook worse, triggering more selling. A classic negative feedback loop.

### Key Discussion Question

South Korea chose maximum openness (free capital flows, floating exchange rate). This makes it efficient in good times and catastrophically vulnerable in bad times. Should South Korea consider temporary capital controls during extreme shocks? What would the impossible trinity implications be?
