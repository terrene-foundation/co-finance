## Part 1: The Shock Chain

The Iran war doesn't just "cause oil prices to rise." It triggers a cascade of events, each one flowing into the next. Here is the chain, step by step:

### Step 1: Physical Disruption → Insurance Withdrawal → Shipping Halt

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman — about 21 miles wide at its narrowest. Approximately 20 million barrels of oil per day transit through it, along with significant volumes of LNG, LPG, and containerized cargo. That's roughly 20% of global oil supply.

When US-Israel strikes began on February 28, Iran's Revolutionary Guard deployed mines and anti-ship missiles near the strait. The immediate effect wasn't military — it was **insurance**. Marine insurers (Lloyd's of London and others) withdrew coverage for vessels transiting the strait. Without insurance, shipping companies cannot operate. Tanker traffic dropped from normal levels to near zero within days.

**Key insight for discussion**: The shutdown wasn't caused by physical blockade alone — it was caused by the insurance market. This illustrates how **financial infrastructure** (insurance, letters of credit, clearing systems) can amplify or transmit geopolitical shocks in ways that military action alone cannot.

### Step 2: Spot Oil Prices Surge

With 20 million barrels per day suddenly unavailable:

- **Brent crude**: Surged from ~$71/barrel (Feb 27) to $119.50 intraday (March 9) — a 68% increase
- **WTI crude**: Similar trajectory
- This was the **biggest weekly price surge since crude futures began trading in 1983**

Pre-war, analysts at CSIS expected a 3 million barrel/day global surplus in 2026. Instead, supplies fell approximately 20 million barrels/day short. The IEA coordinated a record release of 400 million barrels from strategic reserves, which cushioned but didn't eliminate the shortfall.

### Step 3: Beyond Oil — Broader Commodity Disruption

The Strait of Hormuz doesn't just carry crude oil:

- **LNG (liquefied natural gas)**: ~20% of global seaborne LNG transits Hormuz. Europe, Japan, and South Korea depend on Gulf LNG.
- **Fertilizer**: Up to 40% of global urea exports (from the Middle East) are stranded. This threatens agricultural input costs worldwide.
- **Jet fuel and diesel**: Refinery disruption in Gulf states compounds the crude shortage.
- **Containerized cargo**: Ships rerouting around Africa add weeks to delivery times and increase freight costs.

### Step 4: Consumer Price Pass-Through

Oil prices flow into consumer prices through multiple channels:

**Direct channel** (fast, weeks):

- Gasoline: US pump prices jumped 19% in one month to $3.45/gallon national average
- Diesel: Trucking costs rise, affecting price of everything that moves by road
- Jet fuel: Airline ticket prices increase

**Indirect channel** (slower, months):

- Plastics, chemicals, pharmaceuticals (petrochemical inputs)
- Food prices (fertilizer costs + transportation costs)
- Manufacturing (energy-intensive industries pass costs through)

**The IMF multiplier**: Every 10% oil price increase that persists for most of a year pushes global inflation up by 0.4 percentage points and reduces worldwide economic output by 0.15-0.2 percentage points (per IMF Managing Director Georgieva, March 6, 2026).

### Worked Example: Calculating the Impact

Oil rose approximately 60% from $71 to ~$114 (average, not peak).

Using the IMF multiplier:

- **Inflation impact**: 60% ÷ 10% × 0.4pp = **+2.4 percentage points** added to global inflation
- **Growth impact**: 60% ÷ 10% × 0.15pp = **-0.9 percentage points** off global GDP growth

If pre-crisis global growth was forecast at 3.3% (IMF projection), the oil shock alone could reduce it to ~2.4%. If pre-crisis US inflation was ~2.4%, the oil shock alone could push it toward ~4.8%.

_Caveat: This is a rough calculation. Actual pass-through varies by country and depends on duration. If oil prices fall back quickly, the impact is smaller._

### Step 5: Central Bank Reaction

The inflation surge forces central banks to reassess:

- **Before the shock**: BOE was expected to cut rates. Fed was on a path toward cuts by mid-2026.
- **After the shock**: BOE rate cut is "off the table" — now 70% chance of a _hike_. Fed is stuck at 3.50-3.75% with no clear direction.

This is where the theoretical framework connects: the oil shock creates supply-side inflation that central banks can't easily address without hurting growth.

## Part 2: Differential Impact by Country

### Energy Importers — The Hardest Hit

| Country               | Oil import dependence                    | Key vulnerability                                                         | Market reaction                         |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| **Japan**             | 90% from Middle East, 70% through Hormuz | World's third-largest oil importer; weak yen amplifies costs              | Nikkei -6.45% (Mar 9)                   |
| **South Korea**       | ~80% from Middle East                    | Highly export-dependent economy; energy = major input cost                | KOSPI -18% in 2 days (worst since 2008) |
| **India**             | ~85% of oil imported                     | Third-largest oil importer globally; subsidizes fuel prices (fiscal cost) | SENSEX -9% YTD                          |
| **Europe (Eurozone)** | ~60% of energy imported                  | Already traumatized by 2022 Russia/Ukraine energy crisis                  | Euro weakened on energy vulnerability   |
| **UK**                | ~40% oil, ~60% gas imported              | Already above-target inflation (3.4%); BOE policy reversal                | Inflation expected to hit 5% worst case |

**Why importers are hit hardest**: When oil prices surge, importers must pay more for the same volume. This drains money out of the domestic economy (current account deterioration), weakens the currency (more domestic money chasing the same foreign-denominated oil), and raises costs for every business and consumer.

### Energy Exporters — Windfall with Risks

| Country                | Situation                                                                                                        |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Saudi Arabia / UAE** | Normally benefit from high oil prices, but Gulf producers are partially cut off by the Hormuz closure themselves |
| **Norway**             | Europe's largest non-Russian gas supplier; benefits from both high prices and increased demand                   |
| **Canada**             | Benefits from higher oil prices; but also hit by US tariffs on Canadian exports                                  |
| **Russia**             | Higher prices help government revenue, but sanctions limit access to payment systems and insurance               |

### The United States — The Complicated Case

The US is unique — it's both the world's largest oil producer AND the world's largest oil consumer.

- **Producer benefit**: US shale producers see higher revenues. Domestic production can partially replace lost Gulf supply.
- **Consumer cost**: American drivers still pay more at the pump (+19% in a month). Industries still face higher input costs.
- **Net effect**: Roughly neutral on the current account, but consumers feel the pain directly. Plus, the US faces the _additional_ inflation from its own tariffs — the "double supply shock."

## Part 3: Financial Market Channels

### Equity Markets: Why Asia Crashed Hardest

The KOSPI's 18% two-day crash wasn't random. South Korea's economy is:

- **Energy-import dependent**: ~80% from Middle East
- **Export-oriented**: Manufacturing (semiconductors, autos, ships) requires cheap energy
- **Globally integrated**: Capital flows in and out freely (trilemma: they chose free capital flows + monetary independence)

When the oil shock hit, foreign investors simultaneously:

1. Priced in higher costs for Korean manufacturers
2. Sold Korean equities to flee to safe havens
3. Moved money to dollar-denominated assets

The combination created a selling cascade that triggered circuit breakers — automatic trading halts designed to prevent panic but which can also signal panic.

### Bond Markets: Flight to Quality vs. Inflation Repricing

Two opposing forces in bond markets:

- **Flight to quality**: Investors buy safe government bonds (US Treasuries, German Bunds) → pushes yields down
- **Inflation repricing**: If inflation rises, bonds lose value → pushes yields up

In 2026, these forces are roughly offsetting in the US, creating volatile but directionless Treasury yields. In Europe, the inflation repricing is winning — Bund yields are rising as investors price in the possibility of ECB hikes.

### Currency Markets: Safe-Haven Flows

- **Gold**: Surged past $5,000/oz — the ultimate safe haven, no counterparty risk
- **Swiss franc**: Gained against all major currencies; SNB rate at 0.00%, inflation only 0.3%
- **Japanese yen**: Complicated — receiving safe-haven bids but also weakened by carry trade unwinding and energy import costs
- **US dollar**: Short-term safe-haven support, but longer-term structural weakness (tariff retaliation, fiscal deficits)

## Part 4: How Big Is This Shock? Comparison Table

| Crisis                      | Oil price increase     | Supply disrupted           | Duration  | GDP impact                  |
| --------------------------- | ---------------------- | -------------------------- | --------- | --------------------------- |
| **1973 OPEC Embargo**       | ~300% ($3→$12)         | ~7% of global supply       | 6 months  | US recession 1973-75        |
| **1979 Iranian Revolution** | ~167% ($15→$40)        | Iranian output collapsed   | ~2 years  | US recession 1980 + 1981-82 |
| **1990 Gulf War**           | ~135% ($17→$40)        | Iraq + Kuwait offline      | ~6 months | Mild US recession 1990-91   |
| **2008 Commodity Spike**    | ~145% ($60→$147)       | Demand-driven (not supply) | ~1 year   | Global financial crisis     |
| **2026 Iran War**           | ~50-70% ($71→$110-120) | ~20% of global supply      | Ongoing   | TBD                         |

**Key differences**: The 2026 disruption is the **largest by volume** (~20 million bbl/day via Hormuz vs. ~5 million in 1973). But the percentage price increase is smaller because:

1. US shale revolution provides alternative supply (US produces ~13 million bbl/day)
2. Electric vehicles displace ~1 million bbl/day in China alone
3. Strategic reserve releases (IEA coordinated 400 million barrels)
4. The global economy is less energy-intensive than in the 1970s

## Key Takeaways

1. The shock chain runs: **Physical disruption → Insurance withdrawal → Shipping halt → Oil price surge → Broader commodity disruption → Consumer price pass-through → Central bank bind**

2. **Insurance markets** amplified the physical disruption — a key example of financial infrastructure transmitting geopolitical risk

3. **Energy importers** (Japan, South Korea, India, UK) are hit far harder than exporters or mixed economies (US)

4. **The IMF multiplier** (0.4pp inflation / 0.15-0.2pp GDP per 10% oil rise) provides a rough but useful framework for estimating impact

5. By volume, this is the **largest oil supply disruption in history**, but structural changes (US shale, EVs, reserves) are cushioning the price impact compared to the 1970s
