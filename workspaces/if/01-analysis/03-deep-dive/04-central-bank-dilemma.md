## Part 1: Why This Is Every Central Banker's Nightmare

### The Normal Playbook (Demand-Side Inflation)

When inflation comes from excess demand — consumers spending too much, economy overheating — central banks have a clear solution:

1. Raise interest rates
2. Borrowing becomes more expensive → people spend less → businesses invest less
3. Demand cools → prices stop rising
4. Once inflation is under control, gradually lower rates

This works because the central bank is addressing the **cause** of inflation (excess demand) with a tool that **directly reduces** demand. It's painful (slower growth, some job losses) but effective.

### The 2026 Problem (Supply-Side Inflation)

When inflation comes from higher input costs — oil prices, tariffs — raising interest rates doesn't solve the problem:

- Higher rates don't make oil cheaper
- Higher rates don't remove tariffs
- Higher rates don't reopen the Strait of Hormuz
- Higher rates DO slow the economy, adding a growth hit ON TOP of the inflation

**Analogy**: Demand-side inflation is like a fever caused by an infection — antibiotics (rate hikes) treat the cause. Supply-side inflation is like a fever caused by sunburn — antibiotics won't help, and the additional side effects make you feel worse.

The result is **stagflation**: the economy stagnates AND inflation persists. The central bank's only tool (interest rates) can't fix the cause, and using it aggressively creates a recession without necessarily solving inflation.

### The 2026 Data That Scares Central Bankers

| Indicator                | Reading              | Direction           | Signal                 |
| ------------------------ | -------------------- | ------------------- | ---------------------- |
| US GDP growth (Q4 2025)  | 1.4%                 | Slowing             | Recession risk         |
| US job losses (Feb 2026) | -92,000              | Worsening           | Labor market weakening |
| US core PPI (Feb 2026)   | +0.8% monthly        | Accelerating        | Producer costs surging |
| Oil (Brent)              | ~$114 (avg)          | +60% from pre-war   | Supply shock           |
| US effective tariff rate | ~13%                 | Highest since 1930s | Second supply shock    |
| Gas prices               | $3.45/gal (+19% MoM) | Rising              | Consumer squeeze       |

Growth is slowing AND inflation is accelerating. This is the textbook definition of stagflation.

## Part 2: Five Central Banks, Five Different Traps

### The Federal Reserve — Paralyzed

**Rate**: 3.50-3.75% (held since January 2026)
**Dual mandate**: Price stability AND maximum employment

The Fed is uniquely trapped because it has **two mandates** pulling in opposite directions:

- **Price stability** says: Don't cut rates; inflation is rising (oil + tariffs)
- **Maximum employment** says: Cut rates; 92,000 jobs lost in February

What the market expects: 45% chance of a cut by April. But the Dallas Fed's senior adviser Lutz Kilian warned that cutting too soon risks repeating the errors of the 1970s, when the Fed's "stop-go" policy worsened inflation. Markets are starting to price in a 33% chance of rate _hikes_ before year-end.

**The Fed's additional complication**: The US government itself imposed the tariffs causing part of the inflation. The Fed is being asked to clean up a problem created by another branch of government's policy — while the Supreme Court has just invalidated the legal basis for those policies.

### The European Central Bank — Energy Vulnerable

**Rate**: 2.00% (held since February 2026)
**Mandate**: Price stability (single mandate, unlike the Fed's dual mandate)

The ECB was on a rate-cutting path before the Iran war. Now it faces:

- **Energy vulnerability**: Europe imports ~60% of its energy. The 2022 Russia/Ukraine crisis proved how exposed Europe is to energy supply disruptions. This is déjà vu.
- **Euro appreciation**: The euro is up 14% vs. the dollar over 12 months. This helps by making dollar-denominated oil cheaper in euro terms (~40% net oil price impact vs. ~60% in dollar terms). But it hurts European exporters.
- **Inflation repricing**: Eurozone interest rate markets now price a 33% probability of a 25bp rate hike by December.

ECB council member Pierre Wunsch said the ECB would "avoid reacting hastily to any movements in energy prices" — but Goldman Sachs warned that in a worst-case scenario, eurozone inflation could rise 3.6 percentage points by year-end, which would force the ECB to hike.

**The ECB's advantage**: With a single mandate (price stability only, not employment), it has political cover to prioritize fighting inflation even if growth suffers. The Fed doesn't have this luxury.

### The Bank of Japan — The Most Constrained

**Rate**: 0.75% (highest in 30 years; BOJ is in a hiking cycle)
**Key constraint**: Japan imports 90% of oil from the Middle East, 70% through Hormuz

The BOJ faces perhaps the most difficult position of any major central bank:

1. **It was hiking rates** — trying to normalize after decades of zero/negative rates. The oil shock threatens to derail this normalization.
2. **The yen is caught in a tug-of-war**:
   - Safe-haven demand pushes the yen UP (good for import costs)
   - Interest rate differential with the US pushes the yen DOWN (carry trade: borrow in low-rate yen, invest in high-rate dollars)
   - Net effect: yen at ~157/USD, weak enough to amplify oil import costs
3. **Japan's import price index** rose 2.8% year-on-year in February — before the worst of the oil shock hit

Governor Ueda stated he will "closely watch the impact of Middle East developments" — diplomatic language for "we have no idea what to do."

**The yen carry trade dynamic**: Because Japan's rate (0.75%) is so much lower than the US (3.50-3.75%), investors borrow in yen and invest in dollars. This pushes the yen down. If the BOJ raises rates to support the yen (and reduce import costs), it risks slowing the domestic economy. If it holds, the yen stays weak and oil imports stay expensive.

### The Bank of England — The Dramatic Reversal

**Rate**: 3.75% (held in February; 5-4 vote — five members voted to hold, four voted to cut)
**Key constraint**: UK imports ~40% of oil and ~60% of natural gas

The BOE's situation illustrates how fast the calculus can change:

- **Before the Iran war**: A March rate cut was "widely predicted." Markets expected gradual easing.
- **After the Iran war**: The cut is "off the table." Investors now price a 70% chance the BOE will _raise_ rates before year-end — a complete reversal.
- **The numbers**: The Iran war is already adding an estimated extra 1 percentage point to UK inflation. Bloomberg reported inflation could rebound to 5% (from 3.4%). The BOE's target is 2%.

The UK is particularly exposed because:

1. It imports a large share of energy (unlike the US, which produces a lot domestically)
2. UK inflation was already above target (3.4%) before the oil shock
3. The February rate vote was 5-4 (five to hold, four to cut) — the committee was already divided

**Discussion question**: The BOE was one vote away from cutting rates when the Iran war started. If they had cut, they'd now face even worse inflation with a lower starting rate. Does this suggest central banks should always maintain a "cushion" of higher rates for unexpected events?

### The People's Bank of China — Different Rules Entirely

**Rate**: Low (moderately loose stance)
**Key constraint**: Capital controls give the PBOC policy freedom that others don't have

China occupies a different corner of the impossible trinity — it has capital controls, which means it can:

- Cut rates to support growth without worrying about massive capital outflows
- Manage the yuan's exchange rate without market forces overwhelming the peg
- Shield the domestic economy from some external financial market volatility

The PBOC's recent actions:

- Cut the FX risk reserve ratio to 0% (March 2) — removing a tool that discouraged yuan depreciation
- Governor Pan Gongsheng committed to "flexibly and efficiently use RRR cuts and interest rate reductions"
- Managing volatile daily yuan fixings to absorb Iran war fallout

**China's advantage**: Because capital can't flow freely in and out, the PBOC has genuine monetary independence even during a global crisis. The cost is reduced financial integration and foreign investment.

**Discussion question**: China's capital controls give it policy flexibility that the US, UK, and Japan don't have. Is this a good trade-off? What are the long-term costs of restricting capital flows?

## Part 3: Volcker vs. Burns — The Historical Parallel

### The Burns Era (1970-1978): Accommodation

Arthur Burns led the Fed during the first oil shock (1973-74). His approach:

1. Initially raised rates (from 5.75% in 1972 to 12% by July 1974)
2. When the economy weakened, reversed course and cut rates
3. This "stop-go" policy meant inflation was never fully defeated
4. Burns believed cost-push inflation (from oil) was "outside the influence of monetary policy"

**The result**: Inflation came down temporarily but never returned to pre-1973 levels. By 1978, it was rising again. Burns himself later admitted: "I have reluctantly come to believe that fairly drastic therapy will be needed to turn the inflationary psychology around."

**The key error**: By not staying the course, Burns allowed inflation **expectations** to become embedded. Workers demanded higher wages to keep up with inflation, businesses raised prices to cover higher wages, and the cycle fed on itself. This is what economists call a "wage-price spiral."

### The Volcker Era (1979-1982): Confrontation

Paul Volcker took over in August 1979. His approach was the opposite of Burns':

1. Raised the fed funds rate to **20%** (June 1981) — the all-time record
2. Triggered two back-to-back recessions:
   - January-July 1980 (6 months)
   - July 1981-November 1982 (16 months)
3. Unemployment peaked at **10.8%** in late 1982
4. But inflation fell from **14.8%** (March 1980) to below **3%** by early 1983

**The key lesson**: Volcker accepted severe short-term pain to break inflation expectations permanently. His commitment established the Fed's credibility as an inflation fighter for decades. Once markets believed the Fed would do "whatever it takes," inflation expectations re-anchored and the wage-price spiral broke.

### The 2026 Question: Who Will Today's Central Bankers Be?

The Dallas Fed's Lutz Kilian published a paper in February 2026 arguing that "the destabilization of inflation in the 1970s was not the result of a single adverse shock, but the outcome of a sustained shift in monetary policy." In other words, the oil shock didn't cause the 1970s inflation — the Fed's failure to respond decisively did.

**If Kilian is right, the implication for today**: The Fed should hold firm or even raise rates, even if the economy weakens. Cutting rates to support growth would repeat Burns' error.

**The counter-argument**: Today is different because:

1. The US is now a major oil producer (partially insulated)
2. The economy is less energy-intensive than the 1970s
3. Central banks have more credibility and better tools
4. But — the **tariff shock** is something the 1970s Fed never faced. It's a novel complication.

Barry Bannister, chief equity strategist at Stifel, raised the probability of 1970s-style stagflation from 20% to 35% (Fortune, March 2026). The debate is no longer academic — it's the defining question for monetary policy in 2026.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Supply-side inflation** (from oil and tariffs) is the central banker's nightmare because interest rates address demand, not supply. Raising rates doesn't make oil cheaper — it just slows the economy on top of the inflation.

2. **Five central banks face five different versions** of the same problem, shaped by energy dependence, trilemma position, and institutional mandates.

3. **The Volcker vs. Burns debate** is the defining historical parallel: accommodate inflation and risk it becoming embedded, or fight it aggressively and accept a recession.

4. **China's capital controls** give the PBOC a degree of policy freedom that other central banks don't have — illustrating the real-world trade-offs of the impossible trinity.

5. **The BOE's dramatic reversal** (from expected cut to 70% chance of hike) shows how a single geopolitical event can completely overturn monetary policy expectations.
