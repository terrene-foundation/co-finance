## Reinforcing vs. Offsetting Effects

### Reinforcing Effects (Making Things Worse)

1. **Oil inflation + Tariff inflation → Double consumer hit**
   A product that crosses borders AND requires oil is hit three times: energy input + tariff + energy transport. Consumers pay for all three.

2. **Dollar weakness + Dollar-denominated oil → Amplified import costs**
   Oil is priced in dollars. A weaker dollar means more domestic currency per barrel for everyone. For the US, oil is up 60%; for Japan (weak yen), the effective increase is even worse; for Europe (strong euro), it's partially cushioned.

3. **Market selloff → Capital flight → Currency weakness → Higher import costs → More selling**
   South Korea's negative feedback loop: KOSPI crashed → foreign investors pulled money → won weakened → oil imports got more expensive → economic outlook worsened → more selling. Circuit breakers had to intervene.

4. **Uncertainty → Inventory hoarding → Short-term demand surge → More inflation**
   Businesses racing to import before tariff deadlines creates a demand spike that ironically adds to inflationary pressure. The uncertainty itself causes the inflation it's trying to protect against.

5. **BRICS de-dollarization + Dollar weakness → Reduced dollar demand → More dollar weakness**
   BRICS conducting 90% of settlements in local currencies reduces structural demand for dollars, contributing to dollar weakness, which encourages further de-dollarization. A self-reinforcing cycle.

### Offsetting Effects (Providing Some Relief)

1. **Dollar weakness → US export competitiveness improves**
   A weaker dollar makes American goods cheaper abroad. But tariff retaliation partially offsets this benefit — US exports to China fell 26%.

2. **Euro appreciation → Cheaper oil in euro terms**
   The euro's 14% rise vs. the dollar means Europe's effective oil price increase is ~40%, not ~60%. The exchange rate acts as a partial shock absorber.

3. **US shale production → Domestic supply buffer**
   The US produces ~13M bbl/day domestically. In 1973, the US was a declining producer — now it can partially offset the Hormuz disruption with domestic output.

4. **IEA strategic reserve release (400M barrels) → Temporary supply cushion**
   Created after the 1973 crisis specifically for moments like this. Provides months (not years) of buffer.

5. **Electric vehicles → Structural demand reduction**
   EVs displace ~1M bbl/day in China alone, growing ~600K bbl/day per year. Each year, the world becomes slightly less vulnerable to oil shocks.

## Second-Order Effects Students Might Miss

### 1. Insurance Market Stress

The Strait of Hormuz didn't shut down primarily because of mines or missiles — it shut down because **insurance companies withdrew coverage**. Without maritime insurance, ships can't sail, ports won't accept them, and financing collapses. The same insurance markets are also stressed by tariff-related trade credit uncertainty. If insurers simultaneously face marine, credit, and political risk claims, the entire trade finance infrastructure is under strain.

**Why it matters**: Financial infrastructure (insurance, clearing, payments) is the invisible plumbing of global trade. When it breaks, trade stops — even if the physical routes are open.

### 2. Fiscal Pressure from Multiple Directions

Governments are being squeezed from every side:

- **Higher defense spending** (Germany: 2.8% of GDP; US: ongoing military operations)
- **Energy subsidies** (India subsidizes fuel; many governments will face pressure to cushion consumers)
- **Uncertain tariff revenue** (IEEPA refunds of $142-175B; Section 122 revenue limited and temporary)
- **Lower growth** = lower tax revenue
- **Higher interest costs** on record sovereign debt ($61T OECD)

This fiscal squeeze is happening at the exact moment when governments need maximum fiscal flexibility to respond to the crisis.

### 3. Trade Finance Freezing

Letters of credit, customs bonds, and trade insurance all require predictable tariff rates. With three legal frameworks in three weeks:

- Banks can't price trade credit accurately
- Importers can't calculate landed costs
- Exporters can't guarantee delivery prices
- The result: some trade simply doesn't happen, not because it's uneconomic but because it's **unfinanceable**

This is invisible in market data but real in the physical economy.

### 4. Fertilizer and Food Security

Up to 40% of global urea (fertilizer) exports pass through the Strait of Hormuz. With shipments stranded:

- Agricultural input costs rise globally
- The impact is delayed (by one planting season) but potentially severe
- Developing countries — especially in Sub-Saharan Africa (40% already in debt distress) — are most vulnerable
- Chatham House warned this could "destabilize fragile states such as Pakistan"

The food price impact won't show up for months but could be the most devastating long-term consequence.

### 5. The "Super Week" Feedback Loop (March 16-19)

Seven major central banks decide rates in three days. Each decision affects the others:

- If the Fed holds → dollar stable → less pressure on other currencies
- If the Fed signals future cuts → dollar weakens → yen/euro strengthen → reduces oil import costs for Japan/Europe
- If the BOE hikes → pound strengthens → but UK growth slows → UK stocks fall
- If the BOJ holds → yen stays weak → Japanese import costs stay high → Nikkei under pressure

Each central bank is making its decision with one eye on what the others will do — a game theory problem in real time.

## The Big Question for Discussion

**"If you could change ONE variable in this system, which would have the biggest positive cascading effect?"**

| Option                                                  | What it would fix                                                                                     | What it wouldn't fix                                             |
| ------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **End the Iran war**                                    | Oil prices drop → inflation eases → central banks can cut → markets recover → EM capital flows resume | Tariff uncertainty, legal chaos, trade diversion, sovereign debt |
| **Remove all tariffs**                                  | Consumer prices drop → second inflation source eliminated → dollar may stabilize                      | Oil shock, Hormuz, energy security, trade balance goals          |
| **Coordinated central bank action** (like Plaza Accord) | Reduce currency volatility → stabilize capital flows → provide clarity                                | Doesn't fix the physical oil disruption or tariff policy         |
| **Massive strategic reserve release**                   | Temporary oil price relief → inflation pressure eases                                                 | Depletes reserves; doesn't fix the underlying disruption         |
| **Accelerate renewable energy transition**              | Long-term: reduces oil dependence. Short-term: nothing                                                | Takes years/decades to materially affect vulnerability           |

**The uncomfortable answer**: No single variable change solves the whole problem. The 2026 crisis is a **system failure** — multiple interconnected forces that require multiple coordinated responses. But coordination is harder than ever in a fragmented, multipolar world.

This is what makes the current moment so challenging — and so instructive for studying international finance.
