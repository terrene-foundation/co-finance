## Part 1: Tariffs as a Second Inflation Source

### The Double Supply Shock

The US economy is being hit by two supply-side inflation sources simultaneously:

**Source 1 — Oil shock (Iran war)**:

- Oil up ~60%, adding an estimated +2.4 percentage points to inflation (IMF multiplier)
- Affects energy, transportation, manufacturing, food (via fertilizer)

**Source 2 — Tariffs (IEEPA ruling aftermath)**:

- US effective tariff rate now ~13% trade-weighted — the highest since the 1930s-1940s
- Goldman Sachs estimates tariffs added 0.5 percentage points to inflation in 2025, with another 0.3 expected in early 2026
- The NY Fed found nearly 90% of tariff costs fall on American firms and consumers, not foreign exporters

**The compounding effect**: Consider a manufactured good that:

1. Requires oil to produce (higher energy costs from the Iran shock)
2. Is imported into the US (subject to tariffs)
3. Is shipped by truck to retail (higher diesel costs from the Iran shock)

This product is hit **three times** — energy input costs, tariff costs, and transportation costs. The consumer pays for all three.

### Who Pays for Tariffs?

The textbook prediction: Tariff costs are split between domestic consumers (higher prices) and foreign exporters (lower margins). The split depends on relative bargaining power and substitutability.

The reality in 2026: **American businesses and consumers bear almost all of it.**

- In 2025, businesses absorbed ~80% of tariff costs by drawing down pre-tariff inventory. Prices didn't rise immediately because companies had stockpiles bought before tariffs hit.
- By 2026, those stockpiles are depleted. Goldman Sachs estimates the consumer share of tariff costs has risen from 20% to 55%, and may reach 70% as contracts renegotiate.
- Harvard economist Jeffrey Frankel warned the tariffs' impact was "merely delayed — and could materialize with full force in the coming year."

### The Consumer Squeeze

An American household now faces:

| Cost pressure                     | Source                                         | Approximate impact                   |
| --------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Higher gas prices                 | Oil shock                                      | +19% at the pump ($3.45/gal average) |
| Higher goods prices               | Tariffs (13% effective rate)                   | Varies by product category           |
| Higher food prices                | Oil (fertilizer/transport) + some food tariffs | Rising                               |
| Potentially higher interest rates | Central bank response to inflation             | TBD                                  |

Oil price spikes are redirecting consumer cash toward energy costs, "essentially voiding out" the economic boost expected from tax refunds and fiscal stimulus (CNBC analysis, March 10).

## Part 2: The Legal Uncertainty Multiplier

### Three Legal Frameworks in Three Weeks

The rapid legal pivoting is itself a source of economic damage:

| Date       | Legal basis                         | Rate                                    | Duration                           | Scope            |
| ---------- | ----------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ---------------- |
| Pre-Feb 20 | IEEPA                               | Varied by country (up to 145% on China) | Unlimited                          | Country-specific |
| Feb 20     | **STRUCK DOWN** by Supreme Court    | —                                       | —                                  | —                |
| Feb 24     | Section 122                         | 10-15%                                  | **150 days max** (expires July 24) | All countries    |
| Mar 11     | Section 301 investigations launched | TBD                                     | Months to complete                 | 16 countries     |

**Why uncertainty itself is damaging**:

1. **Business investment freezes**: If you don't know what tariff rate will apply to your imports next month, you can't plan production, pricing, or supply chains. Many businesses simply stop investing until clarity emerges.

2. **Trade finance disruption**: Letters of credit, trade insurance, and customs bonds all require known tariff rates to price accurately. Uncertain rates mean higher costs or unavailable financing.

3. **Inventory hoarding**: Businesses rush to import before potential tariff increases, which ironically adds to inflationary pressure in the short term.

4. **The 150-day cliff**: Section 122 authority expires July 24, 2026. The administration must either get Congress to act, complete the Section 301 investigations, or face a sudden tariff removal — any of which creates a major policy discontinuity.

**Analogy**: Imagine a landlord who changes your rent three times in three weeks, each time under a different lease, and tells you the current lease expires in 5 months. Even if today's rent is manageable, the uncertainty about tomorrow's rent makes it impossible to plan your finances.

## Part 3: Trade Diversion Under Dual Pressure

### China's Export Surge Proves Tariffs Reroute, Not Destroy, Trade

China's January-February 2026 data:

- Exports up 21.8% year-on-year (triple expectations)
- Trade surplus: $213.62 billion for the period (record)
- Export destinations: ASEAN +29.4%, Europe +27.8%, South Korea +27%
- US exports to China: Down 26%

**What happened**: US tariffs (~30% effective rate on Chinese goods) didn't reduce Chinese production. Chinese manufacturers found new customers in Southeast Asia, Europe, and elsewhere. This is a textbook case of **trade diversion** — trade doesn't disappear, it reroutes.

**But the oil shock complicates the rerouting**: Many of these diverted goods now travel through supply chains that depend on the Strait of Hormuz for energy. ASEAN economies importing more Chinese goods are also energy-import dependent. They face:

- Higher costs for the Chinese goods (global energy costs are up)
- Higher domestic energy costs (same oil shock)
- Competitive pressure from the surge of Chinese exports

**Double jeopardy for ASEAN**: These countries are absorbing diverted Chinese goods AND facing an energy price shock — both at once.

### Implications for US Trade Deficit Goals

The stated purpose of US tariffs is to reduce the trade deficit. But:

1. Chinese goods reach American consumers anyway — through ASEAN intermediaries, relabeled and re-exported
2. US exports to China have dropped 26% due to retaliation — worsening the bilateral deficit
3. The dollar has weakened (see Part 4), which should help exports — but tariff retaliation offsets this

Net result: The trade deficit hasn't meaningfully improved despite the highest tariff rates since the 1930s.

## Part 4: The Dollar Paradox

### Textbook Prediction vs. Reality

**What trade theory predicts**: Tariffs reduce imports → less demand for foreign currency → domestic currency strengthens.

**What actually happened**: The Dollar Index (DXY) fell 9.4% in 2025 and continued declining in early 2026, trading in the high-90s near multi-month lows.

### Why the Dollar Weakened

Several forces are overriding the textbook prediction:

1. **Retaliation**: Other countries imposed retaliatory tariffs on US exports, reducing demand for dollars through the export channel. The retaliation effect offset or exceeded the import-reduction effect.

2. **Uncertainty premium**: Investors demand a discount for holding assets in a country with unpredictable trade policy. Three legal frameworks in three weeks is not confidence-inspiring.

3. **Fiscal deficits**: The US is running large fiscal deficits while tariff revenue is uncertain (IEEPA refunds of $142-175 billion may be required). This increases Treasury supply, putting downward pressure on the dollar.

4. **De-dollarization trend**: BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa — an economic bloc expanded in 2024 to include several additional members) nations conducting 90% of settlements in local currencies reduces structural demand for dollars.

5. **Fed rate expectations**: Markets anticipate rate cuts (eventually), reducing the interest rate differential that attracts capital to dollar assets.

### The Exchange Rate Pass-Through Feedback Loop

Here's where it gets interesting: a weaker dollar makes imports more expensive in dollar terms (the same tariffed good costs more dollars to buy). This means the dollar's decline is **amplifying** the tariff effect rather than offsetting it.

For oil specifically: Oil is priced in dollars. A weaker dollar means:

- For Americans: Oil is up ~60% in dollar terms (bad)
- For Europeans: Oil is up ~60% in dollars, but the euro appreciated ~14% vs. USD, so the net impact in euros is closer to ~40% (less bad)
- For Japanese: Oil is up ~60% in dollars, AND the yen has weakened vs. USD, so the net impact in yen is even worse than 60% (much worse)

**Discussion question**: The Brookings Institution argues that "tariffs hurt the dollar" because they invite retaliation, reduce growth expectations, and undermine confidence. If this is true, tariffs are self-defeating as a trade balance tool — they weaken the currency, making imports more expensive in exactly the way the tariff was supposed to discourage. What does this imply for trade policy?

## Key Takeaways

1. The US faces a **double supply shock** — oil inflation AND tariff inflation hitting consumers simultaneously. Goldman Sachs estimates a combined 3+ percentage point impact on inflation.

2. **Legal uncertainty** is itself economically damaging — three tariff frameworks in three weeks freezes business investment and disrupts trade finance.

3. **Trade diversion** is real — China's record surplus proves tariffs reroute trade rather than destroying it. But the oil shock complicates the rerouted supply chains.

4. **The dollar paradox**: Tariffs should strengthen the currency (textbook prediction) but have weakened it (reality) because retaliation, uncertainty, and fiscal deficits dominate.

5. The oil shock and tariffs **compound each other**: goods that cross borders AND require oil to produce are hit three times (energy input + tariff + energy transport).
