# Todo: Class Discussion Guide — Structured Questions, Debates, and Activities

## Status: pending

## Description

Create a ready-to-use discussion guide for the instructor with structured questions at different difficulty levels, debate prompts, and group activity ideas. This is the final deliverable that ties everything together and makes it usable in a classroom setting.

## Deliverable

A document in `workspaces/if/01-analysis/03-deep-dive/07-discussion-guide.md` covering:

1. **Opening Questions** (warm-up, accessible to all students)
   - "If you were running a company that imports goods from Asia, what would keep you up at night this week?"
   - "Oil prices jumped 35% in a week. Who wins and who loses?"
   - "The Supreme Court just told the President he can't impose tariffs the way he has been. What happens next?"

2. **Conceptual Questions** (connecting events to theory)
   - "Using the Mundell-Fleming model, explain why Japan's central bank faces a different dilemma than the Fed"
   - "Tariff theory predicts that tariffs strengthen the imposing country's currency. The dollar has weakened. What's wrong with the theory — or with the tariffs?"
   - "The Bank of England was expected to cut rates. Now there's a 70% chance they'll raise. What changed — and what does this tell us about how central banks actually make decisions?"
   - "China's exports grew 21.8% despite US tariffs of ~30%. Does this mean tariffs don't work?"

3. **Analytical Questions** (requiring synthesis across multiple events)
   - "Trace the causal chain: Iran war → oil price → central bank response → exchange rate → emerging market capital flows. At each step, identify the key assumptions"
   - "Compare Japan and the US in this crisis. Both face inflation pressure. Why are their policy options so different?"
   - "Germany just broke its balanced-budget rule for defense spending. How does this interact with the ECB's monetary policy dilemma?"

4. **Debate Prompts** (for structured class debates)
   - "Resolved: The Fed should raise interest rates despite the economic slowdown, because failing to fight inflation now will make it worse later" (Volcker vs. Burns debate)
   - "Resolved: Unilateral tariffs are an ineffective tool for reducing trade deficits, as demonstrated by China's export surge"
   - "Resolved: The Strait of Hormuz crisis proves that energy security is more important than free trade"

5. **Group Activity Ideas**
   - **Role-play: Central Bank Committee** — Assign students to play the Fed, ECB, BOJ, BOE, and RBI. Each group presents their rate decision with justification. Class votes on who made the most convincing case.
   - **Country Analysis** — Assign each group a country (Japan, US, India, Germany, South Korea). Groups prepare a 5-minute briefing on how the triangle affects their country and what they'd recommend to the finance minister.
   - **Causal Chain Mapping** — Give groups a large whiteboard/paper. Map the connections between oil, tariffs, monetary policy, exchange rates, and capital flows. Compare maps across groups.
   - **Historical Parallel Debate** — Is 2026 more like 1973, 1979, or 2008? Groups defend their assigned decade.

6. **Further Reading Suggestions**
   - Key academic concepts: Mundell-Fleming, impossible trinity, Dornbusch overshooting, terms of trade
   - Accessible sources for following the story (specific outlets, columns, data dashboards)

- At least 2 structured debate prompts with clear "for" and "against" positions
- At least 2 group activities that could run in a 50-75 minute class session
