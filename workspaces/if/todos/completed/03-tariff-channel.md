## Description

Explain how the tariff upheaval (IEEPA ruling → Section 122 → Section 301) interacts with and amplifies the oil shock. This is the second arm of the triangle — trade costs layered on top of energy costs.

## Deliverable

A document in `workspaces/if/01-analysis/03-deep-dive/03-tariff-channel.md` covering:

1. **Tariffs as a Second Inflation Source**
   - Oil shock creates supply-side inflation from energy costs
   - Tariffs (now ~13% effective rate, highest since 1930s) create a _second_ supply-side inflation source from imported goods costs
   - The compounding effect: goods that require oil to produce AND cross borders are hit twice
   - NY Fed finding: 90% of tariff costs borne by American firms/consumers

2. **The Legal Uncertainty Multiplier**
   - IEEPA → Section 122 → Section 301: three legal frameworks in three weeks
   - How legal uncertainty affects business investment, supply chain planning, and inventory decisions
   - The "150-day cliff" (Section 122 expires July 2026) creating a deadline-driven policy scramble
   - Impact on trade finance: letters of credit, trade insurance, customs bonds all affected by uncertain tariff rates

3. **Trade Diversion Under Dual Pressure**
   - China's export surge (+21.8%) shows tariffs reroute, not destroy, trade
   - But the oil shock disrupts the rerouted supply chains too (Asian shipping through Hormuz)
   - Double jeopardy for ASEAN: absorbing diverted Chinese goods while energy costs surge

4. **Exchange Rate Effects**
   - Tariff theory says tariffs should strengthen the imposing country's currency (terms of trade improvement)
   - Reality: dollar weakened because retaliation + uncertainty dominates
   - Oil shock adds another exchange rate force: energy importers' currencies weaken
   - Net effect on major currencies

5. **The Consumer Squeeze**
   - US consumer faces: higher gas prices (oil) + higher goods prices (tariffs) + potentially higher interest rates (monetary response)
   - Different country profiles for this squeeze
