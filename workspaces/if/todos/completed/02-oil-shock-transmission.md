# Todo: Oil Shock Transmission — How the Iran War Flows Through Global Finance

## Status: pending

## Description

Trace the transmission mechanism of the Iran/Hormuz oil shock through the international financial system, step by step. Show students how a geopolitical event in the Middle East creates financial consequences in Tokyo, London, Frankfurt, and New York.

## Deliverable

A document in `workspaces/if/01-analysis/03-deep-dive/02-oil-shock-transmission.md` covering:

1. **The Shock Chain** (step-by-step narrative)
   - Physical disruption: Strait of Hormuz closure → tanker traffic drops 95%
   - Insurance market: Marine insurers withdraw coverage → remaining ships can't transit
   - Spot oil prices: Immediate surge as physical supply constrained
   - Futures market: Contango/backwardation dynamics, speculative positioning
   - Refined products: Jet fuel, gasoline, diesel, petrochemicals all affected
   - Consumer prices: Pass-through to CPI with typical lag times
   - Central bank reaction function: How inflation expectations shift

2. **Differential Impact by Country**
   - Energy importers (Japan, South Korea, India, Europe): Current account deterioration, currency pressure, inflation
   - Energy exporters (Saudi Arabia, Norway, Canada, Russia): Windfall revenues but geopolitical risk
   - US (mixed): World's largest producer AND consumer — both sides of the equation

3. **Financial Market Channels**
   - Equity markets: Why Asian markets crashed hardest (KOSPI -18%)
   - Bond markets: Flight to quality vs. inflation risk repricing
   - Currency markets: Safe-haven flows (yen, franc, gold) vs. risk-off EM weakness
   - Commodity derivatives: How futures markets amplify or dampen physical shocks

4. **Quantitative Data Points**
   - IMF multiplier: 10% oil price rise → +0.4% inflation, -0.15% GDP
   - Oil's share of import bills for key countries
   - Historical comparison: magnitude vs. 1973, 1979, 1990, 2008 oil shocks

- At least 3 country-specific examples showing differential impact
