## Status: pending

## Description

Analyze how central banks are trapped between inflation (from oil + tariffs) and growth (slowing from uncertainty + higher costs). This is the third arm of the triangle and the "impossible choice" at the center of the discussion.

## Deliverable

A document in `workspaces/if/01-analysis/03-deep-dive/04-central-bank-dilemma.md` covering:

1. **The Stagflation Trap Explained**
   - Normal inflation: raise rates → cool demand → inflation falls. Simple.
   - Supply-side inflation (oil/tariffs): raising rates doesn't reduce oil prices or tariff costs — it just adds a growth hit on top of an inflation hit
   - This is why the 1970s were so painful and why central bankers fear this scenario
   - Define stagflation clearly with historical context (1973-74, 1979-80)

2. **The Fed's Bind**
   - Rate at 3.50-3.75%, held since January
   - Oil inflation argues: don't cut (or even raise)
   - Growth slowdown argues: cut to support economy
   - Tariff uncertainty argues: wait and see
   - What the market is pricing (45% chance of cut by April)
   - How the Fed's dual mandate (price stability + maximum employment) creates contradictory signals

3. **Other Central Banks' Different Constraints**
   - **ECB**: Energy-vulnerable Europe; euro appreciation helps with import costs but hurts exporters
   - **BOE**: Dramatic reversal from expected cut to 70% chance of hike — illustrates how fast the calculus changes
   - **BOJ**: In a hiking cycle (0.75% is highest in 30 years) but safe-haven yen demand complicates things. Also: yen carry trade dynamics
   - **PBOC**: Easing to support growth but managing yuan stability; cut FX risk reserve ratio to 0%
   - **Emerging markets**: Turkey (37%), Brazil (15%), India (5.25%) — completely different situations

4. **The Impossible Trinity in Action**
   - Map each central bank to its position in the Mundell-Fleming triangle
   - Show which "corner" each has chosen and what it costs them
   - Why the ECB and Fed have different degrees of freedom despite similar dilemmas

5. **What History Teaches**
   - Volcker's choice (1979-82): crush inflation with high rates, accept recession
   - Burns' choice (1970s): accommodate inflation to protect growth, got stagflation
   - The lesson: delaying the inflation fight usually makes it worse
   - Is today's situation different? (Arguments both ways)
